So far in the _Politics,_ we have explored the nature of community, the variety of political regimes (including many ancient regimes Aristotle uses as his examples), and the varieties of permissible regimes. Last week, we focused on what causes regimes to fall. We saw, for instance, that oligarchies are prone to two kinds of factional conflict (within the oligarchy and against the people) and that monarchies tend to sustain themselves by reducing their power. In Book 6, Aristotle turns to institutions.

If you’re reading the Lordes’ translation, there is an outline of the entire _Politics_ which I have found very useful. It is found on pages xliii-xlv. Here is how Lordes outlines Book 6:

-   Varieties of Regimes and Political Institutions (book 6)
    
    -   Regimes and Political Institutions (6.1)
        
    -   Varieties of Democracy and How to Establish Them (6.2-5)
        
        -   Character of democratic rule (6.2)
            
        -   Democratic equality (6.3)
            
        -   Varieties of democracy (6.4)
            
        -   Preserving democracies (6.5)
            
    -   Varieties of Oligarchy and How to Establish Them (6.6-7)
        
    -   Varieties of Executive Institutions
        

Given that Aristotle is, let’s say politely, a bit meandering when he introduces a new topic, this outline proves especially helpful.

* * *

One illusion that Aristotle has dispelled for us is that classifications of regimes are exclusive. We tend to think in absolute terms about institutions: _do we live in a democracy or an oligarchy or a tyranny?_ The question wants to be answered in a particular way: you pick just one option. But many institutions are ‘blended.’ They may be ‘of a more democratic cast’ yet have some institutions which are oligarchically arranged.

We also see Aristotle’s pluralism at the beginning of book 6: since ‘peoples are different,’ their sort of democracy will naturally vary. But the institutions within a regime also affect its character. To understand the full character of a regime, then, we have to look at the institutions which exist within it. This is also helpful if one is interested in establishing a democracy. Those who ‘seek to combine everything that derives from the basic premises of the regime’ err, Aristotle says.

Democracies aim at freedom, and some say that only in a democracy is freedom partaken in. But there are two aspects of freedom here:

-   Ruling and being ruled in turn
    
-   Living as one wants
    

Within a democracy, there are also two notions of justice:

-   The justice of the majority, which tends to the poor ruling since they are more numerous.
    
-   The justice of merit, which I believe is signified when Aristotle speaks of ‘the freedom that is based on equality.’
    

Given these features of democracy, certain popular institutions tend to emerge:

-   Election to all offices from among all the citizens
    
-   Rule of all over each, and of each over all in turn
    
-   Having all offices chosen by lot, or those not requiring experience and art
    
-   Having offices not based on any assessment, or based on the smallest possible
    
-   The same person not holding any office more than once, or doing so rarely, or in a few cases, apart from those relating to war
    
-   Having all offices of short duration, or those where it is possible
    
-   Having all adjudicate or persons chosen from all
    
-   The assembly having authority over all matters or the greatest, and no office having authority over any, or having it over as few as possible
    
-   Providing pay — particularly for all, for the assembly, courts, and offices
    

It’s helpful to think about the regime (or regimes — there are many international readers among us) we currently live under. In the United States, we have some of these. We don’t have official assessments for offices, though many people cannot secure a major party nomination without proving they can raise a significant amount of money; we have term limits for some offices, though primarily for the executive branch; we have persons chosen from all play a role in adjudicating some things (juries), though we rely on (mostly) appointed judges for major legal controversies; purportedly, the legislature (the closest we have to an assembly) has authority over all matters or the greatest, but frankly we have a Congress that has shirked its responsibilities for decades; we pay our officers, meaning that we do not require lavish wealth as a _de facto_ requirement for serving as an officer.

In brief, I do not think Aristotle would say we have a popular democracy. We have a highly impure democracy — one that may be closer to a polity but which _de facto_ may be closer to an oligarchy.

You may take this as a damning criticism of the United States (I agree to some extent) but Aristotle believes that a democracy, just like an oligarchy, involved inequality and injustice — they just happen to involve different sorts of inequality and injustice. At root, this is because Aristotle does not seem to talk about justice as a matter concerning individuals. He is concerned about justice for groups; this is the only way to make sense of his comments in 6.3, where he speaks of the distribution of power between differently sized groups. This may also be why Aristotle refers back to his discussion in 4.6 at the beginning of 6.4, where he speaks of the best sort of democracy — Lordes helpfully clarifies that he is speaking of a democracy in which rich and poor have equal power. He is concerned with the idea of pure popular rule, which reduces to the rule of the poor over all.

Thus, Aristotle concludes that the best sort of democracy will include assessments:

> Hence it is both advantageous and customarily belongs to the sort of democracy spoken of earlier to have all elect to the offices and audit and adjudicate, but for persons elected on the basis of assessments to hold the offices, and the greater from the greater assessments — or else to elect none on the basis of assessments, but rather capable persons.

This ensures some equality of rich and poor, but also it is the best because ‘because the people are of a certain quality.’ As regimes change (or deviate from the best sort of democracy), they must progressively separate out a worse multitude for citizenship. If a city wants to have all participate, the people must meet certain requirements. If the people aren’t virtuous or fine enough, this sort of democracy will not last.

* * *

Instituting a regime is not the only task of a legislator, nor is it the greatest. In 6.5, we turn to the _preservation_ of regimes. In a democracy, one of the critical components is to ‘that there will be abundance over all.’ This is necessary for two reasons:

1.  If the multitude are too poor, then they will be unable to participate in the democratic process. This could be solved by paying those attend the assembly, but this requires taxes and confiscations. If this grows too extreme, we are likely to see new factional conflicts emerge.
    
2.  If the city’s surplus is directly distributed, the poor will always ask for more. Aristotle calls this the ‘punctured jar’, a reference to the daughters of Danaus. These women are punished in Hades for the murder of their husbands by being tasked with pouring water into a leaking jar. In other words, they will always be unable to complete their task. Simply distributing the city’s surpluses to the poor will not solve the problem. A more general abundance must be ensured.
    

Sometimes, there will need to be distributions, but this should go to tribes or parts of the city (rather than individuals). These distributions can be used to acquire land, ensuring some economic stability. ‘And in the meantime, the well off should be taxed to provide pay for necessary meetings, while at the same time being released from pointless sorts of public service.’ The well off are given something in return for their taxes, presumably, to avoid feeling resentful, which could lead to more factional conflict.

Something that becomes clear in these discussions is that Aristotle does not primarily see democratic regimes, or regimes in general, as a matter of huge masses of individuals coming to pursue their individual self-interest in collective deliberation. Rather, he conceives of politics as a matter of groups trying to live together. The earlier forms of communities – families, tribes, etc. – do not cease to exist within a city.

This is a remarkable contrast from how American democracy thinks of itself.

* * *

Aristotle believes that the material conditions of a people affect the sort of regime that is naturally instituted. We see this in his discussion of oligarchy in 6.7, where two sorts of oligarchy naturally emerge:

-   When the country is suitable for horses, a strong oligarchy emerges where the powerful (in terms of horses, part of the military element) rule.
    
-   Similarly, when it is suitable for heavy arms, another sort of oligarchy emerges.
    

In contrast, light arms and naval forces are popular — they require large numbers of the multitude to participate, making power more difficult to concentrate with the few. If a conflict emerged between the popular elements and the more elite military elements, the oligarchy is worse off. Aristotle’s prescription is to combine these elite military units with the more popular sort. This is a recurring theme: to sustain a certain kind of a regime, you can borrow elements from another sort of regime. Monarchs give up power and hand it over to the people; oligarchies work to wed themselves to the popular element; democracy embrace oligarchy to some degree.

* * *

Finally, Aristotle turns to the offices that must exist.

1.  Superintendence of the market to ensure agreements and orderliness. Having a market allows cities to sell things internally, ensuring self-sufficiency for the city.
    
2.  Superintendence of public and private problem in town. This again is to ensure orderliness, but also to see to things like the preservation and repair of buildings and roads and to mediate disagreements over boundaries. This is ‘town management.’
    
3.  Superintendence of property in the country. This is ‘field management.’
    
4.  Those who oversee revenue and how it is managed. These are ‘treasurers.’
    
5.  Someone to register private agreements and judgments from courts. These have several names, but ‘recorders’ is the easiest for us to grasp.
    
6.  Those who take action against those found guilty in court and over prisoners. Because this is odious work, Aristotle recommends that it be split up among many different people/offices. This is perhaps the most necessary office.
    
7.  Generals and war officers to manage the defense of the city.
    
8.  An office which audits the other offices. Call these ‘auditors.’
    
9.  A council to oversee the other offices.
    
10.  Those who handle sacrifices to the gods on behalf of the city — priests, sacrificers, temple guardians, treasurers of sacred funds.
     
11.  An office which specializes in common sacrifice not lawfully done by priests. This office stands in for the city. These are sometimes called ‘kings’ or ‘presidents.’
     

As cities grow, however, other offices might be created, e.g. an office which manages women or children, exercise officials, etc.
