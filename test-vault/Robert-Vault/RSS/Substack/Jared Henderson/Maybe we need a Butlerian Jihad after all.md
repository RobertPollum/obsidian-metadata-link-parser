In Frank Herbert’s _Dune,_ there are no computers. Instead, there are mentats: highly-trained individuals who can do extended, advanced calculations in their heads. All of the great houses, like House Atreides, employ mentats; without them, the universe’s economy would crumble — and as readers of _Dune_ know, the spice must flow.

In the background of _Dune_ is an event, never fully explained.[1](#footnote-1) It’s the reason they have no computers. They call it the _Butlerian Jihad._ In an appendix to _Dune,_ we get a brief description:

> Then came the Butlerian Jihad—two generations of chaos. The god of machine-logic was overthrown among the masses and a new concept was raised: “Man may not be replaced.”

During the Butlerian Jihad, the great religions of the world learn to set aside most of their differences and converge on a point of common agreement: ‘Thou shalt not disfigure the human soul.’ The thinking machines that had come to dominate the world were, presumably, one such disfigurement. These teachings were compiled into the Orange Catholic Bible, a syncretic document produced in the wake of the Jihad. One commandment from the O.C. Bible makes its way into _Dune_:

> Thou shalt not make a machine in the likeness of a human mind.

I’ve become more sympathetic to this idea lately.

* * *

I really should be working on my book this morning — my deadline is fast approaching, and while I can see the end I can also very clearly see the distance between me and it. But I made the mistake of checking Twitter while sitting on the toilet and, as my college roommate used to proclaim whenever he left the bathroom, I’ve just seen some shit.

In particular, I saw an ad [for a new app called 2wai](https://x.com/CalumWorthy/status/1988283207138324487). Because Substack and Twitter still don’t like each other, I can’t embed the Tweet, so you’ll have to click through to watch the video — it is brief and disturbing.

It shows a woman – cast to perfectly balance being attractive and approachable – talking to her phone. She’s speaking with her mother and showing off her pregnant belly. The video cuts to different phrases of life: the baby is born and needs to hear a bedtime story, he’s now a teenager and talking about his crush, he’s eventually showing a photo from a sonogram to his grandmother. The whole time, grandma is unchanging; she never ages. That’s because grandma is not grandma, but rather an AI avatar made before she died. She’s a facsimile, a copy — in other words, a fake.

Calum Worthy, the founder of 2wai and a former Disney Channel star, asks us to ponder this question: _What if the loved ones we’ve lost could be part of our future?_

From a very young age, I was incredibly close with my paternal grandfather. He was my favorite person in the entire world. He was my best friend, and nothing brought me more joy than spending my summers at my grandparent’s house in Florida. He died when I was in my junior year of college.

Even now, more than a decade later, I get misty-eyed when I think about him. I’ve had to pause writing this paragraph several times to wipe my eyes and regain my composure. I will never have words to describe how I miss him.

After a few years, I thought that I had come to terms with my grief. Death is a part of this earthly life, after all, and it is something we must learn to endure. But when my son was born, the wounds were torn open. The pain rushed in. It was because I wanted the two of them to meet. What sort of life would Teddy have, I asked myself, if he could never meet my grandfather? He was the man who taught me what kindness and loyalty were. He was the one who showed me most clearly what being a man was: a life of trying your damnedest to do good for those around you. Eventually, I figured it out: if I wanted Teddy to learn those things, I would have to be like my grandfather. An impossible task, but one I had to attempt. It was the only way.

I think this is the only option we ever have. The dead are just that — dead. I am an Eastern Orthodox Christian, and we have a strange relationship with the dead. We pray for them. We ask the saints to pray for us. But even if we believe the boundaries between the living and the dead are more porous than a naturalistic account of the world would allow, there’s the bare fact that we must reconcile ourselves with. The dead are somewhere else; they certainly aren’t here. If I wanted to do right by Teddy, I would have to do what my grandfather had done for me.

* * *

There are many reasons to oppose an app like 2wai. I have not looked at its terms of service, but I have confidence that several things are true:

-   They are collecting your private information
    
-   They are processing that information non-locally, so on their own servers
    
-   They retain the rights to alter their ToS whenever they see fit
    

These are standard clauses in apps these days, and if you give them so much information that they can create seemingly realistic avatars of your loved ones then they surely have _a lot_ of your data. It is a security nightmare, and that alone would be a good reason to shout from the rooftops ‘Don’t do it!’ But that’s not the only reason.[2](#footnote-2)

In the 2wai ad, people get older — they grow up, they build lives. But they also come back to the AI on the screen. But let’s ask ourselves a question: _who is this for?_ It isn’t for the AI. The AI is not a conscious entity. It is a highly sophisticated program, but it isn’t conscious. _It does not love you._ And that is fine, so far as it goes, but the clear intention of an app like 2wai is for you to form a relationship with this avatar. The more realistic they make this, the easier it is for you to forget that the AI isn’t real. But you have to remember that. It’s critical.

The app, then, presumably is supposed to benefit the users. I can imagine some short-term uses where someone reports feeling better after using 2wai; some people might even say that it helps them initially process their grief. But that’s not the usecase that 2wai is advertising. They are promising that this AI avatar can come with you throughout your life, journeying with you. Your dead grandmother can see your newborn baby, even if she’s been gone for years.

For a moment, I might think that Teddy can meet my grandfather.

But he cannot. He could meet a copy, a fake. So now let’s ask: _what does that do to us?_ Does it help you process your grief, or is it more likely that it helps you indefinitely delay confronting the pain of loss, the tragedy of finite human life? Does it help you cultivate relationships that make life meaningful, or does it allow you ignore those relationships while talking to an avatar on your screen? Does it help you build a life, or does it offer an unfulfilling substitute?

I think the answer is clear.
