_The post about Aristotle’s Politics will be out tomorrow. I had family in town this weekend, and that pushed all of my work back by a day. Apologies for the delay!_

My goal for 2025 was to reach 1,000 paid subscribers. My reasoning for this is that, with 1,000 paid subscribers, I could stop taking sponsorships on my YouTube channel and focus on giving away more of my writing and videos for free. While I haven’t met this goal, I’m not complaining — everything is growing, though a little slower than last year, and I’ve been able to keep growing on Substack without aggressively paywalling my work.

Perhaps in 2026, I’ll meet my goal. One option would be to start aggressively paywalling, but I’m reluctant to do that. The best business decision would be to make the reading groups a premium, paid experience, with my other articles given away for free, but I think keeping the reading groups free is an important service. Anyone can follow along with our readings of classic works of fiction and philosophy.

In that spirit, I’m announcing the second year of the **Commonplace Philosophy Reading Grants.** You can read more about the idea in last year’s post.

My goal is to have roughly 10% of my paid subscriptions be given away. That’s, again, a nice round number. If I had 1,000 paid subscribers, I’d be giving away 100. Last year, I offered 25 comped subscriptions. More than 25 people applied, and I gave nearly everyone who applied the subscription; I believe the total number was closer to 50. This year, I’m going to treat it like I already have 1,000 paid subscribers, and so I’m offering **100 comped subscriptions** for 2026.

Like last year, I’ll be using a Google form. There are only four questions. You do not need to convince me that you can’t afford a subscription, and I certainly won’t audit your finances. You do not need to disclose any personal details aside from your name, email, and a brief description of yourself. (This will be clear when you see the form.) But the way I see it, if you can’t answer four questions (in total taking less than five minutes) then I don’t know if you really want the subscription. A small amount of friction in the process operates like a filter.

A few notes:

-   This is run on the honor system. I cannot and will not follow up to ask about your finances. I am asking people to apply if financial hardship or worldly circumstances prevent them from purchasing a subscription that they would otherwise purchase.
    
-   Winners will find out that they’ve won when they get a notification email from Substack. I won’t be emailing anyone directly about this. Don’t fall for scams, which are all too common online.
    
-   None of the questions are marked as ‘required.’ But you have to fill out all four questions to be eligible for a free subscription. I’m writing this in the middle of the list to see if you’re reading the whole thing.
    
-   The new subscriptions will start January 1, 2026, just in time for our philosophy of technology reading group.
    
-   Previously, I’ve been asked to offer a lower rate for subscriptions to help people in other locales. International currencies are complicated, and $8/month can be quite difficult for someone in a country like, say, Indonesia to afford. (I’m picking Indonesia here just as an example, since I’ve traveled there and seen the relative cost of living.) This is very hard to do without platform support for localized prices. These reading grants are my way of trying to help obviate that problem within platform limitations and my current circumstances.
    

[The form can be found here](https://docs.google.com/forms/d/e/1FAIpQLSfcpcEY4Tfb8s95EaSZUXXorYMYfIOMSI_vPxzjNd7i2HMWLA/viewform?usp=publish-editor). You have until the end of November to fill it out.

Now, if you _can_ afford a subscription, and you think what I’m doing with this newsletter is worth doing, then I’ll ask you to consider becoming a paid subscriber. Not only will you be helping me personally, but you’ll be subsidizing access for others who cannot afford a subscription.

In 2025, we read the works of Hannah Arendt, Virginia Woolf, Ursula K. Le Guin, Plato, Kazuo Ishiguro, Zhuangzi, and Aristotle together. In 2026, we’ll be reading:

-   January: _Non-Things: Upheaval in the Life World_ by Byung-Chul Han and ‘The Question Concerning Technology’ by Martin Heidegger ([Link to PDF](https://bpb-us-e2.wpmucdn.com/sites.uci.edu/dist/a/3282/files/2018/01/Heidegger_TheQuestionConcerningTechnology.pdf))
    
-   February: _The Circle_ by Dave Eggers and ‘The Narcissistic Personality of Our Time’ by Christopher Lasch
    
-   March: _You and Your Profile_ by Hans-Georg Moeller and Paul J. D’Ambrosio and ‘A Cyborg Manifesto’ by Donna Haraway ([Link to PDF](https://warwick.ac.uk/fac/arts/english/currentstudents/undergraduates/modules/fictionnownarrativemediaandtheoryinthe21stcentury/manifestly_haraway_----_a_cyborg_manifesto_science_technology_and_socialist-feminism_in_the_....pdf))
    
-   April: _The Right to Oblivion_ by Lowry Pressly and ‘The Girl Who Was Plugged In’ by James Tiptree Jr.
    
-   May: _Alone Together_ by Sherry Turkle
    
-   June: _The Ethics of Authenticity_ by Charles Taylor and ‘Why I Am Not Going to Buy a Computer’ ([Link to PDF](https://classes.matthewjbrown.net/teaching-files/philtech/berry-computer.pdf)) and ‘Feminism, the Body, and the Machine’ ([Link to PDF](https://religioustech.org/wp-content/uploads/2019/09/Berry-Wendell-Feminism-the-Body-and-the-Machine.pdf)) by Wendell Berry.
    
-   July: _Pattern Recognition_ by William Gibson and “The Work of Art in the Age of Mechanical Reproduction” by Walter Benjamin ([Link to PDF](https://web.mit.edu/allanmc/www/benjamin.pdf)).
    
-   August: _Technics & Civilization_ by Lewis Mumford
    
-   September: _What is Called Thinking?_ by Martin Heidegger
    
-   October: _Technopoly_ by Neil Postman and _Filterworld_ by Kyle Chayka
    
-   November: _Stop All the Clocks_ by Noah Kumin, [‘The Medium is the Medium’](https://www.newcartographies.com/p/the-medium-is-the-medium) by Nicholas Carr, and _Atlas of AI_ by Karen Crawford
    
-   December: _The Life of the Mind_ by Hannah Arendt (part 1).
