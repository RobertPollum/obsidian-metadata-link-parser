X put $1 million on long-form.

Everybody’s celebrating like we just discovered depth again.

But long-form “utility culture” has a darker temptation:

Gnosticism with better branding.

Not robes and candles.

Frameworks. Systems. Hacks. “Knowledge” that promises transformation.

And it sneaks into the church the same way it sneaks into business:

You start treating reality like it exists to serve your outcomes.

Even God.

Jesus Christ is the ultimate purpose of all existence.

Not a feature.

Not an add-on.

Not a “pillar” in your personal operating system.

> “For by him were all things created… all things were created by him, and for him… that in all things he might have the preeminence.”

> (Colossians 1:16–18, KJV)

For Him.

Not for your productivity.

Not for your peace-of-mind.

Not for your brand.

Not for your “best life.”

**For Him.**

If He doesn’t have preeminence, you don’t have Christianity.

> You have a religious productivity plan.

* * *

We live in the age of high-utility content.

> Everything must “pay rent.”

Every paragraph must be actionable.

Every post must be “tactical.”

Every sermon must end with “three steps.”

And without noticing, we train the soul to ask one question:

> “How does this help me?”

That is not discipleship.

That is self-worship with Bible verses taped on top.

* * *

Paul warned the Colossians about people who look spiritual but are actually selling a counterfeit.

> “Beware lest any man spoil you through philosophy and vain deceit…”

(Colossians 2:8)

Here’s the modern translation:

Beware of anything that turns Christ into a means instead of the end.

Because the moment Christ becomes a means, you start hunting “deeper knowledge.”

Secret keys.

Hidden codes.

Perfect methods.

And you become “puffed up” while starving to death.

Paul describes it plainly:

> “…vainly puffed up by his fleshly mind, and not holding the Head…”

(Colossians 2:18–19)

Not holding the Head.

You can know ten thousand facts and still lose the Person.

That’s gnosticism.

> Knowledge without communion.

* * *

So what’s the counterpunch?

Not “more information.”

Focused reading.

Slow. Daily. Receiving, not consuming.

Because the problem isn’t that you aren’t reading enough.

It’s that you’re reading like a man scrolling.

Fast. Hungry. Unpresent.

> And the Bible is not meant to be skimmed like a timeline.

* * *

“Selah” shows up again and again in the Psalms.

A pause.

A break in the noise.

A forced stillness so the words actually land.

Modern life trains you to skip.

Scripture trains you to stay.

Selah is a focus-reader that forces the stay.

One word at a time.

Not because it’s trendy.

Because it’s corrective.

It makes your eyes stop sprinting.

It makes your heart stop performing.

It turns reading back into encounter.

* * *

**Proof (Two Kinds)**

**1) Biblical proof**

> “For in him dwelleth all the fulness of the Godhead bodily. And ye are complete in him…”

(Colossians 2:9–10)

Complete in Him.

Not complete in your systems.

Not complete in your “daily stack.”

Not complete in your content diet.

In Him.

**2) Human proof**

When you remove skimming, people actually finish.

That’s what the one-word format does.

It forces attention.

And attention is where communion begins.

* * *

**This week, do something painfully simple.**

• Open your Bible.

• Choose one passage in Colossians (start with Colossians 1:15–20).

• Read it slow.

• Ask one question: “Where have I demoted Christ?”

• Repent like a man who means it.

No theatrics.

Just preeminence restored.

* * *

Selah is a tool.

Not a savior.

But it can help you fight the real enemy: skimming your way through holy things.

Paste any text.

Export in about 60 seconds.

One word at a time.

Use it for content if you want.

But I built it for this:

So Scripture stops being “information about God” and becomes time with God again.

* * *

When was the last time a verse changed you

not merely informed you?

Reply with the reference.

And tell the truth.

Selah is live TODAY.

One word at a time.
