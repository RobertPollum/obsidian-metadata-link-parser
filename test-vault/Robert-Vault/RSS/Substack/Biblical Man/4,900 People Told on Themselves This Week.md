I wrote something about Scott Adams.

Not a eulogy.  
Not a hot take.  
Just a question:

**When did we become the gatekeepers of grace?**

22,000 people liked it.  
4,900 replied.

And in those replies, I watched something I didn’t expect.

Christians—good ones, Bible-believing ones—couldn’t stop parsing a dead man’s words.  
They weighed his tone.  
Measured his sincerity.  
Ran his final breath through some invisible algorithm and found it wanting.

“That’s not real faith.”  
“He was hedging his bets.”  
“Pascal’s Wager doesn’t save.”  
“Cramming for finals.”

And I realized: this isn’t about Scott Adams.

It never was.

* * *

The older brother stood outside the party.

His pig-stinking sibling got the fattened calf.  
The robe.  
The ring.

And the faithful son who never left—who worked every day, who obeyed every rule, got nothing but an invitation to celebrate someone else’s rescue.

He refused.

Not because the theology was wrong.  
Because the math was wrong.

I gave more.  
I stayed longer.  
I sacrificed harder.

And this guy gets the same inheritance?

That’s not a theological objection.

That’s a clenched fist.

* * *

The workers showed up at dawn.

They labored through the heat.  
Bore the burden of the full day.

And when the landowner paid the one-hour guys the same wage, they didn’t question his generosity.

They resented it.

“You have made them equal to us.”

There it is.

The scandal of grace isn’t that it’s free.

It’s that it feels unfair.

And something in us recoils—not because grace rejected us, but because we’ve been keeping score.

And the score says we earned more.

* * *

So I need to ask you something.

What are you clutching so tightly that you’d choose it over obedience?

Is it the scorecard?

The years you’ve put in?

The resentment toward someone who got mercy you think they didn’t deserve?

Is it a relationship you’ve baptized as “God’s will” because letting go would cost too much?

A career?  
A reputation?  
A version of yourself that feels safer than surrender?

Here’s what I’ve learned:

**The thing you refuse to release is the thing that owns you.**

And you can dress it up in theology, call it discernment, wrap it in Bible verses, but if your hands are clenched, you can’t receive anything.

* * *

Mary knew this.

We wrote songs about her yes.

We didn’t write about the cost.

The temple, when Simeon told her a sword would pierce her soul—and she stayed.

Cana, when her Son pushed her away in public—and she pointed to Him anyway.

Golgotha, when she watched Him die and could do nothing but stand there with empty hands.

Three surrenders.  
Each one harder than the last.  
Each one requiring her to let go of the only thing she truly loved.

That’s not comfortable Christianity.

That’s what faith actually looks like when it costs you everything.
