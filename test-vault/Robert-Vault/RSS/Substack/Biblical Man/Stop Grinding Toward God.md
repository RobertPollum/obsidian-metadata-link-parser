* * *

Hit the heart. Restack it.

* * *

A bodybuilder doesn’t grind to eat healthy.

He would have to grind **not** to.

I need to confess something.

I spent years grinding toward a calling that was never mine to chase. I had the charts. The ministry plan. The five-year vision board. I knew exactly what kind of Christian leader I was supposed to become.

And the swamp almost swallowed me whole.

* * *

A secular writer named Dan Koe wrote something last week that got 66 million views. That’s more eyeballs than most preachers will see in a lifetime.

His thesis: identity precedes behavior.

The bodybuilder eats clean because he **is** healthy. Not to become healthy.

I read it and felt something crack open in my chest.

_The Christian already has this answer. We just forgot it._

* * *

## I — The Swamp Nobody Talks About

In _Pilgrim’s Progress_, Christian falls into the Slough of Despond.

A swamp of despair.

Heavy. Dark. Sinking.

But here’s what most miss about that scene:

Christian didn’t fall in because of sin alone.

**He fell in because he was carrying a burden he was never meant to carry.**

I see the same swamp swallowing men and women in the church today.

Not from rebellion.

From unmet aspiration.

I know because I’ve been there. Neck-deep. Gasping for air. Wondering why God wasn’t showing up for the ministry He supposedly called me to.

* * *

## II — We Were All Told We’re Special

Mr. Rogers looked into the camera and told me I was special.

Marketing companies have been saying it ever since.

And somewhere along the way, we started believing that “special” meant we were destined for something big.

Something visible.

Something that would finally make people see what we see in ourselves.

So we read Ephesians 4 and 1 Timothy 3, and we identify:

-   “I’m called to be an evangelist.”
    
-   “God made me to be a teacher.”
    
-   “I have the heart of a pastor.”
    
-   “I should be leading.”
    

And maybe that’s true.

But here’s what happens when nobody else sees it.

Bitterness.

Resentment.

A slow drift into the swamp.

Because our aspiration was never reciprocated.

Our “calling” was never confirmed.

And now we’re stuck between what we think we’re supposed to be and what we actually are.

I lived there for years.

Angry at God for not opening the doors.

Angry at the church for not recognizing my gifts.

Angry at myself for not being good enough.

* * *

## III — The Bodybuilder Problem

This is where Dan Koe’s insight hit me like a truck:

> “Do you think the bodybuilder has to ‘grind’ to eat healthy? The truth is that they can’t see themselves living any other way. The bodybuilder has to grind to eat unhealthily.”

He’s describing something Scripture has always taught about the new creature in Christ:

> “Therefore if any man be in Christ, he is a new creature: old things are passed away; behold, all things are become new.”  
> (2 Corinthians 5:17, KJV)

The successful soul winner doesn’t wake up every morning and grind through mantras in the mirror.

They don’t force themselves to talk to strangers.

They don’t discipline their way into evangelism.

They simply share Christ with people because it’s an extension of who they are.

It flows.

Like water from a spring.

Like fruit from a branch.

> “I am the vine, ye are the branches: He that abideth in me, and I in him, the same bringeth forth much fruit: for without me ye can do nothing.”  
> (John 15:5, KJV)

**The fruit isn’t forced.**

**It’s produced.**

* * *

## IV — The Gap Nobody Identifies

Here’s the problem I see:

Most Christians don’t know how to walk with Christ anymore.

Not theoretically.

Practically.

They don’t know how to:

-   Lay in the grass with Him, looking at the sky, talking about nothing and everything
    
-   Walk a trail with Him
    
-   Ride a bike with Him
    
-   Drink their morning coffee with Him
    
-   Sit in traffic with Him
    

We’ve made the Christian life about outcomes.

About callings.

About ministry positions.

About finally becoming the evangelist, teacher, preacher, bishop, or deacon we believe we’re supposed to be.

And because we’re so focused on the destination, we never actually walk anywhere.

We just grind.

**And grinding doesn’t produce fruit.**

**Abiding does.**
