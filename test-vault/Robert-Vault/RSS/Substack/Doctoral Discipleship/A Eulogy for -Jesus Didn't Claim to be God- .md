“I am firmly convinced that Jesus never talked about himself as God.” - Bart Ehrman

“Nowhere in Mark, Matthew, or Luke does Jesus actually claim to be God in his own words…at best, it’s just in John’s Gospel that divine claims begin to appear.” - Alex O’Connor

Growing up in predominantly Evangelical and Catholic circles, the idea that Jesus didn’t claim to be divine never even occurred to me. The real question was whether the Resurrection actually happened. As I delved into New Testament scholarship, I quickly discovered that Jesus’ self-disclosure of his divinity had not only been debated but was, in fact, settled. Specifically, that our earliest Christian sources (Matthew, Mark, and Luke) do not portray Jesus declaring himself to be divine. I was a bit taken aback by this. So, I did a quick Ctrl + F search in my Synoptic Gospels for “I am God” and, sure enough, found nothing. The panic started to set in. Had I been duped?

The leading scholar on the idea of a non-divine Jesus is the highly renowned Bart Ehrman. For scholars like Ehrman, the debate is straightforward: Jesus never explicitly claims to be God, so any interpretation of Jesus as divine emerged much later in the development of Christian thought. But many might think, don’t the Synoptics show Jesus performing miracles and other remarkable acts? Ehrman would answer, “Of course.” However, in the Greco-Roman world, performing miracles alone doesn’t make someone divine. So, while Jesus may be intriguing, he doesn’t consider himself to be God. So goes the theory…

But let’s not kid ourselves here. The question of Jesus’ divinity is always influenced by theological presuppositions. For example, Bart Ehrman is a former Christian and a dogmatic one. So, it’s no surprise that for someone like Ehrman, the Jesus he describes cannot be divine. As for me, I am a Christian and a dogmatic one. Therefore, it won’t surprise anyone that I believe Jesus saw himself as divine in our early sources—and, in fact, I think it’s evident throughout. My aim in this article isn’t to persuade people like Bart Ehrman or Alex O’Connor, as they have already made up their minds. Instead, I want to correct their language. My argument is that _to claim that Jesus didn’t consider himself divine in the earliest Gospels is patently false_. If scholars like Ehrman don’t believe Jesus saw himself as divine, they need to look for that evidence elsewhere because the Synoptic Gospels do not support their conclusions.

Scholars who believe that Jesus didn’t consider himself divine typically focus exclusively on the Synoptic Gospels (Matt, Mark, Luke) because, in their view, these texts present a much lower and reliable view of Jesus’ divinity, often referred to as “low Christology.” This contrasts with John’s Gospel and the Epistle to the Hebrews, which present a much higher, or “high Christology.” While I disagree with their interpretation, let’s give them the benefit of the doubt. Let’s stick strictly with the Synoptics and ask the question: did Jesus consider himself to be God?

His work is significantly more thorough and deeply grounded in a plausible historical method. I’ll be posting a review of it later on._

## Hate Your Mom and Dad

_“Whoever comes to me and does not hate father and mother, wife and children, brothers and sisters, yes, and even life itself, cannot be my disciple” -_ Luke 14:26 (also in Matthew 10:37; Mark 10:29–30).

Discussions about what Jesus of Nazareth actually said inevitably lead to debates surrounding the ongoing Quest(s) for the Historical Jesus. The central claim of these quests is that many sayings attributed to Jesus are not historically authentic. Thus, scholars attempt to determine which sayings are more likely genuine using historical methods. However, the conclusions of these quests have often been shaped by theological perspectives—primarily secular ones. Moreover, the criteria for authenticity are frequently shifting and inconsistently applied, with a level of scrutiny rarely, if ever, used for any other historical figure than Jesus. Nevertheless, based on the sayings attributed to Jesus, _it is highly likely that the Historical Jesus conveyed the idea that he is more important or valuable than family, particularly one's mother and father._

What does this have to do with Jesus’ self-understanding of divinity? Quite a lot, especially within the context of first-century Judaism. In Jewish tradition, the only being worthy of greater devotion and respect than one’s parents was God alone (Sirach 3:1-16; Lev 19:13; Ex 20:12). Additionally, in Philo’s commentary on _The Decalogue_, he equates honoring your parents as tantamount to honoring God (_Decalogue_, 106-108). Or take the later writings in the Talmud on marriage and family relations.

> _And so too, the equating of one’s attitude toward his parents to his attitude toward God is a logical derivation, as the three of them are partners in his creation. As the Sages taught: There are three partners in the forming of a person: The Holy One, Blessed be He, who provides the soul, and his father and his mother. **When a person honors his father and mother, the Holy One, Blessed be He, says: I ascribe credit to them as if I dwelt between them and they honor Me as well**. -_ Talmud (Kiddushin 30b)

Therefore, it is fair to say in Jewish thought, the hierarchy of devotion was primarily to God, and then the immediate second was one’s parents.

Yet, this story from Jesus is often interpreted as him calling his disciples to radical devotion, but that interpretation falls short. Jesus is not introducing a new or radical concept—_he is reaffirming the traditional Jewish principle of devotion to God_. What is radical, however, is the identity of the one placed above mother and father: Jesus himself. To a first-century Jewish audience, the implication would have been unmistakable—Jesus was positioning himself on the same level as God, claiming a level of devotion that belonged to God alone.

Take a modern example: if I stood up and said let’s sing the American pledge of allegiance and said, “I pledge allegiance to the flag of the United States of America and to the republic for which it stands, _one nation under Daniel_, indivisible, with liberty and justice for all.” There would be no mistaking to any modern-day American what I just claimed. The same is true for Jesus of Nazareth. Now, of course, for scholars like Bart Ehrman, who have devoted so much scholarship to this subject, one would assume they have placed a keen eye towards very Jewish ways Jesus claimed to be God. But alas, this saying, which is routinely ascribed to the Historical Jesus, has never made it into Ehrman’s work (that I know of, that is).

## Walking on Water

_“When evening came, the boat was out on the sea, and he was alone on the land. When he saw that they were straining at the oars against an adverse wind, he came towards them early in the morning, walking on the sea. He intended to pass them by. But when they saw him walking on the sea, they thought it was a ghost and cried out; for they all saw him and were terrified. But immediately, he spoke to them and said, ‘Take heart, it is I; do not be afraid.’”_ - Mark 6:47–50 (also in Matthew 14:22–27).

For secular scholars, of course, no one walks on water. So, let’s grant it to them. Suppose Jesus was simply walking on a sandbar, appearing as any normal human would.

Now, consider what Jesus says at this moment: “Take heart, it is I; do not be afraid.” This is where we need to delve into some Greek and head over to the Old Testament. In the story where God appears to Moses in burning bush, Moses asks God how he should identify YHWH to the Egyptians, God responded simply: “I Am” (Greek: _egō eimi_). This statement is profound, but I’ll set that discussion aside for now. The key point is that God defines Himself with the phrase “I Am” (_egō eimi_). So, when Jesus is walking on water (or a sandbar), and his disciples are terrified, how does he identify himself? You guessed it: “I Am” (_egō eimi_).

The Ehrman crowd is likely ready to pounce on me now. Their argument is that _egō eimi_ cannot be a divine statement because it is also used by non-divine figures, such as Judas (Matt 26:25)!

_But not so fast_.

It’s true that _egō eimi_ appears in the speech of several non-divine individuals in both the New Testament and the Septuagint (e.g., John 9:9, Romans 11:13, Genesis 37:22). So, we can acknowledge that simply saying _egō eimi_ does not necessarily constitute a divine declaration. However, let’s probe a little deeper. Can we find any indication that Jesus is making a divine assertion at this moment?

If we compare YHWH’s and Jesus’ declarations, we notice something striking: _both manipulate the natural elements_. YHWH appears in a bush that burns but is not consumed (Ex. 3:2). In the same way, Jesus appears on the water but is not overtaken by it. Even if secular scholars reject the idea that Jesus literally walked on water, there’s no doubt that Mark and Matthew intentionally depict him as doing so—a clear sign pointing to his divinity. Consider the Septuagint translation of Job, which states that it is God alone who “stretched out the sky and _walks on the sea_ as on dry ground” (Job 9:8, LXX). Read that again. It is God _alone_ who _walks on the seas._

Now, let’s take another crucial detail: after Jesus says _egō eimi_, he immediately tells his disciples, “Do not be afraid.” Why is this significant? Let’s look at Isaiah 43 and Isaiah 44 for insight. In these chapters, YHWH twice declares, “Do not fear” (Isaiah 43:1; 44:2), in conjunction with his own assertion of divinity using _egō eimi_ (Isaiah 43:10; 43:25). Furthermore, YHWH reassures Israel:

> “When you pass through the waters, I will be with you; _and through the rivers, they shall not overwhelm you;_ when you walk through fire you shall not be burned, and the flame shall not consume you.” (_Isaiah 43:2_)

Given this context, when Jesus appears walking on water, declares _egō eimi_, and follows it with “Do not be afraid”, are we really to believe that this carries no divine significance? I find it not only unlikely but erroneous to think that a first-century Jew hearing this story would interpret it as anything other than Jesus claiming to be divine—claiming to be _egō eimi_.

## Forgiveness of Sins

“_When Jesus saw their faith, he said to the paralytic, ‘Son, your sins are forgiven.’ Now some of the scribes were sitting there, questioning in their hearts, ‘Why does this fellow speak in this way? It is blasphemy! Who can forgive sins but God alone?’”_ - Mark 2:5–7 (also in Matthew 9:2–3; Luke 5:20-21).

Every Synoptic Gospel includes a version of Jesus healing a paralytic. But beyond simply healing him, Jesus also forgives his sins—and in every account, the scribes recognize this as a claim to divinity. Bart Ehrman has argued, “It’s Jesus’ enemies who say that only God can forgive sins. That’s an important point.” And yes, it is an important point—so let’s examine it. Many secular scholars have attempted to argue that the Gospel writers misunderstood Jewish thought and that other figures, such as priests or messianic leaders, also had the authority to forgive sins. But these arguments are grasping at straws. In his extensive study on the subject, New Testament scholar Daniel Johansson concludes:

> “With the possible exception of one or two passages which may ascribe the authority to pardon sin to the Angel of YHWH, _no firm evidence can be found which demonstrates that other figures than God forgave sins._”

In other words, when Jesus says, “Your sins are forgiven,” we don’t have to speculate about how a first-century Jew would have understood it—_the texts tell us directly_. The scribes and Pharisees clearly recognize that Jesus is claiming to do something only YHWH can do. For someone like Ehrman, the argument seems to be: _Jesus himself doesn’t say he’s doing something divine—his enemies do!_ To which I say, exactly. Jesus’ divine claims were so clear and undeniable that even his enemies understood exactly what he was doing.

## Jesus, the Weather Man

_“And leaving the crowd behind, they took him with them in the boat, just as he was. Other boats were with him. A great windstorm arose, and the waves beat into the boat, so that the boat was already being swamped. But he was in the stern, asleep on the cushion; and they woke him up and said to him, ‘Teacher, do you not care that we are perishing?’ He woke up and rebuked the wind, and said to the sea, ‘Peace! Be still!’ Then the wind ceased, and there was a dead calm. He said to them, ‘Why are you afraid? Have you still no faith?’ And they were filled with great awe and said to one another, ‘Who then is this, that even the wind and the sea obey him?’_” - Mark 4:36–41 (also in Matthew 8:23–27, Luke 8:22–25).

Let’s head to the water one more time. Just like in our last nautical episode, let’s grant the secular scholars the miracles. Let’s say that when Jesus spoke, by some non-divine meteorological phenomenon, the wind and waves suddenly ceased. Fair enough. In this account—which, once again, appears in all three Synoptic Gospels—Jesus is able to “rebuke” the elements, and they respond to him. Whether or not (pun intended) this happened exactly as described, the disciples clearly understand that Jesus was the one who caused it.

The idea that God controls the weather, particularly storms, is littered throughout Second Temple literature and the Old Testament (Wisdom 1:15-19; Sirach 43:13-33; Nahum 1:3; Jonah 1:12; Ezekiel 13:13; Psalm 107:29). Few things in biblical scholarship are universally accepted, but the notion that only God commands the weather may be one of them. Furthermore, it’s not as if Jesus simply calmed the storm with a ritual or an incantation; he rebukes (_epitimaō_) the storm. To suggest that Jesus was doing anything other than the works of God would be, to say the least, a distortion.

And once again, we don’t need to speculate about how this would have been perceived in the first century. In every account, the disciples respond with the same stunned question: “Who then is this, that even the wind and the sea obey him?” Make no mistake—the disciples know exactly what this means. The only one the winds and the seas obey is not a mere rabbi from Nazareth with fine moral teachings; it is God, and God alone.

## Putting it Altogether

This article is a bit punchier than I typically prefer. I usually lean on words like “may,” “perhaps,” or “seems.” But on this topic, I can’t help it, and these examples are just a sample; I could go on.

For years, scholars like Ehrman have dominated discussions on Christology. His book's top best-seller lists and his arguments appear on magazine covers, all portraying Christians as simple-minded people who have projected divinity onto a Middle Eastern man who never claimed to be God.

What needs to be said about Ehrman’s argument, and others like it, is that they evaluate Jesus' divinity _on their own terms_. They search for explicit statements, expecting Jesus to declare, “I am Jesus, I am God.” But that is how a 21st-century Western scholar looks for divinity—not how a first-century Jew in Roman Palestine. For the first-century audience, after hearing the Synoptic Gospels, there would be no doubt: Jesus of Nazareth placed himself in the role of God and claimed to be God. For Ehrman, somehow Jesus literally walked like a God and talked like a God, but never claimed to be. Such a conclusion seems, at best, incoherent.

My direct interaction with Ehrman at academic conferences has been limited. He comes across as kind, witty, and exceptionally intelligent. I genuinely appreciate many of his works, especially those on the Gnostic Gospels. That said, I believe Ehrman ultimately misses the mark on the central question that has been echoed for two millennia: “Who is this man?” For a first-century Jew, and for me, it’s evident that Jesus of Nazareth unmistakably claimed to be God.
