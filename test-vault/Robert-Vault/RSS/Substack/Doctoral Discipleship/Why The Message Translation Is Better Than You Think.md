Whenever I’m teaching or leading a Bible reading exercise, I like to have people read 2–3 different translations aloud for the group. Usually, they’ll stick to versions they’re familiar with, like the ESV or NIV. But I always make sure to include _The Message_ (lamentably abbreviated as MSG). It almost always gets a laugh. I don’t always push back on the reaction, but if I’m in the right mood, I will. So, here’s my slightly more developed explanation of why I not only find _The Message_ valuable—but why, in some cases, I think it’s actually better than many standard translations.

## The Best Translations

Every youth pastor gets asked at some point, “What’s the best Bible translation?” For Reformed folks, it’s the ESV; for Baptists, the CSB; for charismatics, the NIV—you get the idea. But let’s clear up a major misconception: _there’s no such thing as a purely “word-for-word” translation of the Bible_. Anyone who’s bilingual knows this, and it’s especially true for ancient languages. Take Biblical Hebrew, for example—it’s wildly idiomatic. If you translate the words instead of the intent, you’ll completely miss the point (trust me, I’ve had some awkward moments). A classic example is Exodus 34:6. Most translations render it beautifully as “God is slow to anger.” Lovely, right? But in Hebrew, it literally says, “God’s nose has length.” Not exactly the vibe most English readers expect. The Old Testament is full of expressions like this. And even with thought-for-thought translations, updates are necessary when the conveyed meaning misses the mark—just compare the 1984 and 2011 versions of the NIV. _The bottom line is that all translations involve interpretation._

## So What’s the Problem with The Message?

For many people, their aversion to _The Message_ stems from the sense that it’s just too “cool” or casual. It makes Scripture feel overly approachable—as if something so sacred shouldn’t sound so conversational. Deep down, many of us still imagine the Bible as a text handed down on golden tablets, with God speaking in some kind of divine, elevated language.

But that instinct isn’t entirely off base. For years, scholars assumed the New Testament was written in an exalted, classical form of Greek—something fitting for holy writ. That is, until scholars stumbled across ordinary [grocery lists](https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/251788) from the Greco-Roman period written in the exact same style.

_Heraclides wants his poultry, bread, lupines, chickpeas, and kidney beans._

It quickly became clear: with a few exceptions (like Luke-Acts and Hebrews), the New Testament wasn’t written in highbrow Greek—it was written in _Koine_ Greek, meaning “common.” This wasn’t a text crafted for the elite; it was designed for everyday people. In fact, most of the New Testament would have been heard rather than read, by people who were largely illiterate. It wasn’t written like a formal email to your boss; it was more like a text to a friend. The language of the New Testament was meant to be accessible and understandable by both a slave and a scribe.

## The History of The Message

Very few people who’ve heard of _The Message_ actually know why it was written—or who wrote it. So let’s cover both. _The Message_ was the work of Eugene Peterson. For many writers and pastors, Peterson is a true north star. He pastored a small church in Maryland, far from the spotlight, and had little patience for the rise of celebrity, CEO-style pastors. He believed in a ministry of presence—thinking deeply, praying often, writing slowly. Fame found him late in life, and he never welcomed it. While others were booking keynote spots at big conferences like Exponential, Peterson was hiking the mountains of Montana. He was famously hard to reach—unless you were a weary pastor in need of a listening ear. He’d rather spend an afternoon translating Isaiah than hanging out with Bono. [Literally](https://bibleandmission.org.uk/2011/07/11/isaiah-eugene-peterson-and-turning-down-bono/).

The origin story of _The Message_ borders on legend, but it’s beautifully simple. While teaching through Galatians at his church, Peterson was struck by the power of the text, but his congregation wasn’t. They seemed unmoved, disconnected. Convinced the issue wasn’t the message itself, but how it was being heard, Peterson decided to translate a portion of Galatians into the everyday language of Harford County, Maryland. That Sunday, something clicked. The congregation lit up. So he kept going and soon discovered that he not only enjoyed the process but felt something come alive in him. He eventually sent a sample to NavPress, writing, “I think I have found my ‘voice’ and so am giving a fair sample of what I am capable \[of\] in this, this rendering into ‘_koine American_’” (cited in Collier, p. 215). From that point on, the project grew—and _The Message_ would go on to sell over 20 million copies.

There are two important things about _The Message_ that often go unmentioned. First, Eugene Peterson was no amateur when it came to biblical languages. He pursued doctoral studies in Semitic Languages at Johns Hopkins, taking advanced coursework in Akkadian and Ugaritic. Although he didn’t complete the PhD, Peterson had a deep command of Hebrew and Greek—he likely forgot more than most of us will ever learn. Second, Peterson didn’t create _The Message_ in isolation. He worked alongside a team of [top biblical language scholars](https://messagebible.com/translation/) who reviewed and supported his work. So while _The Message_ is undeniably readable and conversational, it wasn’t a rogue or careless project. It was rooted in serious scholarship and guided by someone who knew exactly how to handle the text.

## What “The Message” Gets Right

Most Bible translations struggle to render the Old Testament in a way that feels smooth and natural. Personally, I read from the ESV most of the time, but I often find its Old Testament translations to be overly rigid and difficult to follow. That’s not the case with _The Message_.

Take a well-known example:

> “The Lord is my shepherd; I shall not want.” — Psalm 23:1 (ESV)

This is arguably the most famous Psalm among Christians. But let’s be honest—phrases like “I shall not want” don’t land the same way today. We just don’t talk like that. Now look at how Peterson renders it:

> “God, my shepherd! I don’t need a thing.” — (MSG)

From the perspective of the Psalmist, this hits closer to the emotional heart of the text. It’s not just modernized language—it’s a clearer window into the original intent.

Let’s look at Proverbs 16:18:

> “Pride goes before destruction, and a haughty spirit before a fall.**”** — _ESV_

Compared to _The Message_

> “First pride, then the crash—the bigger the ego, the harder the fall.” — _The Message_

The ESV gives you a clear, faithful rendering of the original Hebrew. It’s accurate and understandable. But _The Message_ delivers it in a way that hits harder. You don’t just grasp the meaning—you _feel_ the weight of it. It’s the difference between reading a proverb and hearing a warning you can’t shake.

Peterson brings this same clarity to the New Testament. Here are two examples that I find extremely helpful.

#### Matthew 11:28

**ESV:**

> "Come to me, all who labor and are heavy laden, and I will give you rest..."

**The Message:**

> "Are you tired? Worn out? Burned out on religion? Come to me. Get away with me and you’ll recover your life... Learn the unforced rhythms of grace."

#### John 1:14a

**ESV:**

> "And the Word became flesh and dwelt among us”

**The Message:**

> "The Word became flesh and blood, and _moved_ _into the neighborhood_.”

Again, I think in many of these cases, Peterson doesn’t just translate accurately—he gets to the heart of what’s really being said. And there are countless examples like the ones above. My point is this: _The Message_ isn’t just a decent option among many; in some instances, it might actually be the _best_.

Now, I know critics of _The Message_ will have their concerns, especially those who champion the most “literal” translation—whatever that means. But as many readers can tell you, the more “literal” a translation is, the more illiterate you feel. I’m not looking to get into a debate about dynamic equivalence versus formal equivalence, but my point is that the best translation is the one you can _understand_, not just the one you can technically read. And in that sense, _The Message_ may not be literal—but it is, quite often, painfully accurate.

## All in All

I use _The Message_ all the time. Whenever I preach, I make a point to read it several times. And honestly, I don’t have many reservations about it. As I’ve argued above, _The Message_—like any translation—is ultimately an interpretation. But what sets it apart is its aim: it doesn’t just strive to make the Bible _readable_; it works to make it _thoroughly understandable_. While there are moments where I feel it stretches a bit too far, I still find it overwhelmingly helpful. In many ways, I think Peterson’s rendering captures the heart of what the New Testament letters were always meant to be. These weren’t messages delivered from the Holy of Holies—they were written in the cramped space of a prison cell or scribbled on the road from one town to the next. And _The Message_ makes that human, gritty, pastoral tone come alive.
