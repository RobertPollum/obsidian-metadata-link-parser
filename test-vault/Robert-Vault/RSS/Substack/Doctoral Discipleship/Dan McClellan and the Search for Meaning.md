“Have you heard of the Bible expert on TikTok!?”[1](#footnote-1)

A friend of mine from church asked a few years ago. I hadn’t, and given the vast industrial complex of pseudo-religious “experts” online, I wasn’t eager to learn about another one.

But to my surprise, though, this TikToker, Dan McClellan, wasn’t a pseudo-expert at all. He’s a bona fide biblical scholar, trained at Oxford and Exeter in biblical studies, cognitive linguistics, and the Hebrew Bible. He has a gigantic following on TikTok (almost a million followers as of today). Additionally, he published a [monograph](https://www.sbl-site.org/wp-content/uploads/2024/11/9781628374407.pdf) with the Society of Biblical Literature (SBL) and is a member of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints (LDS), having served a two-year mission in Uruguay.

“Who is this guy?” I thought.

Though what I found most interesting about McClellan is that he regularly uses his platform to debunk or poke holes in evangelical theology, such as [inerrancy](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GklUQpXKmcY), the [historicity of the Bible](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QF5pkO2p4_k), and so on. And he does so while continually referring to his mantra, “[Data over Dogma](https://www.youtube.com/@dataoverdogma).”

While there is much I appreciate about his scholarship (i.e., _[The Gods-Complaint: Psalm 82 as a Psalm of Complaint](https://www.jstor.org/stable/10.15699/jbl.1374.2018.452196)_ is excellent), his work is not without consequences. The friend who first asked me about him eventually left organized religion altogether, due in no small part to McClellan’s TikTok content. Thus, what are we to make of McClellan?

In McClellan’s search for meaning of the Bible, he has vocally and vehemently eschewed any sense of piety or dogmatism, pleading for a call to the Bible that is “just the facts.” In this article, I aim to highlight aspects of McClellan’s approach that I commend and those that I condemn.

## Just Two Dans and the Bible

As a fellow Dan, I genuinely appreciate many aspects of McClellan’s approach. First, McClellan is often careful and precise with his language. As an academic, language matters to me, and I get the sense that in every video McClellan is thoughtful and purposeful, not to overstate his case, and trying to be precise with his critiques. Additionally, McClellan rightfully and helpfully points out aspects of evangelicalism and the Bible that have given over to anti-intellectualism. Thus, McClellan’s challenge to evangelicals is to substantiate their points or beliefs, rather than merely stating them as if they were evidence. For example, stating that the Bible is inerrant _is not_ an argument; it is an assertion or belief that must be proven, and this must be done meticulously. And while there are many aspects of McClellan’s approach that I don’t personally like, I appreciate his goal.

Furthermore, McClellan, unlike say, Bart Ehrman, frequently only explains the academic majority view. Ehrman often, but not always, presents views that are at best third or fourth on the list as the most likely (i.e., Paul thought that Jesus was an angel). However, McClellan hardly breaks with the majority view (and when he does, he makes it clear), which I appreciate, because it truly feels like he is following the data over dogma. For example, McClellan’s book _[The Bible Says So](https://www.amazon.com/Bible-Says-So-Scriptures-Controversial/dp/1250347467)_ is surprisingly introductory in terms of its main arguments. And that’s not a diss to McClellan, I thought it was one of the book’s biggest strengths. It’s not easy to explain and summarize the history of canon and _Asherah_ in layperson’s terms. While I often, but not always, disagree with McClellan, I think his approach is honest and can push evangelicals to think more critically about their most dearly held beliefs. That said, there are two aspects of McClellan’s pursuit of data over dogma that I do take issue with, and I would like to address them.

## Data _Is_ Dogma

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!fQVQ!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F21bbed2e-90b0-4f47-91ba-312a1459732e_293x445.jpeg)



](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!fQVQ!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F21bbed2e-90b0-4f47-91ba-312a1459732e_293x445.jpeg)

The mantra that McClellan espouses relentlessly is “data over dogma.”

If only things were that simple.

The problem with McClellan’s claim that, unlike dogma, which is riddled with imposed belief and anachronism, the data are neither in favor of piety nor secularism; data just are. Unfortunately, that is not how the data work. Biblical data, whether in the form of archaeology, manuscripts, material culture, rhetoric, or other sources, is not neutral and must be interpreted through analysis, argumentation, imagination, and other methods. And all of this is done by humans, who, you guessed it, have dogma. Let me give you an example.

In the world of New Testament studies, Christology (the identity/divinity of Jesus) remains a hotly debated topic. Essentially, did the early followers of the Messiah Jesus consider him to be divine? And if so, to what extent (i.e., an angel or intermediary)? Scholars have the same access to the data, including Roman imperial worship artifacts, early Christian worship practices, writings, and funerary inscriptions. And yet, the data points scholars in the exact opposite directions. Scholars with a “high” Christology see Messiah devotion everywhere. Scholars with “low” Christology see adoptionism and a non-divine Jesus in every nook and cranny. This example illustrates that data must be interpreted; _data alone does not provide a solution_.

Now, this is not to say that we can’t know anything or that McClellan is wrong to desire data. My only point is that the data are dogma. The data is never neutral. It is not self-interpreting. Imperfect beings with a stake in their research being tied to tenure or the Trinity will always make the data become dogma and vice versa. While in some cases the data may be overwhelmingly in favor of one conclusion (i.e., Jesus of Nazareth was a historical figure), as historians, we rarely have the luxury of data being overwhelmingly in favor of one conclusion. While McClellan may claim his approach is above the fray of approaches like those of pious, dogmatic evangelicals, I can assure you it is not. The historical critical approach (which I love!) is just as easily manipulated by scholars with an agenda as any other. Thus, the difference between a neo-political Marxist interpretation of animals in the Priestly literature and a Southern Baptist evangelical literal reading of the longer ending in Mark is effectively nil. Both follow the data in very dogmatic ways.

## McClellan and Mormonism

To be clear, this section is _not_ a take-down of McClellan’s faith. Whatever and whoever he chooses to worship is not my primary concern. McClellan has spoken about his faith in many outlets, his book, his channels, and other interviews. Additionally, he served as a scripture translation supervisor for the LDS Church. In the past, commentators have accused McClellan of showing partiality towards his Mormon brethren and not fairly critiquing his own religious affiliation.

In response to these accusations, McClellan has provided several rebuttals. [For example](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=g6oj-QtqfWc), he states that he holds many positions that contradict orthodox Mormon teachings; thus, he’s a reliable person who follows the data rather than dogma. However, he has also [offered responses](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VFMisPp7NFU) that he’s only academically trained in the Bible/cognitive linguistics, thus he isn’t qualified to speak on such texts as the Book of Mormon. Additionally, he states that the Latter Day Saints’ scripture includes the Bible, thus he is fairly criticizing his own movement. But these responses are misleading; let me explain why.

McClellan’s assertion that his words must be limited to his expertise is ironic. He seems to have no problem responding to [Christian nationalism](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=HTYCjRLw1n8), [DEI](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=LLQY72nn3nw), [Trump](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fdjE2_vtoAM), a host of political issues, and [even pastors watching Joe Rogan](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=NVPzPj9XeZw). Now, my point isn’t that McClellan shouldn’t speak about these things—more power to him. But the idea that his expertise limits the use of his platform is just not true. Thus, while he claims he ought to stick within his scholarly limits, he regularly does not.

Second, McClellan is correct that the Bible is included among Mormon scriptures. But it is not the tradition’s central text; that position unquestionably belongs to the Book of Mormon. It would be like a Christian New Testament scholar on TikTok who claims to be “critiquing Scripture” but focuses almost exclusively on the Apocrypha while carefully avoiding the Gospels. No one would be fooled about what he’s actually doing.

At this point, I would like to include a response that McClellan provided on a [Mormon podcast](https://www.youtube.com/shorts/0l0XseFqTvY) regarding the historicity of the Book of Mormon.

> _“The data pretty firmly points in the opposite direction of a historical Book of Mormon. And that’s not to say that anyone who has faith in a historical Book of Mormon is somehow entirely irrational. It is just to say the data right now points pretty firmly in a given direction. That’s not to say that it is impossible that any other position is true. It’s just to say that’s where the data point.”_

To be clear, McClellan understands that the Book of Mormon is _not_ a historical document. It’s not that the data is pointing one way and could potentially point another way, as McClellan frames it. Then notice that he says if someone believes the Book of Mormon is historical, they are _not_ irrational, whereas for McClellan, [if an evangelical believes in inerrancy](https://www.instagram.com/reel/DQ16TIzCSk9/), they are irrational. McClellan’s last point is that “it is not impossible” that another position is true, leaving the door open for a historical Book of Mormon. This is the [exact type of response](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=do3FS9IPUsU) that McClellan takes Wes Huff to task for. Saying something is possible when it assuredly is not. If only McClellan had offered the same grace to Christians and the Bible.

Lastly, and this is probably my biggest concern with McClellan’s platform, there is essentially one type of person he consistently targets: evangelical Christians. I know what you’re thinking, “Oh, poor evangelicals!” But what McClellan is doing is incredibly easy because he’s criticizing a group he doesn’t belong to. It’s the lowest-risk form of courage or scholarship. To illustrate, I could just as easily, as an evangelical, launch a platform where I constantly post videos “destroying” Mormon theology, complete with edgy thumbnails like “I award you no points._”_ That would be effortless, precisely because I’m not part of that community and we don’t share the same values. There’s nothing brave or impressive about taking shots at people whose world you don’t inhabit.

My point in this entire section is that McClellan’s platform claims to operate on data rather than dogma, and yet, “the data” repeatedly pushes him in the same predictable direction, targeting evangelicals and their theology. Or to put it differently, when data is used repeatedly to specifically target and undermine another group’s beliefs, _it is dogma._

## Saving the Saved

In all of this, McClellan, who identifies as a [liberal Democrat](https://ballotpedia.org/Dan_McClellan), consistently produces content aimed at taking on the theologies of conservative Christianity. On topics such as [abortion](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=AhLcD2PT8D4), [LGBTQ+ issues](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IKP6JHKlbVE), and politics, his work challenges traditional interpretations and presents data intended to liberate people from their oppressors. He has also stated [elsewhere](https://www.sltrib.com/religion/2025/04/29/lds-bible-scholars-new-book-says/) (see also the introduction in _The Bible Says So_) that he chooses to engage in polemics in order to benefit the marginalized and the oppressed. From what I have read, McClellan seems to genuinely want to help those who are oppressed or marginalized. And yet, again and again in every study, the most diverse set of people in the world (socio-economically and geographically) are Christians. That is to say, statistically speaking, some of the most oppressed people in the world, refugees, immigrants, the urban poor, widows, and single mothers, are people who feel liberated by the Bible, not by McClellan’s deconstruction of it.

After spending too much time watching videos on McClellan’s platform, reading his book, and combing through some of his academic publications, I’ve come away skeptical of his approach. As an aspiring academic and a Christian, I care deeply about the data, but I am not foolish enough to pretend that my motives, religious or otherwise, don’t influence my research or my conclusions. When I watch McClellan’s stuff, I see dogma _everywhere_, just as much as anywhere else. While McClellan’s goal is admirable, I find his approach to be misleading.

Or to put it in terms that McClellan would understand, “I award you no points, and may God have mercy on your soul.”
