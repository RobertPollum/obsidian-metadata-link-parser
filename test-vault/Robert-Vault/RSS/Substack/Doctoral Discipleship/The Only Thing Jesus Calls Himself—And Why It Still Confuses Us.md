As the end of the semester approaches, I figured it’d be a good challenge to distill some of the research I’ve been deep in lately. Right now, I’m immersed in christological methodology (_essentially, how do scholars construct an understanding of Jesus’ identity)_, and there’s no greater challenge than trying to make sense of and offer some kind of helpful guide to the notoriously complex “the Son of Man” problem. For those lucky enough to have avoided this debate until now, here’s the gist: in the Gospels, Jesus exclusively refers to himself as “the Son of Man.” No one else really uses that title for him, and to this day, _we have no clue what it means_.

I’ll admit, this one is for the nerds, but for anyone looking to dig a little deeper, I hope you find it at least a little intriguing.

### The First Responders

Many Bible-believing Christians interpret Jesus’ use of the title “Son of Man” as a direct reference to Daniel 7:13, which portrays a messianic figure who comes with the clouds of heaven and is given authority by the Ancient of Days: _“I saw in the night visions, and behold, with the clouds of heaven there came one like a son of man, and he came to the Ancient of Days and was presented before him.”_ The conclusion seems straightforward—Jesus is this divine figure who will receive everlasting dominion. Scholars in the 20th century widely supported this interpretation as well.

But alas, it can never be that simple.

Ever since the discovery of the Dead Sea Scrolls, the debate has gotten increasingly complex.

First, in the Gospels, Jesus’ use of the phrase “the Son of Man” is striking from a grammatical standpoint. In Greek (_stay with me here_), it’s a definite expression, literally, _“the_ Son of _the_ Man_”_ with two definite articles. This exact phrase is unique; _it appears nowhere else in Greek literature outside of the New Testament_. An attentive Bible reader might respond, “Didn’t Jesus speak Aramaic? Maybe the Gospel writers were just translating an Aramaic phrase.” Fair point. Especially since the phrase in Daniel 7:13 is indeed written in Aramaic. However, here lies the issue: _there is no known Aramaic expression equivalent to this Greek construction_. And to make things even worse, linguistically speaking, Jesus’ phrasing doesn’t clearly mirror what’s found in Daniel 7:13. This creates a longstanding challenge for grammarians and theologians alike: what exactly is Jesus referring to?

### Messiah or Man?

The central question that has emerged from this debate is whether the phrase “the Son of Man” carries any christological emphasis, or if it’s simply a statement of identity. In other words, is Jesus intentionally declaring, “I am the preexistent Son of Man from Daniel,” invoking a divine, messianic figure? Or is he making a more grounded claim: “I am _the/a_ man”?

Proponents of the first view argue that the concept of “the Son of Man” was already well-established in Second Temple Judaism (as seen in texts like _1 Enoch_, _4 Ezra_, and certain Qumran writings) as a preexistent, apocalyptic, kingly messianic figure. From this perspective, when Jesus refers to himself as “the Son of Man,” he is intentionally invoking Daniel 7:13 and making a claim of christological significance. This interpretation seems to fit passages like Matthew 16:27: “For the Son of Man is going to come with his angels in the glory of his Father.” Moreover, some scholars of early Judaism agree that a developed “Son of Man” theology was part of the religious imagination by the first century.

However, this view faces several challenges. First, many of the texts that contain this “Son of Man” theology are generally dated after the time of Jesus (with _4 Ezra_ being definitively post-70 CE). Even if some of these writings were earlier, the evidence for a clearly preexistent, messianic “Son of Man” figure remains relatively sparse. It is, therefore, unlikely that such an idea or title would have been widely recognized by Jesus’ audience. Additionally, the connection between Jesus’ phrase and Daniel 7:13 is not as straightforward as it might seem. In Daniel, the phrase “one like a son of man” is descriptive, not titular; it refers to a figure “like a human,” not someone named _the Son of Man._ This weakens the argument that Jesus was using a well-known title with an obvious messianic claim.

Perhaps most tellingly, the early Church didn’t seem to latch onto “the Son of Man” as a central christological title. It didn’t become a dominant way of describing Jesus as divine in the earliest Christian writings. In other words, if Jesus was deliberately referencing Daniel 7:13 to make a bold theological statement, not many in his time, or even shortly after, seemed to catch on.

On the other hand, some scholars interpret Jesus’ use of the phrase as simply meaning “I am a man” or, in a more theologically nuanced reading, “I am _the_ man.” This perspective holds that since the expression “the Son of Man” does not function as a formal title or fixed idiom in Greek, it is unlikely that Jesus used it to convey a claim about his divinity or messiahship. If Jesus had intended to reveal his messianic identity through this phrase, it becomes difficult to explain how it would have been understood as such, given its lack of recognized titular status. Moreover, the Gospels themselves appear to use “the Son of Man” and personal pronouns like “I” or “me” interchangeably in some contexts (e.g., Matt 10:32–33; Luke 12:8).

However, this view faces several significant challenges. First, while it is true that “the Son of Man” may not have functioned as a fixed title prior to Jesus, the phrase clearly takes on a titular or at least distinctive role within the Gospels. To claim that it merely means “I” or “me” seems inconsistent with the weight and emphasis it is given throughout the canonical narratives. Second, there are key passages in which “the Son of Man” is directly linked to the apocalyptic imagery of Daniel 7 (e.g., Matt 16:27; Mark 8:38; Luke 9:26; Acts 7:56), reinforcing its theological significance. Furthermore, in texts such as Mark 2, where “the Son of Man” is portrayed as one who has the authority to forgive sins and as Lord of the Sabbath, it becomes difficult to argue that this designation refers merely to “a man.” It would be theologically and contextually odd to assert that an ordinary human being could claim such divine prerogatives.

## Where Do I Stand?

As a general rule, I’m not particularly fond of “both-and” arguments in scholarship. But my stance lies somewhere between “I don’t know” and “it’s both”—though I lean more toward the latter. It seems to me that arguing “the Son of Man” carried any clear christological significance during Jesus’ lifetime is highly unlikely. Virtually every non-canonical text that presents a messianic understanding of “the Son of Man” appears to postdate Jesus, and thus isn’t especially helpful for reconstructing his own usage. At the same time, the phrase does occasionally take on genuine messianic resonance within the Gospels. That tension is hard to ignore. Thus, my stance is the phrase _cannot_ denote Jesus’ unique identity, and somehow, at times, _it does_.

Of course, this tiny post can’t do justice to the debate; oceans of ink have been spilled, and forests of trees sacrificed over the various nuances and theories surrounding “the Son of Man” problem. Scholars like Jimmy Dunn have rightly called it one of the great embarrassments of New Testament scholarship that no clear consensus has ever emerged. In many ways, “the Son of Man” is the New Testament’s Melchizedek: it appears suddenly, fades just as quickly, and leaves behind no lineage by which we might trace its origins or significance. I’m just a little bit frustrated that when Jesus is asked in the Gospel of John, “Who is this Son of Man?” he didn’t take the opportunity to give a straight answer. But alas, the “Son of Man” problem will continue to spark debate, fund dissertations, and grant tenure for years to come.
