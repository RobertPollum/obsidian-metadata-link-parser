My relationship with Paul the Apostle is, to say the least, complex.

On one hand, I find Paul to be one of the most engaging, subversive, and insightful writers of the first century. On the other, as an aspiring scholar, I often find him perplexing. Many aspects of his theology and rhetoric elude me, and some even make me feel uneasy. His restrictive texts on women, in particular, fall into this category. For me, even the most compelling egalitarian or complementarian interpretations fail to fully soften his language in a way that feels entirely “kind” or “loving.” Some passages simply come across as harsh.

Nonetheless, it is not Paul’s job to give me warm fuzzies (that’s reserved for the Gospel of John). Therefore, we ought to not only deal with these texts but take them seriously. Below, I attempt to give a somewhat brief overview of how _most_ egalitarians _generally_ read the more sticky passages of Paul and women’s role in the Church. For the sake of brevity, I will not cover every Pauline text about women (such as the household codes in Ephesians 5 or Colossians 3),[1](#footnote-1) but the three “foundational” texts for both sides of the argument. Let’s begin with 1 Corinthians 11.

## 1 Corinthians 11:2-16

Paul’s well-known discussion on women wearing head coverings is largely considered cultural today and is rarely followed. However, this argument also touches on women’s role and participation in the Church, and thus relevant for us. Let’s start with verse 3.

> “But I want you to understand that the _**head**_ of every man is Christ, the _**head**_ of a wife is her husband, and the _**head**_ of Christ is God.” - 1 Cor. 11:3

There are unending debates (not hyperbole) about what Paul means here by the “head” (_kephalē_ in Greek). The question is: _does Paul’s use of the term “head” imply a sense of authority?_ Egalitarians tend to read “head” in Paul’s letter as either “source” (i.e., head of a river) as in the order of Creation or “preeminence” or “honor” (the two genders are distinguished).[2](#footnote-2) In other words, Paul does _not_ imply authority when uses the term _head_.[3](#footnote-3) The strength of this reading is that if Paul implies a sense of authority, then we are left with somewhat of a conundrum when Paul says, “the _head_ of Christ is God.” Which could imply some sort of _[subordinationist](https://www.logos.com/grow/the-fight-for-the-trinity-mike-bird-explains-the-eternal-subordination-debate-part-1/)_ view of Christ.[4](#footnote-4)

**1 Cor. 11:4-5**

> “Every man who prays or prophesies with his head covered dishonors his head, **5** but _**every wife who prays or prophesies**_ with her head uncovered dishonors her head, since it is the same as if her head were shaven” 1 Cor. 11:4–5.

Both egalitarians and complementarians agree that men and women can pray and prophesy, though the debate over a woman’s role in evaluating prophecy comes later. However, 1 Corinthians 11:4-5 can pose a significant challenge to the complementarian view of Paul’s “silent passages” (1 Cor 14:33b-36; 1 Tim 2:11-12). In other words, _if Paul was not addressing a cultural issue in the “silent passages,” then reconciling them with 1 Corinthians 11:4-5 becomes difficult._

**1 Cor. 11:7-9**

> “For a man ought not to cover his head, since he is the image and glory of God, but woman is the glory of man. **8** _For man was not made from woman, but woman from man_. **9** _Neither was man created for woman, but woman for man_.” 1 Cor 11:7–9.

Egalitarians argue that Paul’s appeal to Creation is not about authority but about _relationship_ and _honor_.[5](#footnote-5) A woman covers her head in worship not because man has authority over her, _but to honor the distinctiveness given to each gender in Creation_.[6](#footnote-6) In other words, men and women should present themselves differently to reflect their God-given differences. As Mike Bird aptly states, “Paul’s remarks are principally concerned with behavior in Corinthian worship that might be perceived as a denial of the differentiation between men and women according to culturally extant notions of gender identity.”[7](#footnote-7)

Therefore, the general egalitarian reading of 1 Cor. 11:2-16 is as follows:

1.  Men and women are distinct in honor and preeminence but _not_ in their roles within the Church.
    
2.  The primary gender distinctions Paul addresses are in relation to dress and appearance, _not function or authority_.
    

#### **Assessment of the Argument:**

The egalitarian interpretation of 1 Corinthians 11:2-16 is arguably one of the strongest arguments in their favor. From my perspective, it is indeed difficult to square this text _completely_ with a sense of authority. There is no doubt that there is an emphasis on _distinctiveness_, which does not necessitate hierarchy. When examining the passage as a whole, authority does not appear to be (at least to me) the central theme; rather, Paul seems _primarily_ concerned with gender distinctiveness.

However, a significant weakness of this interpretation is that I find the argument that _head_ does not imply at least _some sense_ of authority to be unconvincing. A simple search of the Greek translation of the Old Testament (aka the Septuagint, which was Paul’s Bible) suggests that _head_ seems to be associated with authority. Additionally, Paul’s reference to the order of Creation seems to highlight more than mere distinctiveness—it suggests some degree of hierarchy. But again, the _head_ debate will never be settled.

Thus, from my point of view, the egalitarian reading of 1 Corinthians 11:2-16 does helpfully emphasize distinctiveness, but I remain unconvinced that the concept of authority is completely absent from the text.

## 1 Corinthians 14:33b-35

> As in all the churches of the saints, **34** the women should keep silent in the churches. For they are not permitted to speak, but should be in submission, as the Law also says. **35** If there is anything they desire to learn, let them ask their husbands at home. For it is shameful for a woman to speak in church. 1 Cor 14:33–35.

To be frank, these 2.5 verses are among the most contentious in all of Paul’s letters. Notably, Gordon Fee (of blessed memory), one of the foremost egalitarian scholars of the past fifty years, could not reconcile them with the rest of Paul’s writings and therefore deemed them inauthentic.[8](#footnote-8) He was not alone in this assessment, some Bible translations, such as the NRSV, even place these verses in brackets! The argument for this view stems from the fact that a handful of manuscripts show scribes either relocating these verses to a different position within the passage or shifting them to the margins of the scroll.

A more recent and increasingly popular argument suggests that Paul is actually quoting and then refuting a commonly held belief about women.[9](#footnote-9) The reasoning goes as follows:

-   In verses 33–35, Paul quotes a widely accepted pagan view that women should remain silent.
    
-   In verse 36, Paul stops quoting and immediately challenges this notion, saying, “Or did the word of God originate with you? Or are you the only ones it has reached?” (1 Cor. 14:36). Thus, they take v. 36 as “Oh really!? You think you know better than God?”
    

In other words, Paul follows a rhetorical pattern “You have heard it said… but I say…”

Both of these arguments are commendable attempts to make sense of what, at face value, seems inconsistent with Paul’s overall message. For instance, if one agrees with Fee and reads the passage up to 1 Cor. 14:32 and then skips to 1 Cor. 14:37, the text flows seamlessly. However, I personally do not find either argument persuasive.

Regarding Fee’s argument, while there is some evidence of scribal movement of these verses, the number of manuscripts reflecting this is minimal. The overwhelming majority of manuscripts maintain the passage consistently in its traditional placement. As for the second argument, while Paul does frequently quote others (e.g., 1 Cor. 7:1; 8:1), his quotations follow a recognizable pattern, and this passage does not align with that pattern in a convincing way.

Thus, we are left with the conclusion that Paul did, in fact, say these words, and they are authentically his.

A more consistent egalitarian argument is that Paul was prohibiting a specific _type_ of speech, rather than forbidding women from speaking altogether. This view suggests that, for various cultural reasons, women were less likely to engage in structured learning and more prone to asking questions or disrupting moments of spiritual expression during public worship. As a result, Paul addresses women directly because such interruptions were occurring too frequently.

In summary, the general egalitarian argument for this interpretation is as follows:

-   Paul cannot be imposing a universal silence on women since, just a few chapters earlier, he acknowledges and affirms their praying and prophesying in the church (1 Cor. 11:2–16).
    
-   Instead, Paul is likely addressing a specific issue, women disrupting the exercise of spiritual gifts, because this was a recurring concern in the Corinthian churches.
    

#### **Assessment of the Argument:**

This is a challenging passage, and I fully support any egalitarian scholar who seeks to make Paul’s message consistent throughout his writings. The undeniable strength of the egalitarian reading is that if Paul intended to impose a universal rule of silence on women, _it would be difficult to reconcile with what he just previously affirmed about their participation in worship_. Thus, egalitarians strive to interpret Paul in a way that maintains coherence across the entire letter. Scholars such as Cynthia Long Westfall have provided valuable insights, demonstrating that there is historical precedent for what Paul may be addressing—namely, that women in certain cultural settings did openly interrupt proceedings or communal meals.[10](#footnote-10)

However, the weakness in these arguments, to me, is that they often risk reinforcing a problematic assumption. While my words may sound strong here, I would argue that, to some extent, they come across as unintentionally sexist. Let me explain. Most egalitarian scholars suggest that women were incorporating elements of their previous pagan or cultic practices into Christian worship and that Paul is specifically correcting these behaviors. But the lingering question remains, _why only women_? This argument implicitly assumes that women, more than men, were susceptible to carrying over their former ways of speaking and worshiping into the church. Egalitarian scholars often counter this by arguing that Paul is addressing both men and women in principle. However, as much as I try to consider this view charitably, I struggle to find that interpretation supported clearly in the text.

## 1 Timothy 2:11-15

> 11 Let a woman learn in silence with full submission. 12 _I permit no woman to teach or to have authority over a man; she is to keep silent._ 13 _For Adam was formed first, then Eve_; 14 and Adam was not deceived, but the woman was deceived and became a transgressor. 15 _Yet she will be saved through childbearing_, provided they continue in faith and love and holiness, with modesty. - 1 Ti 2:11–15.

Let’s jump straight to verse 12, “_I permit no woman to teach or to have authority over a man; she is to keep silent_”. Almost every word in this verse is contested, thus the egalitarian reading of these verses requires careful attention. For egalitarians, the main question boils down to: _When Paul says “teaching” and “authority,” does he mean it in a positive light or a negative one?_ In other words, is Paul talking about beautiful line-by-line John MacArthur-like expositional preaching _or_ false heresy being expounded by the women in Ephesus to dominate men?[11](#footnote-11) The egalitarian argument is obviously the latter.

Thus they read verse 12 as: “_I permit no women by teach \[false teachings\] to dominate men._”

What’s the evidence for this interpretation? First, the argument for a cultural reading ties back to what Paul has already said women can do, particularly regarding prophecy. The key question is: what’s the difference between prophesying and teaching? For egalitarians, the answer is—not much. Therefore, Paul cannot be saying that women are universally prohibited from teaching if he allows them to pray and prophesy in public worship. This suggests that something cultural is at play here (we’ll explore that more shortly).

Then verse 13 on the order of Creation. Complementarians tend to say there in lies universal application. But as we have already seen in 1 Corinthians, _that cannot be the case_.

I like Keener’s take.

> “\[Paul\] often applies biblical examples just to analogous groups within particular local congregations in his own day…Since Paul elsewhere uses Eve as an analogy for the gullibility of the whole Corinthian church (2 Cor. 11:3)—the men no less than the women—_**it is clear that he does not simply regard Eve as a standard symbol for women, any more than the consequences of Adam’s fall apply only to men in other Pauline passages**_ (Rom. 5:12–21; 1 Cor. 15:45–49).[12](#footnote-12)

While the appeal to the Creation order is interesting, for egalitarians, it is not seen as universally applicable in nature.

Next, verse 15 is the lynch pin the egalitarian argument.

> _“Yet she will be saved through childbearing”_

What on earth does Paul mean here? No one (that I know of) argues that Paul is suggesting two separate paths to the Kingdom of God. Instead, Paul is clearly responding to a growing issue in Ephesus. Several scholars have pointed out that during this time, a women’s sexual liberation movement (often referred to as the "New Women’s movement") was emerging in the first century. This movement explicitly celebrated freedom from childbearing and domestic responsibilities. In response, Paul reaffirms that having children is not only good but _godly_. I can already anticipate my complementarian compadres’ typing out their objections: "That’s not in the text!" But hold on.

Paul does seem to be addressing cultural issues related to women in this letter. First, he discusses their attire and reminds women to dress appropriately (1 Tim. 2:9). He then mentions individuals who are "forbidding marriage" (1 Tim 4:1-3). Additionally, later in the letter, Paul speaks about women who, "when their passions draw them away from Christ, desire to marry and so incur condemnation for having abandoned their former faith. Furthermore, they learn to be idle, going from house to house, and not only idle but also gossips and busybodies, saying things they should not" (1 Tim 5:11-13).

Therefore, at the very least, something significant is happening with women in Ephesus, and Paul seems to be directly addressing it in his letter.

#### **Assessment of the Argument:**

**Strengths**: When I first read Paul’s letters in college, I was convinced that 1st Timothy was the death of the egalitarian argument. However, I’m no longer so certain. If Paul isn’t specifically addressing an issue in Ephesus, I find it difficult to reconcile 1 Tim. 2:11-15 with the rest of Paul’s letters, particularly verse 15. The strength of this interpretation is that it helps maintain consistency in Paul’s overall message. Additionally, egalitarians have put considerable effort into in-depth word studies of nearly every term in this passage, offering valuable insights that support the idea that Paul isn’t giving a blanket statement on church structure. Instead, he is using negative language and imagery to describe something problematic happening in Ephesus. In light of this, my conclusion on the egalitarian reading of 1 Tim. 2:11-15 is, _touché_.

**Weakness:** I still have reservations about verse 12. The egalitarian argument that the words “teaching” and “authority” are undeniably negative seems questionable to me. What’s particularly interesting is that both complementarians and egalitarians have conducted extensive word studies on these terms and reached completely opposite conclusions. As a reader, it’s frustrating that both sides have confidently arrived at what they believe are definitive conclusions, claiming these words are either positive or negative. Moreover, the biggest weakness in the egalitarian interpretation is that it requires a fair amount of supplementation or denial. To make sense of Paul’s words, one has to introduce a woman-specific heresy, negative interpretations of teaching and authority (which would be unusual for Paul), and somewhat dismiss the invocation of the Creation order. Essentially, the egalitarian reading often requires looking beyond the text itself to make sense of it, which is always a problematic approach.

#### **Conclusion:**

My goal in this post is to demonstrate that egalitarians offer strong and plausible interpretations of the challenging Pauline texts. The persistent idea that egalitarians have abandoned biblical authority must be debunked as plainly false. Egalitarians present a robust, culturally grounded reading of Paul, and this perspective should be appreciated within the complementarian camp. The debate should no longer be framed as egalitarians valuing early church history while complementarians prioritize the texts. Or, to put it another way, egalitarians love Paul’s women (e.g., Priscilla) but not his words, and complementarians swim in Paul’s words and read over his women. The reality is that both camps deeply value the texts but emphasize them in different ways. For egalitarians, the emphasis is on the cultural context behind the text, ensuring Paul’s consistency. As for complementarians... well, you'll have to wait for the next post for that.
