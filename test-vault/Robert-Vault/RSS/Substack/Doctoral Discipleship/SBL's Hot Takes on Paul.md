### **1\. Douglas Campbell - Paul** _**Did**_ **Write Ephesians**

Douglas Campbell, a leading Paul scholar at Duke, began by calling for a return to making Ephesians Pauline again. Campbell was the first, but not the last, to bring up the new book _[Counting Paul: Scientificity, Fuzzy Math, and Ideology in Pauline Studies](https://www.amazon.com/Counting-Paul-Scientificity-Ideology-Pauline-ebook/dp/B0F2XCBR32/ref=sr_1_1?crid=2NUN7BVA4ES0M&dib=eyJ2IjoiMSJ9.xU8U8s384dNsMQvw0boVVmdR3OuHwl09S3UpuWy-nnj3XKxlesYCyrhqx11nE5R6e_bWLOMiHm6WH64SAWe50xYt4cA6dF3pJE069XX7P7DPsGzsu7gIGJA4PfghE9Uw8AKUoOendy96CRmEZllfZL4zFhil-II6rU31G9ZxvaFvYnRFUFFLAb91sZ_A8gyJjb2GWVptmtS6l6J-GcOo9ZxKxSHLLGH1oPgLaayueN4.U5Xz2Jv-eYzhJEKbdUjdeKjSGLr9ytCcl4l9Vxtn5gs&dib_tag=se&keywords=counting+paul&qid=1763951630&s=digital-text&sprefix=counting+paul%2Caps%2C116&sr=1-1)_ by Benjamin White. Campbell pointed out that many of the arguments for deutero-Pauline authorship of Ephesians have stemmed from a group of biblical scholars trying to figure out how to apply statistical analysis to Greek vocabulary. Campbell’s point was well taken. As a biblical scholar, I should be nowhere near a calculator. Additionally, Campbell states that the arguments for deutero-Pauline are question-begging and often concern subjective arguments about style, tone, logic, and other aspects.

### **2\. Jason Staples - Romans is a Synagogue Sermon (plus several minor hot takes)**

at NC State came out with several mino**r** hot takes. Such as: the authentic authorship of 2 Thessalonians, Philippians 2 is not pre-Pauline, Philemon is filled with eschatological jubilee language, and Romans was written with the knowledge of James’ letter. However, the _real_ main course hot take was that Paul’s letter to the Romans is a synagogue sermon, or at least filled with his typical points that he would preach at synagogues during his journeys. During the Q&A, Campbell argued that if Romans were a sermon, there would need to be an emphasis on Christ crucified, which Staples rebutted with Rom 1:4. Campbell again argued that no Jews in synagogues were considering not being circumcised, which Staples refuted with Philo, who deals with this exact issue.

### **3\. Paula Fredricksen - Jesus ἐξ ἀναστάσεως νεκρῶν (Rom 1:4)**

Fredricksen is Professor Emerita from Boston University and an absolute legend in the field of Pauline studies. Her hot take came from her 2017 book, _[Paul: The Pagan’s Apostle](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=paul+the+pagan+apostle&adgrpid=187230189358&hvadid=779664816845&hvdev=c&hvexpln=0&hvlocphy=1018127&hvnetw=g&hvocijid=5039029141380200888--&hvqmt=e&hvrand=5039029141380200888&hvtargid=kwd-2084829820692&hydadcr=22165_13655718_97883&mcid=20299ddb13753d6facaa86484545de5a&tag=googhydr-20&ref=pd_sl_1reuwnb35i_e),_ which she believed had not received enough attention. She argues that, based on the Greek syntax in Romans 1:4, Paul believes that Jesus’ designation of power was not related to his resurrection of the dead, but rather to the general resurrection of the dead (cf. 1 Cor 15:12-21). Or to put it differently, that the cosmic acknowledgment of Christ that occurs at the _parousia._ Her argument stems from the Greek words ἐξ (by) and ἀναστάσεως νεκρῶν (resurrection of the dead _ones_). As Paula states in her book, “Rather, according to Paul’s Greek, here as elsewhere in his letters, Christ’s manifestation in power—in public, to the cosmos—is tied to the general resurrection, thus to the coming of the Kingdom at the End” (p. 142).

### **4\. Michael Peppard - 2 Timothy is 1 Timothy, and Oh BTW Paul Wrote It**

Michael is my _doktorvater_, so I am somewhat biased in his favor regarding his scholarship. Michael argued that, based on several statistical analyses, 2 Timothy is a genuinely Pauline work. But Michael acknowledged that this has been argued elsewhere, especially by [Jerome Murphy O’Connor](https://www.jstor.org/stable/44089062). Michael’s real hot take was that 2 Timothy was _actually_ 1 Timothy in antiquity. For his evidence, he cites a quote from Clement, who claims to be quoting 1 Timothy, but the content is clearly from 2 Timothy. He then examined the Codex Claromontanus, which has a section that orders books in terms of length, and the Codex states that 2 Timothy is _longer_ than 1st, which it is not. 1 Timothy is longer, thus the logic being, 1 Timothy in the Codex is actually the real 2 Timothy. Which I thought was fascinating. Thus, 2 Timothy was first and authentic, while 1 Timothy came later and was forged.

### **My Thoughts**

Overall, I was underwhelmed with most of the takes because very few presented substantial evidence, aside from Michael’s (again, I am biased). But I guess that’s the point of a hot take? It’s just a take, maybe not all the way thought out yet. However, I did find it hilariously ironic that over half of the “hot takes” are standard positions held by 95% of Paul's readers worldwide, namely that 2 Thessalonians, Ephesians, and 2 Timothy were written by Paul. But overall, this was a very enjoyable session, with some spicy Q&A action, which is always fun.
