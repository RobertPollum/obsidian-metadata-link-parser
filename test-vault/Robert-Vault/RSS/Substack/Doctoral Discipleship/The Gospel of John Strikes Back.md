The Fourth Gospel is fervently beloved by Christians while equally dismissed in biblical scholarship for the exact same reasons. In Christendom, John’s Gospel is prized because Jesus is unambiguously God. He’s also a miracle worker, raising folks from the dead and turning water into wine. John’s Jesus is simply glorious, which is the problem for those involved in higher criticism. Traditionally, John’s Gospel has been disavowed for any historical value in terms of getting to the “authentic” or “genuine” Jesus of history. That is to say, for scholars, John’s Jesus is simply too good to be true.

However, this accusation isn’t totally off base. To put some numbers on it, John’s Gospel is _90% original_ (don’t ask me how they count this), while Matthew, Mark, and Luke (the Synoptic Gospels) are remarkably similar. John isn’t interested in telling you stories about Jesus’ birth, baptism, transfiguration, or institution of the Lord’s Supper. You’ve heard all of those; it’s time for some new material. Stories like the rising of Lazarus, the Samaritan woman at the well, the appearances to Peter post-resurrection, and many more are all uniquely Johannine. Thus, with such historical peculiarities, John’s Gospel has often been subjected to three major accusations: 1) it is a historically untenable source in terms of discovering the authentic Jesus, 2) the Fourth Gospel does not have a relationship with the Synoptic Gospels, and 3) John’s Gospel is too late to be considered a genuine account of Jesus’ life.

As we speak, all of these positions are being challenged with great arguments.

## **History in High Christology? Revisiting John’s Jesus**

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!XNnm!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F5c15797e-fa1a-4714-a3d2-68252eca0749_1004x1500.jpeg)



](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!XNnm!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F5c15797e-fa1a-4714-a3d2-68252eca0749_1004x1500.jpeg)

Those of you who are unfamiliar with the Quest(s) for the Historical Jesus, I genuinely envy you. To be overly simplistic, the Quest(s) for the Historical Jesus are periods in biblical scholarship marked by a variety of methods to determine who or what within the Synoptic Gospels is indicative of the genuine historical Jesus. The primary supposition for questers is that the Synoptics are theologically motivated and therefore cannot be considered entirely or even primarily historical documents. Over the years, various methodological emphases have marked each quest from philosophical rationalism, form criticism, to Jesus within Judaism; however, each quest has crumbled in its ability to set a convincing methodological standard for what is and is not historical within the Synoptic Gospels. Though one common thread between these quests is that the Gospel of John is irrelevant for the study of the historical Jesus. At least until now.

More recently, scholars like Craig Blomberg and Paul Anderson have questioned this anti-John approach. In Blomberg’s recent and excellent work, _[Jesus the Purifier](https://www.amazon.com/Jesus-Purifier-Gospel-Fourth-Historical/dp/1540962954/ref=sr_1_1?crid=TD2MI09T0N77&dib=eyJ2IjoiMSJ9.FGeJepYJQ6hNzj7cRtQgx6tHNiB-96FstwNs83MmWScLOThZQFr2wUscRqePlGbB06MbKhWgVaOmoGBWGUCBmBdUdxISO_n7U3glWNQk70V4qCmYhjaSmAXFwhax1SXnqX-uZIIBUcmRKgg6uNkPJnjAPdJCPniUXEhZHn2WhXaas--R1HTOR2DkLjbFoZHr.-lgzBQGqxg6LCV7jmrhGFCAYGel89mZc5cS-3DgmGT4&dib_tag=se&keywords=Jesus+the+Purifier&qid=1753803132&s=books&sprefix=jesus+the+purifier%2Cstripbooks%2C173&sr=1-1)_, he analyzes John’s Gospel using the theme of Jesus as a purifier and an authentication criterion known as “cutting against the grain,” which focuses on a Gospel writer’s major redaction emphasis. Blomberg works through the entirety of John’s Gospel and shows how there are a vast number of moments that John describes that are historically and religiously accurate to the first century. For example, in John 2, Jesus at the wedding at Cana, Blomberg points out that John does not highlight the wine but rather highlights that there were six stone water jars used for “Jewish rites of purification” (2:6) and mentions three times that Jesus started with water (2:6, 7, 9). Blomberg convincingly argues that the emphasis for John is on the elements of Judaism and how Jesus works within the elements to fulfill them (p. 239).

While this is an interesting way to interpret the story, Blomberg adds historical depth by showing how these exact types of water jars have since been discovered by law-abiding Jews in the region. Take another example, at the pool on the Sabbath, which only John recounts, Jesus heals a man, and it states, “Now there is a pool in Jerusalem” (5:2). Archaeological evidence has recently confirmed that such a pool did exist. Again, this purification story emphasized by John also provides historical detail. Blomberg points this out repeatedly; where John emphasizes the purification power of Jesus, there are also bits of historical detail that must be considered accurate. Thus, if John’s Gospel proves reliable in such historically insignificant details, such as a water jar or a pool, why are we so quick to doubt his information about Jesus?

While John’s Gospel may be unique, does that mean it is historically unreliable? With each passing year, the answer is increasingly becoming not-so-fast. The great Albert Schweitzer once commented that the origins of the Quest for the Historical Jesus were openly anti-theological. That all claims of divinity and miracles must be set aside, and thus John’s Gospel was banned from the conversation. As future quests will undoubtedly incorporate the Gospel of John, such anti-theological ambitions must equally be put to rest. And researchers must grapple with the uncomfortable question of what happens when a deeply theological text is also historical.

## **John, the Synoptic Gospel?**

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!XpQT!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1ff03c41-a2c3-4e77-952a-3f8bb0730dfc_596x894.jpeg)



](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!XpQT!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1ff03c41-a2c3-4e77-952a-3f8bb0730dfc_596x894.jpeg)

John’s originality has led to its exile from consideration of being a Synoptic Gospel (Greek for “seen together”); however, a question that has long plagued scholarship is what relationship, if any, does John’s Gospel have with the Synoptics? Some have historically argued that John had no relationship to the Synoptics, and any overlap between the two can conveniently be attributed to oral sources of some sort. Over the years, scholars have begun to recognize that John does parallel the Synoptics in ways that are a bit more complicated than previously assumed, thus complicating the question of John’s relationship to the Synoptics.

Enter Mark Goodacre, a superb scholar at Duke who studied under the giants like E.P. Sanders and N.T. Wright. Goodacre is provocative, which I love. He’s argued persuasively [against the existence of Q,](https://www.amazon.com/Case-Against-Studies-Priority-Synoptic/dp/1563383349/ref=sr_1_3?dib=eyJ2IjoiMSJ9.hRhAgvhwSF80ZlWlD7UczSOJhVt9Wu5JiW1-dopX39Hvxyn8HBBiWqfimxvH9cKhl3fUXXz61T_DK-y4BIJ_pgZsl7fHpUtIPJTnDx6TH-2Cx5JhRkfzxc-NTJcWsYi3JDgQfhT0aZ4RkuqN7CEokHIxCQugeiYTDA7x4qwi9Su_Jniih0OP6E4mfeeaN1Wiu2cy-2u4C76-Kqd-9pyKJHYFGxNsSvRcSpRkclZAuxk.JyMeZe_YjE7b9ZLmUy9Fs9n4jCtS1T8kXO9pF2Jz-ws&dib_tag=se&qid=1753803772&refinements=p_27%3AMark+Goodacre&s=books&sr=1-3&text=Mark+Goodacre) that the [Gospel of Thomas had access to the Synoptics](https://www.amazon.com/Thomas-Gospels-Thomass-Familiarity-Synoptics/dp/0802867480/ref=sr_1_5?dib=eyJ2IjoiMSJ9.hRhAgvhwSF80ZlWlD7UczSOJhVt9Wu5JiW1-dopX39Hvxyn8HBBiWqfimxvH9cKhl3fUXXz61T_DK-y4BIJ_pgZsl7fHpUtIPJTnDx6TH-2Cx5JhRkfzxc-NTJcWsYi3JDgQfhT0aZ4RkuqN7CEokHIxCQugeiYTDA7x4qwi9Su_Jniih0OP6E4mfeeaN1Wiu2cy-2u4C76-Kqd-9pyKJHYFGxNsSvRcSpRkclZAuxk.JyMeZe_YjE7b9ZLmUy9Fs9n4jCtS1T8kXO9pF2Jz-ws&dib_tag=se&qid=1753803788&refinements=p_27%3AMark+Goodacre&s=books&sr=1-5&text=Mark+Goodacre), and now his [forthcoming volume](https://www.amazon.com/Fourth-Synoptic-Gospel-Knowledge-Matthew/dp/0802875130) is on the Gospel of John’s relationship to the Synoptics. Based on several interviews that Goodacre has done promoting the book, his argument is essentially threefold. First, we know there are many moments where John parallels the Synoptics. Second, there are an equal number of moments where it appears John assumes the reader knows the stories via the Synoptics. Third, if John does parallel and work off the material of the Synoptics, then there are, by definition, moments where John is Synoptic. Goodacre doesn’t advocate that John should be called a Synoptic, but his point is that the category of Synoptic really comes down to “degrees.” After all, the Gospel of Luke is quite different from Matthew, but nonetheless is considered a Synoptic. Thus, even though John is radically different than Matthew, Mark, and Luke, it still has a relationship with and can behave like the Synoptics.

One point of Goodacre’s argument that is quite thrilling is John’s use of naming. Let’s look at an example.

> _“Then **Simon Peter**, having a sword, drew it and struck the high priest’s servant and cut off his **right ear**. (The servant’s name was **Malchus**.)” - Jn 18:10._

This account is covered in all four Gospels, but there’s an interesting detail in John’s version. John mentioned which ear is cut off, the right one. Only Luke’s version also says that (Luke 22:50). Such a minor detail makes it highly likely that John used Luke. But furthermore, notice what John does here. He mentions that it is Simon Peter who draws the sword; in every other account, the person is anonymous. But notice John gives another name to a nameless figure in the Synoptics. In the parenthetical note, John identifies the servant’s name as Malchus. What is John doing here? Like any great writer, he knows you know the story (i.e., the Synoptics). He also knows that these characters have been nameless, so John steps in and gives them a name, and this is just one verse. John does this all the time. Thus, John’s relationship to the Synoptics may be less than that of a distant relative and more akin to that of a fraternal twin.

## **John the Early Evangelist**?

Shane Rosenthal over at Humble Skeptic has been doing some great work in compiling resources in regard to the paradigm shift that is taking place in Johannine studies. Recently, [Rosenthal](https://www.humbleskeptic.com/p/the-date-of-johns-gospel-revisited) alerted me to George van Kooten’s [article](http://file:///Users/danielkunkel/Downloads/nt-article-p310_2.pdf) in the _Novum Testamentum_ concerning the date of John’s Gospel. Traditionally, it has been argued that John’s Gospel comes much later than the Synoptics. Dates for John have ranged from 50 CE to 170 CE; there is no other New Testament book that has such a discrepancy in terms of dating. As with most dates and the Gospels, the question comes down to indications of the Jewish revolt in 70 CE, that is to say, can we as readers discern the effects of the devastation of the destruction of the Second Temple? If so, then our source definitely postdates the Jewish revolt.

van Kooten’s argument on John’s date is not necessarily new; scholars like John A. T. Robinson and Daniel Wallace have been arguing for a version of this for years. The first part of van Kooten’s argument is derived from John 5:2:

> _Now there is_ (ἔστιν) _in Jerusalem (_ἐν τοῖς Ἱεροσολύμοις) _by the Sheep Gate a pool (_κολυμβήθρα)_, in Aramaic called Bethesda, which has five roofed colonnades._

Excuse the technicalities, but grammatically speaking, van Kooten demonstrates that when you have the following construction in Greek:

“There is” formula + “locative dative” (in Jerusalem) + architectural reference in the nominative case (pool).

It is often used when the writer is describing something that is current, not past. That is to say, John is actively describing a scene that would have almost certainly been destroyed by the Jewish revolt, as if he were currently looking at it. In other words, John seems to be writing from a place _prior_ to the Jewish revolt. van Kooten goes on to argue that Luke and Matthew _used_ John instead of the other way around. I think this is highly unlikely. Even folks like Clement and Eusebius regarded John as the last of the four Gospels. Nonetheless, the argument is bringing John’s date back into question. Personally, I am not sold on van Kooten’s argument, but I do think it helps us to reassess that John’s Gospel could have been written during or right after the Synoptics.

Thus, we are currently in the midst of a John revolution. Should John be considered reliable for the study of the historical Jesus? Did John have a relationship with the Synoptics? Was John written before 70 CE? Who knows, but the fact that we are even asking these questions shows just how much John is being seriously reevaluated. Long live the Gospel of John.
