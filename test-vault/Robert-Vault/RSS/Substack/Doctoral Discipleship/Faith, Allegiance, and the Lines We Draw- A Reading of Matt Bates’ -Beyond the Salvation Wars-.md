_“Has the Church Misunderstood the Gospel for 2000 Years?”_

This provocative question is posed by Albert Mohler, Tom Schreiner, Jim Hamilton, and Steve Wellum (hereafter, SBTS & Co.) [in their hour-long](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=tp3P0FYq0Rg) response to Matthew Bates’ latest and already controversial book, _**[Beyond the Salvation Wars: Why Both Protestants and Catholics Must Reimagine How We Are Saved](https://www.amazon.com/Beyond-Salvation-Wars-Protestants-Catholics/dp/1540961737)**._

Thanks for reading! Subscribe for free to receive new posts and support my work.

Engaging in this debate, at times, feels like being a child caught between two parents. I studied at Southern Seminary, came to know Tom Schreiner personally, and hold the deepest respect for his scholarship. My time at SBTS was profoundly formative, and I regard it as an exceptional place to study and visit. Thus, I carry a strong sense of loyalty to Southern, both to the institution and the community it fosters (plus the campus is simply beautiful).

At the same time, I’ve come to appreciate the voices of the scholars who align with Bates, such as Nijay Gupta, Scot McKnight, and Mike Bird (Bates & Co.). Though these two groups agree on perhaps 99.3% of theological content, within the Protestant evangelical landscape, they represent starkly different postures. SBTS & Co_._ is firmly grounded in the hardline Baptist tradition. Bates & Co., by contrast, are more adventurous, deeply shaped by the work of Tom Wright and Jimmy Dunn.

I have to admit, I’m a contrarian by nature and tend to root for the underdog. So when I saw SBTS & Co. criticizing this book, despite my closer theological alignment with them, my instinct was to side with Bates. I love a good New York Giants vs. New England Patriots dynamic in biblical scholarship, and I set out to see for myself what to make of Bates’s arguments. What follows is not a formal review, but rather my general reflections on what I think Bates gets right, and where, preferably in a setting like the _[On Script](https://onscript.study/)_ podcast, I’d enjoy the opportunity to challenge him.

## What’s This Debate About?

At the heart of this debate lies the question of how accurately the Reformers understood the Gospel. In the historic divide between Catholics and Protestants, the Reformation articulated a theology that came to be summarized in the five _Solas_. The one most directly in Bates’s crosshairs is _Sola Fide_, the conviction that faith, understood as belief in Jesus rather than works, is the sole requirement for salvation. Yet much has changed since the time of the Reformers. In the centuries that followed, biblical scholarship has advanced dramatically. Entire languages have been recovered, ancient commentaries translated, the Dead Sea Scrolls published, and Gnostic texts rediscovered (See Bates, 97, 119, 120). Each of these developments has brought us closer to the historical and cultural world of early Christianity, enabling more precise grammatical, etymological, and contextual engagement with Scripture.

Thus, Bates enters the conversation on salvation with a scholarly lens, aiming to present readers with the most current arguments and evidence in biblical scholarship to develop a more precise understanding of the Gospel as articulated in the New Testament. Bates is not here to defend the Reformation or a denomination; in fact, it is mostly the opposite. From the outset, Bates makes this clear:

> I recognize that the short-term effect of a sharply pointed book like this may be more squabbling. In fact, let’s get this out of the way now: readers who are inflexibly committed to a specific denomination, church confession, or systematic theology of salvation will probably hate this book. If that is you, then you have been forewarned: proceed at your own risk. But for those who love to rediscover Scripture and early Christian history, for those who are eager to reappropriate foundational truths, please read on. I trust you’ll find this exploration worthwhile (pp. 8-9).

Now that we’ve been warned, let’s take a look at what I appreciate in Bates’s argument and where I have some questions.

## Cheering Bates On: _Pistis_

There are two primary sections of this book that I was reading like a mystery thriller, they had me on the edge of my seat! The first is Bates’ argument about the age-old Catholic and Protestant divide between faith and works. Bates argues that the Protestant Reformation didn’t _fully_ understand what the word faith (Gk, _pistis_) meant in its Greco-Roman context. Bates writes:

> _“Works are fundamentally saving as an embodiment of allegiance_. We cannot earn salvation through works of the law (works-righteousness or legalism), but good works have a foundational positive saving function because they are part of _pistis_, the word traditionally translated as ‘faith’” (p. 24).

It’s important to recognize that Bates builds on a paradigm popularized by the so-called Protestant “bogeyman” of Pauline studies, E. P. Sanders, and what came to be known as the New Perspective on Paul (NPP), though Bates does not cite Sanders directly. I’m tempted to recount the full history of this debate, especially given how frequently Sanders’s work is criticized by those who haven’t actually read him or any Second Temple Jewish texts. But in brief: Sanders, building on earlier German and Jewish scholarship from the 19th and 20th centuries, reexamined a wide range of Jewish literature, including Tannaitic texts, the Dead Sea Scrolls, the Apocrypha, and more, to assess their soteriology. His central claim is that Judaism, in its various forms (Rabbinic, Second Temple, Qumranic, etc.), was not a works-based religion. Rather, Jews understood themselves to be brought into covenant with God by divine grace, and their obedience, what Sanders called _covenantal nomism_, was a response to that election, not a means of earning salvation. In this view, salvation was grounded in God’s mercy and covenant faithfulness, not legalistic merit.

This reorientation radically reshaped how we understand Paul’s theology. According to Sanders, Paul wasn’t critiquing Judaism for being works-based; he was critiquing “works of the Law” because they did not intrinsically lead to Christ. In this paradigm, works are appropriate for _remaining_ in the covenant, but not for _entering_ it. Thus, works were a part of the salvation process in that they enabled one to stay in the covenant. This whole debate has been taken up by the great scholars Jimmy Dunn, Tom Wright, and Scot McKnight, who furthered Sanders’ work to better understand what Paul means by faith and works, and argue that there need not be such a dividing line between the two.

For Bates, when Paul speaks of “faith,” he is not contrasting it with “works” in the traditional Protestant sense. Rather, drawing from the semantic and cultural world of the first century, Bates argues that faith did not simply mean intellectual assent or mental belief. It encompassed embodied loyalty, what he calls “trusting allegiance,” a form of bodily submission that naturally included action, or what we might otherwise call “works.” In this way, Bates is reframing the classic faith/works dichotomy, suggesting that the distinction itself may be a misreading of the Pauline context. Bates thus proposes that “_works are foundationally saving as an embodiment of allegiance_” (p. 24). In contrast to classical Protestantism, where works serve as evidence or confirmation of salvation, Bates views faith and works as inseparable. To split works from faith is akin to trying to separate hydrogen from oxygen in water: it simply cannot be done. For Bates, works are not merely the fruit of faith; they are its embodiment.

Here, I think Bates is on to something. I am not a full-blown NPP guy, nor is Bates, but I think Bates is correct in the foundation of the argument that the idea of “faith” in the New Testament has a deeper dimension than just mental acknowledgement, that is, I don’t think reciting the Sinners Prayer is the fundamental saving action. We see this in the classic James and Paul divide. In my reading, Paul and James are addressing different concerns under the same term _pistis_. Thus, showing that the term _pistis_ did have a variety of meanings or dimensions. James is explicit in rejecting the notion that mere intellectual assent constitutes as true _pistis_: “You see that a person is justified by works and not by faith alone” (Jas 2:24). (Your annual reminder that Protestant declaration _sola fide_, faith alone, appears only once in Scripture, and there it is explicitly denied.) It’s no surprise, then, that Luther famously loathed the epistle of James, as it presents faith in terms quite different from his own framework. For both James and Paul, it seems clear that faith and works were never meant to be _fully_ divorced. Thus, I would agree with Bates that faith and works have a much more robust relationship than classical Protestantism argues for.

## Cheering Bates On: Baptism

I’ll keep this section brief, as I find myself in strong agreement with nearly everything Bates says about baptism. He argues that:

> “_In the New Testament and during earliest Christianity (through AD 200), baptism was deemed effective for personal salvation only on the basis of voluntary personalist repentance and expressed loyalty to King Jesus_” (p. 97).

To which I say, amen! Like Bates, I affirm _credo-baptism_, the view that baptism should follow personal faith, not be administered to infants. This section of the book has much to commend. Bates effectively challenges the traditional support for infant baptism, particularly the appeal to “household” baptisms in Acts. He argues that:

> “Household baptisms are described (e.g., Acts 16:15, 33; 18:8; 1 Cor. 1:16). These were based on the choice of a household leader—usually the _paterfamilias_—but we have no descriptions of children participating, nor any indication that members of a household ever undertook baptism in anything other than a conscious voluntary capacity” (p. 111).

With careful historical attention, Bates offers a compelling rebuttal to both Catholic and Reformed understandings of baptism, one that I believe my Presbyterian friends would do well to seriously consider. From the mode of baptism (immersion) to its meaning as a public declaration of allegiance to King Jesus, I think Bates gets it exactly right.

But now let us turn to where Bates and I part ways.

## Challenging Bates

One piece of disagreement that I have with Bates is what he understands to be the true Gospel. For Bates, the Gospel summarization is: “Jesus is the saving king” (p. 238). In Bates’ argument, the primary way the New Testament displays the Gospel is not the cross, but his reign as king or the Messiah. As he states,

> “Surprisingly, neither Jesus’s death nor the cross is what the Bible most emphasizes when it describes the Gospel’s content…Rather, it is Jesus’s present reign as the Christ or king” (p. 33).

Here, Bates is critiquing the Protestant view of the cross as the _primary_ point of the Gospel. And I first want to say that I strongly agree with Bates that Jesus’s identity as King/Messiah is _a_ central aspect of the Gospel. After all, Jesus began his public ministry by announcing the arrival of the Kingdom of God, he was inaugurating a new reign under a new King. This kingdom emphasis is often underdeveloped or quickly subordinated to Jesus’s role as Savior in classical Protestant frameworks.

However, this is also where I begin to diverge from Bates. To be candid, I found it surprising that in his scriptural assessment of the Gospel’s content, he does not engage with what is likely the earliest Gospel summary in the New Testament:

> “For God has destined us not for wrath but for obtaining salvation through our Lord Jesus Christ, who died for us, so that whether we are awake or asleep we may live with him” (1 Thess 5:9–10).

For Paul, the earliest articulation of the Gospel is this: Jesus died. Yes, the text affirms that Jesus is the Christ, a profound confession, but it also calls him _Lord_ (_kyrios_), a title that in the Septuagint frequently refers to God himself. The emphasis here is not on choosing between Jesus as King or as crucified Savior, but rather on affirming that _the Lord and King died_. Paul’s Gospel summary does not sideline the cross; it puts the crucified Messiah at the center of divine kingship. Any account of the Gospel that downplays this reality risks detaching Jesus’s reign from the redemptive means through which it was established.

Furthermore, I believe Bates’s emphasis on Jesus’ messiahship causes him to downplay the significance of other titles attributed to Jesus. For example, in discussing how Mark summarizes the Gospel, Bates argues:

> “The first words do not say, ‘The beginning of the Gospel of Mark,’ as if this story is about Mark himself and his authorship of a ‘Gospel.’ Nor does it say, ‘A beginning of the Gospels about Jesus the Christ’…Nor does Mark write, ‘Beginning of a gospel about Jesus the Christ,’ suggesting that there could be many different gospels and that his is merely one offering. Rather, Mark tellingly writes, ‘Beginning of the gospel of Jesus the Christ.’ Mark firmly asserts that there is only one definitive gospel. And he proceeds to tell a story about how Jesus was sent, was anointed as the king, proclaimed his forthcoming rule as king, and died as a ransom for many, and then his tomb was discovered empty” (p. 41).

But what Bates fails to do is finish the verse. Mark 1:1 reads, _“_The beginning of the gospel of Jesus Christ, _the Son of God.”_ While there is some manuscript debate over the inclusion of “the Son of God,” growing scholarly consensus affirms its authenticity as original to Mark. Yet Bates focuses exclusively on Jesus as _Messiah_ and does not meaningfully engage with the other titles attributed to Jesus, such as Son of God or Son of Man, except in passing when quoting Scripture. In my reading, this is a significant oversight. It results in a Gospel presentation that centers almost exclusively on divine kingship and reign, while undervaluing critical aspects like Jesus’s divine sonship, preexistence, and cosmic authority. These elements are not peripheral, they shape how the Gospel is understood.

Moreover, the title messiah was not unique to Jesus; others in the Old Testament and Second Temple period were called “anointed ones.” _That title alone was never the full content of the good news_. While I agree that Jesus’s kingship is a foundational aspect of the Gospel, it must be held alongside his other titles. For instance, when Stephen is about to be martyred, he doesn’t merely see Jesus reigning; he sees _the Son of Man_ standing at the right hand of God (Acts 7:56). That vision, and that title, speaks volumes about who Jesus is and what the Gospel proclaims.

## Challenging Bates: The Individualized Gospel

Time and again, Bates argues in almost every chapter (the true Gospel, election, the indwelling of the Holy Spirit) that practically nothing in the process of salvation is meant to be individualized (excluding baptism). His main evidence for this is the use of the Greek plural pronouns (you/y’all, ours, etc.) in order to argue that the New Testament presents salvation in fundamentally communal, not individual, terms. As charitable as I can be, this is not an argument. These texts are, quite obviously, written to communities. Paul writes to groups of believers, so of course, he uses plural pronouns (though he doesn’t in Philemon, which Bates doesn’t talk about). What else is he supposed to use? This does not mean the Gospel excludes individual dimensions or that personal faith and election are theological missteps. While I fully acknowledge the corporate nature of ancient Mediterranean society, _the presence of plural pronouns in epistolary literature is not a sufficient basis for constructing a doctrine that eliminates individual soteriological language altogether_.

For example, Bates’ claim that New Testament predestination applies only to groups, not individuals, brings the issue to a head. He writes, “Scripture teaches the election of a group in Christ for final salvation but does not teach that specific individuals have been predestined or otherwise chosen ahead of time to belong to that group or be excluded from it” (p. 148). But is this distinction truly meaningful? I would argue it is not. Groups are made up of individuals, and to say that God predestines a group without predestinating its members reduces election to a vague abstraction. If no individual is predestined to be part of the group, what exactly is being predestined?

Thus, I find Bates’s evidence for a communal approach to salvation and sanctification to be flimsy at best. The connection he draws between Paul’s use of plural pronouns and Paul’s broader theological framework strikes me as unconvincing—and, at times, misleading.

## Other Critiques

As an aspiring scholar and avid reader, there’s one type of phrase I always flag when I come across it in a book or article: the absolute claim that a belief or argument has “no basis whatsoever.” Bates makes such claims frequently; here are just a few examples:

> “We must admit that, candidly considered, the story of God’s prior-to-creation secret choice of individual humans for eternal life over against others _is not found in the Bible at all._” (p. 132)

> _“ Scripture does not teach_ individual election for eternal life._”_ (p. 137)

> “While a person who is presently born of God has the Holy Spirit, _we have no evidence_ that someone who is presently ‘born of God’ or who presently possesses ‘eternal life’ cannot die spiritually if they renounce King Jesus.” (p. 182)

> “Despite the overwhelming popularity of imputed righteousness among Scripture-loving Protestants, imputed righteousness _is not a biblical metaphor at all_” (p. 218)

In reviewing books, I genuinely strive to approach each work with charity, and I found the repeated use of absolute phrases like “no basis whatsoever” disheartening for two key reasons. First, the stated aim of this book is to offer a third way in the so-called salvation wars. That goal would seem to call for a tone that is warm, generous, and winsome. Yet, I did not find that tone here. Though Bates does warn us that this book will be pointed, I found such repeated claims to be not only unnecessary but incorrect.

For example, in critiquing the Calvinistic notion of “once saved, always saved,” Bates claims there is no biblical evidence supporting such a view. Early in the chapter, he surveys a few commonly cited passages, most notably John 10:29: “My Father, who has given them to me, is greater than all; no one can snatch them out of my Father’s hand.” Bates acknowledges that, on the surface, this appears to support eternal security. However, he argues that Jesus is referring specifically to external threats, writing, “Although Jesus will never cast away any who approach him (John 6:37), and no external power can snatch God’s people away from Jesus or the Father (10:28–29), personal failure to remain in Jesus…will result in destruction” (p. 165). In other words, while no external force can separate believers from God, one’s own unfaithfulness or “unrepentant departure” can.

Yet this interpretation overreaches. The Greek in John 10:29 does not explicitly distinguish between external and internal threats; the phrase “no one” (_oudeis_) can just as easily be read as “no person” or “nothing,” encompassing both categories. To say the passage excludes internal failure is not textually grounded; it’s interpretive. Therefore, Bates’s claim that there is _no_ evidence for “once saved, always saved” is itself overstated. His interpretation is one possible reading, not the definitive one.

Second, such sweeping dismissals strike me as, and I say this with love, intellectually arrogant. _Are we really to believe that countless scholars, many with doctorates, years of research, and fluency in multiple ancient languages, who have reached different conclusions than Bates have done so without any evidential basis_? That seems implausible at best. This is perhaps what I’ve found most frustrating, not only with Bates but with other scholars who take a similar approach. It is entirely possible to hold a strong position without demeaning those who disagree. I recognize this isn’t a critique of his evidence per se, but I do think it’s important to name. I’m a Protestant PhD student at a Catholic university—I am, in many ways, the ideal reader for this book. I long for a work that offers a fresh, compelling vision for Christian life, grounded in the best of what biblical studies has uncovered in recent decades.

At the same time, I come from an academic tradition that values charity, humility, and intellectual caution. We’re taught to state our evidence clearly, acknowledge our limits and biases, and then make our case without overstating it. That’s why I’m skeptical of scholars who write in ways that dismiss opposing views with strong or absolute language, without acknowledging what their claims truly are, interpretations, however well-informed.

## Conclusion

In conclusion, I want to emphasize that I was genuinely rooting for this book. As someone increasingly engaged with the thornier questions in New Testament studies, I was excited to see Bates offer a lay-accessible introduction to debates that are typically reserved for the academic world, and to do so with an eye toward practical Christian life. I also want to say that I genuinely appreciate Bates as a scholar. Though I’ve never met him, I’ve listened to his podcast for years and have found much in his writing to enjoy and reflect on.

But I didn’t finish this book with joy, I finished it with frustration. And not for the reasons Bates anticipates in his warnings throughout the book. I’m not tied to a particular denomination, and I’m certainly not resistant to change; I regularly revise my views on major New Testament issues. My frustration comes from the book’s missed potential. I was hoping for a thoughtful, well-evidenced, and winsome call to reexamine long-held assumptions, a book that could embody the spirit of _semper reformanda_ while doing so charitably and carefully. Instead, I found too many instances where the evidence was both understated and overstated, and where sweeping claims were made that ignored legitimate counter-evidence.

In the end, this approach left me more skeptical than convinced. And so, it seems, the war continues…
