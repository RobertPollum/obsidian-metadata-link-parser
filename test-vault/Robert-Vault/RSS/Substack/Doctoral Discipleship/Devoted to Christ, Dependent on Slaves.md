We’re never short on problems in New Testament studies. From the hundreds of thousands, perhaps millions, of textual variants in the manuscript tradition, to discrepancies in how Judas died (cf. [Matt. 27:1–10; Acts 1:18–20](https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Matthew%2027%3A3-10%2C%20Acts%201%3A18-20&version=NRSVUE)), to lingering questions like: Why did Paul actually write Romans? Is the John of Revelation the same as the one behind the Gospel and epistles? Or who wrote the Gospels? These long-standing debates have been granting academic tenure for ages.

Yet most of these issues are historical and textual, not ethical. When we entertain the question(s) of slavery in early Christianity, we move into far more unsettling territory. Because the question is not whether the early church participated in or accommodated Greco-Roman slavery. The question is—why did they?

### Freedom Written by Bound Hands

_“I, Tertius, who wrote this letter, greet you in the Lord.” - Romans 16:22_

Romans 16 is every egalitarian’s dream, its got all the hits: Phoebe (16:1), Prisca (named before her husband Aquila!) in 16:3, and, of course, the apostle(?) Junia in 16:7. But nestled among these more well-known names is a lesser-discussed figure: Tertius, the scribe, who in a rare moment breaks the fourth wall to greet the recipients himself (16:22). This naturally raises a question: _Did Paul use slaves to write his own letters?_ The answer is likely in the affirmative. For example, in Galatians, Paul’s most fiery letter, he explicitly mentions taking the pen from the scribe, writing “See with what large letters I am writing to you with my own hand” (Gal. 6:11), a detail that also implies that Paul, the greatest theologian of all time, was struck with poor handwriting. Similar notes appear at the end of other Pauline letters (1 Cor 16:21; Col 4:18; 2 Thess 3:17).

_Most of us picture Paul writing his epistles as depicted in this painting, but we know this is not true._

Paul’s consistent use of scribes and secretaries may lead one to imagine these scribes as ancient equivalents of modern stenographers or executive assistants, but that’s almost certainly inaccurate. In many cases, the people physically writing Paul’s letters were likely enslaved individuals trained as scribes in the Greco-Roman world. Writing in the ancient world was grueling, laborious, and physically uncomfortable work. Thus, it is a sobering irony that in Romans 16, a chapter overflowing with evidence of men and women partnering equally in ministry, Tertius inserts himself with a greeting, not-so-quietly reminding us that such equality did not extend to everyone.

Let’s move past the scribes for a moment and look at what Paul writes in his letter to Philemon.

### When a Prisoner Writes About a Slave

_Paul with Tychicus from his Epistle to the Ephesians_

I have to admit, _I love Paul’s letter to Philemon_. For all the uncomfortable questions it raises about slavery and early Christianity, it remains one of the most powerful and personal letters in the New Testament. (I once spent an entire semester researching _a single word_ that Paul uses in Philemon, _presbytēs_ in Philem 9.)

The basic storyline is that Paul has developed a relationship in prison with a slave named Onesimus. (While many commentators assume he’s a runaway, the letter itself never says so explicitly.) Paul also knows Onesimus’ “owner,” Philemon, a fellow Christian and host of a house church. So Paul writes to Philemon and his church, asking them to receive Onesimus not as a slave but as a beloved brother, and to forgive any wrongs he may have committed.

What’s painfully absent from this letter is a first-century version of Martin Luther King Jr.’s _I Have a Dream_ speech. Many of us want Paul to seize the moment, to raise his voice and write, in capital Greek letters, _“CHRISTIANS DO NOT OWN SLAVES.”_ But he doesn’t. Instead, Paul closes with a personal note: he hopes to visit soon and asks them to prepare a room. And that’s how the letter ends. We’re left with an apostle who likely relied on enslaved people to write his letters and wrote a letter _about_ a slave, and never explicitly asked for his freedom.

Hopefully, by this point, you are uncomfortable. I am.

And I’m just focusing on Paul, we could go on. For example, some believe Mark (of the Gospel of Mark) may have been a slave or servant of Peter. This idea comes from one of our earliest sources, Papias, who identifies Mark as Peter’s _hermēneutēs_ (interpreter or translator).

But what does it mean that many parts of the New Testament may have been physically written by enslaved people? That the words we hold as sacred, words that have brought us hope, dignity, and freedom, were shaped, at least in form, by those who had none?

Here’s how I’ve begun to cope with it.

## 1\. What Enslaved Means

Many Christians who preach on the book of Philemon often fall into the trap of downplaying Roman slavery, suggesting that it wasn’t _that_ bad. The problem is that it’s simply not true. While Roman slavery was different from the chattel slavery of the Atlantic slave trade in the United States, it was still a system marked by brutality, exploitation, and dehumanization. To soften or sanitize that reality risks turning historical oppression into hagiography.

That said, it is important to understand that Roman slavery was _different_. First, it was not based on race. Enslavement in the Roman world was largely driven by class and circumstance: people were often enslaved due to debt or as a result of being captured in war. Second, some forms of Roman slavery allowed for the possibility of manumission; individuals could, in some cases, work their way out of bondage, particularly if they were enslaved due to debt. I admit I hesitate even writing this paragraph, because far too much harm has been done by romanticizing Roman slavery as somehow more “humane.” But clarity matters. And understanding those distinctions helps us engage the text more honestly and responsibly.

## 2\. Paul Does Do Something Radical

Why doesn’t Paul call for the end of slavery in his letter to Philemon? Is it because he benefits from it and is complicit in the system? Some commentators think so. Or is he, in fact, subtly dismantling slavery altogether? Others argue that’s exactly what he’s doing. But I think both views miss the mark. To the former, I respond: What about Paul’s bold claim in Galatians 3:28, “There is neither slave nor free”? And to the latter, I ask: if Paul’s intention in Philemon was to call for the abolition of slavery, why did no one seem to understand that for nearly two thousand years? To truly appreciate the depth of Paul’s letter to Philemon, I believe we need to consider two things.

First, we need to stop judging Paul by 21st-century ethical standards. I say that cautiously, not to excuse Paul’s relationship to slavery, but to acknowledge the historical reality. In the Greco-Roman world, _slavery was everywhere_; some estimates suggest that one in five people was enslaved. It wasn’t just tolerated, it was woven into the fabric of life. No one, as far as we know, called for its abolition, not because it wasn’t cruel, but because people couldn’t imagine a world without it. So when we expect Paul to denounce slavery outright, we’re asking him to do something virtually unheard of for centuries. We can and should still wish he had, but our critique should be grounded in historical context.

Second, I’d argue that Paul’s letter to Philemon offers something quite radical. While Paul doesn’t tell Philemon to free Onesimus (which again, would have been possible), _he tells him to receive him as a brother._ In doing so, he places Onesimus and Philemon not only on equal footing, but in the same family. Even more, Paul refers to Onesimus as his _son_ and urges Philemon to treat him as he would treat Paul himself. This isn’t just social reform, it’s a call to embody the Kingdom of God, _where all are family_. And I cannot overstate this: in all my reading on the Roman world_, nothing compares to the dignity Paul extends to Onesimus. The use of familial language is truly one of one_ (compare [Pliny the Younger’s letter to Sabinianus concerning a slave](https://ministrytoday.org/2019/Philemon/PDF/Lesson%205%20-%20Pliny%20to%20Sabiniianus%20about%20Freeman.pdf), roughly dated to the same time as Philemon)_._

Furthermore, Paul takes the most dehumanizing labels of his world and transforms them into marks of Christian identity. For example, he was no stranger to the Roman prison system, which was brutal, _yet he never called for its abolition_. Instead, he reclaims the language: a prisoner for Christ Jesus (Eph 3:1). The same is true of slavery. A term loaded with class stigma, Paul adopts freely to describe his devotion: a slave of Christ Jesus (Rom 1:1).

So when we expect Paul to explicitly condemn slavery in Philemon, we risk missing what may be even more radical: a call to fully receive a slave as a beloved family member, as an equal in Christ.

Does Paul call for the abolition of slavery? No, not directly. But that doesn’t mean he did nothing. While he likely relied on enslaved scribes to pen his letters, he personally embraced the slave Onesimus as a spiritual son. We ought to hold these tensions together.

## 3\. Let Us Not Point the Finger

As I write this, I’m sitting at my MacBook, wearing an athletic t-shirt, denim jeans, and socks. And the chances that each of these items has come into contact with slave labor somewhere in the supply chain? Probably close to 99%. The truth is, much of what we consume, fast fashion, electronics, and our same-day Amazon deliveries, comes at the cost of exploited labor, often involving modern-day slavery. The statistics are staggering: from child labor to sex trafficking, not just in distant places like Asia or Africa, but right here in the United States. And even though many of us know this, we keep consuming, the cheaper the better. The same is true for many of our Christian resources, books, church merchandise, and even Bibles, often produced in places that undeniably rely on exploitative, slave-like labor.

While many of us are rightly concerned with how Christianity intersected with slavery 2,000 years ago, perhaps we should be even more troubled by how it remains entangled with slavery today.
