Nijay K. Gupta, _The Affections of Christ Jesus: Love at the Heart of Paul’s Theology_ (Grand Rapids, MI: Eerdmans, 2025) Pp. xvii + 241. Paper 39.99

In Pauline scholarship, there is an unspoken rule that discussions of emotions, such as love or joy, are best left to the pulpit, while soteriology, justification, and apocalypticism remain the domain of exegetes. In most monographs or commentaries on Paul’s theology, emotions are typically relegated to a footnote or a brief appendix under the category of “ethics.” However, Nijay K. Gupta challenges this norm in his latest work, _**[The Affections of Christ Jesus: Love at the Heart of Paul’s Theology](https://www.amazon.com/Affections-Christ-Jesus-Heart-Theology/dp/0802877168)**_. In this relatively concise study, Gupta argues that “love belongs in the conversation about the center of Paul’s theology” (11).

### **Summary**

The book is broadly divided into two main sections. The first section (chs. 1–4) provides an overview of different perspectives and terminologies on love from Jewish traditions, Greco-Roman culture, and the Jesus tradition. The second section (chs. 5-11) shifts to Paul’s relationship to love. Gupta first argues that love in Paul’s writings is not merely an ethical principle; rather, it appears in some of Paul’s most theologically dense sections (e.g., Rom. 5–8). He then examines the various ways Paul speaks about love, ultimately challenging the idea that _agapē_ and its cognates are his only terms for love. In the following chapter, Gupta analyzes Romans 5:1–11 and 8:31–39, concluding that all of Paul’s theology is rooted in “deep, unreserved love” (124). For Gupta, Paul can discuss justification only because it is grounded in the righteousness of God—a righteousness that itself flows from God’s love. Next, Gupta explores the role of love in Paul’s religious framework. He addresses the apparent absence of the _Shema_ command in Paul’s writings, particularly in relation to devotion to God. Gupta argues that Paul expresses this Deuteronomic devotion and love for God in various ways, primarily through the concept of faith (_pistis_). For Gupta, faith and love in Paul’s vocabulary function as a hendiadys, encapsulating “the sum of Christian religion, loyal love, singular devotion, and passionate trust” (148). The next two chapters explore love within Pauline Christian communities and Paul’s perspective on loving outsiders. The penultimate chapter functions as a brief commentary on love in Ephesians. While the authorship of Ephesians is debated, Gupta, who supports Pauline authorship, acknowledges that regardless of one’s stance, the letter “functions as a kind of greatest hits of his apostolic proclamation” (183). The book concludes with a summary of Gupta’s six key theses.

### Review

Gupta’s work serves as a valuable and necessary corrective in Pauline theology. First, as he rightly highlights, love is not just a distinctive feature of Paul and the Christ movement—_it is arguably one of the key distinctions between early Christianity_ and its surrounding religious landscape. While elements of Greco-Roman religion overlapped with the Christ movement, the radical emphasis on both the love of God and love for others set it apart. Gupta effectively demonstrates just how audacious the concept of God’s love would have been in the first century. Second, he convincingly argues that love has been sidelined for far too long in Pauline studies. Not only does love appear in every single Pauline letter, but it functions as more than just an ethical principle. Gupta successfully brings Paul’s theology of love out of the margins, positioning it as possibly central to understanding Paul’s worldview.

Despite its strengths, I have two main critiques of this work. First, like many attempts to correct prevailing trends in New Testament scholarship, it overcorrects. For Gupta, love permeates Paul’s theology in every aspect, but as a reader, I sometimes struggled to follow him to those conclusions. For example, Gupta identifies the Holy Spirit as God’s “_loving_ presence,” arguing that “where the Spirit of the Lord is, there is _love_” (_emphasis original_, 125, 127). While Paul does explicitly connect the Spirit with love (e.g., Rom. 5:5), reducing the Spirit’s role in Paul’s letters primarily to love feels like an overreach, especially given how frequently Paul links the Spirit to other theological concepts (e.g., Rom. 8:6). This is a common challenge for scholars attempting to define the “heart” of Paul’s theology—such efforts can sometimes stretch the evidence too far. Second, and more significantly, one would expect a book on the centrality of Paul’s theology to engage deeply with Paul himself. Yet, in Gupta’s 218-page work, just over 80 pages focus on the undisputed Pauline letters. Far too much space is dedicated to theoretical discussions of love and its conceptual background in the Hebrew Bible and the Greco-Roman world. This creates a noticeable gap between the book’s central thesis and its actual method and argumentation. While Gupta is right that love in Paul deserves greater scholarly attention, I would argue that it also needed more attention within this book itself.

Nonetheless, I have read a great deal of Gupta’s scholarship (which is no small task, given his prolific work!), and I always find his research engaging and insightful. This book will be a valuable resource for pastors and students of Pauline studies, helping to address Tina Turner’s iconic theological question: “What’s love got to do with \[Paul\]?”
