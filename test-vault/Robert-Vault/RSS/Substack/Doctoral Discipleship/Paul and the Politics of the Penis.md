It’s Society of Biblical Literature (SBL) meeting week! (And Evangelical Theological Society meeting, shout out to my ETS peeps!) This means in a few days, thousands of highbrow biblical scholars will descend upon Boston and argue about Paul’s understanding of Abraham’s penis. In light of SBL’s annual meeting, I thought it would be appropriate to invite my readers into the latest trends in biblical research (some might say “cutting edge”). One _Schule_ of thought that has gained traction in the last 15-20 years is the Paul within Judaism (PwJ) camp (similar to but separate from the New Perspective). The PwJ scholars affirm the PwJ Creed, which states that Paul was not a Christian. He had no gripes with Judaism. Nor did he think Judaism was legalist or ethnocentric. In other words, _Paul was not trying to start a new religion because there was nothing wrong with his current one._ Contra E.P. Sanders’ famous but weak conclusion, “What Paul finds wrong in Judaism: it is not Christianity.”

But is this reading of Paul correct? Below is a breif introduction to the major claims of PwJ scholarship/readings, along with my thoughts on what I like and dislike.

# The Gentiles and their Genitalia

For PwJers, Paul was (and is) not a Christian, though Christian readings of Paul have dominated biblical scholarship. Since the term “Christian” is not really found in the New Testament (Acts 11:26, 26:28, 1 Peter 4:16), it is not only unlikely that Paul was a Southern Baptist in his lifetime, but altogether impossible. Therefore, PwJers argue that if Paul did not convert (whatever that means) to Christianity, he must have stayed a practicing Jew. And not only did he remain a practicing Jew, _but had no qualms with Judaism_.

But doesn’t Paul say some pretty nasty things about Judaism? Yes, he sure does. Paul says that the “law brings wrath” (Rom 10:4) and any and everyone who relies on the Law is “under a curse” (Gal 3:10), or that “neither circumcision nor uncircumcision counts for _anything_” (Gal 5:6, _emphasis_ Paul’s). Or even “Christ is the end of the law” (Rom 10:4). And much, much more…

PwJers point out that Paul’s harsh statements about the Torah/Law and circumcision _are communicated to Gentiles_. Paul does not think these things are bad for a Jew, but they're bad for _me_! Paul is not only okay with Jews still getting circumcised; _he expects_ them to. Additionally, the Torah/Law and circumcision are still reserved for Jews, and if a Gentile partakes in such practices, it’s not only useless but damaging.

But why does it matter to Paul if a gentile wants to cut their penis?

Because the Law is not meant for Gentiles. But thanks be to God, Gentiles can still be included via the Messiah Jesus. Thus, Paul’s frustration with Gentiles cutting their penis is because the Messiah Jesus died and was raised, not for Gentiles to become Jews, that would be impossible (according to Paul), but for Gentiles to now be a part of God’s family.

# Paul’s Exegesis of Abraham’s Appendage (A PwJ Reading)

In Paul’s most aggressive and frustrated letter, Galatians_,_ he provides an “allegorical” (_allēgoroumena_) interpretation of Genesis 21, focusing on Abraham, Sarah, and Hagar. Paul starts off by mocking the Gentiles, “Oh, you really want the Law? You want to be from the seed of Abraham via circumcision?” (Gal 4:21, _my paraphrase_). However, Paul reminds them that Abraham had _two circumcised_ sons: Isaac, the heir, who was circumcised as prescribed on the eighth day (Gen 17:12-14), while Ishmael, the son of a slave, was circumcised when he was thirteen (_no bueno_).

Thus, Paul shouts, “Drive out the slave and her child; for the child of the slave will not share the inheritance with the child of the free woman” (Gal 4:30). Many within the PwJ believe that Paul, like some Jews, understood _any_ circumcision past the eighth day as not only not good but absolutely forbidden. Therefore, Paul, in this allegory of Genesis, says any Gentile who circumcises themselves (e.g., past the eighth day) shares in the inheritance of Ishmael and not Isaac; instead, Gentiles are sons via the spirit not the flesh (Gal 4:29). Put differently, Gentiles don’t circumcise yourself if you want to share in the true inheritance of Abraham’s penis (see Thiessen’s excellent walk through, _A Jewish Paul,_ pp. 94-99).

According to the PwJ camp, Paul’s problem here (and the rest of his letters) is not Judaism; _it’s Gentiles’ appropriation of Judaism onto themselves inappropriately._

# What PwJ Gets Right and Wrong

The PwJ camp is stacked with talented scholars, including Paula Fredriksen, Matthew Novenson, Matthew Thiessen (no relation to Relient K), Mark Nanos, and Saul of Tarsus, among others. Fundamentally, what the PwJ camp gets right (in my opinion) is that Paul’s theology (i.e., messianism, eschatology, soteriology, and other ologies) is undeniably Jewish or at least is derived from and explained out of a Jewish framework. Paul never left Judaism, at least his understanding of it. For example, Paul states in 2 Cor 11:24 that “Five times I have received from the Jews the forty lashes minus one.” A standard punishment by synagogue leaders, implying that Paul was still under their authority, although considered perhaps reckless or deviant by the mainstream of Judaism. Or as E.P. Sanders put it, “punishment implies inclusion.” Additionally, Paul expects his Gentile audience to become monothestic-messiah-believing-non-idol-worshipping converts. Thus, Paul expects others to conform to his version of Judaism. I think these, and many other elements of PwJ, are helpful. The centuries of readings of Paul as a fundamentalist Baptist preacher have probably done irreparable damage to our understanding of Paul and his _religo_ in the 1st century, but the PwJ might be our biggest help in a balanced portrayal of Paul.

But PWJ makes me worried (and it’s not because I am Protestant) because I am skeptical of scholarly pendulums that swing so fast and so quickly that they domesticate their version of Paul. The problem with NT academic research is that it is simply not enough to advocate for a nuanced version of Paul; he must be an original entrepreneurial Christian or a Torah-observant Jew who wouldn’t step foot in a church, but I digress.

The main hickups, for me, with PwJ are their inability to clearly explain or admit just how harsh Paul’s words are concerning aspects of Judaism. Such as the Law died (Gal 2:19-21), his former life in whatever _Ioudaismō_ means (Gal 1:14), or that he had to become a Jew (1 Cor 9:19), etc. While scholars such as Novenson, Fredriksen, and Thiessen all have plausible readings, it seems to me they are grasping at straws. Frankly, I am not convinced whether someone can pull a Pauline-like statement from the Rabbinic corpus or a minor clause in Second Temple literature. The point isn’t that if you go looking, you can find a Jew here or there speaking similar to Paul’s language; the point is that you have to go looking for it at all.

Second, I think fundamentally, the PwJ camp hasn’t clearly worked out its relationship to Paul and circumcision. Some PwJers think that Paul doesn’t want Gentiles to be circumcised because it’s past the eighth day (Jubiliees 15), or that it is an ethnic marker and therefore can only be done by Jews. The problem is that, of course, Paul never says these things, and there is ample evidence from the Hebrew Bible to the contrary. People could, if circumstances permitted, get circumcised past the eighth day (Josh 5), and non-Jews partook in the practice as well while remaining ethnically distinct (Ex 12:48). The point being, I haven’t read a PwJ’s account, and I have read a lot, which makes sense to me why Paul is so enraged at such a practice.

# PwJ, WWJD, and SBL

Oh, how far Paul has come, dragged from the grip of Luther back into the pages of Leviticus. The PwJ movement is the latest scholarly attempt to untangle and reinterpret the enigmatic, often bewildering Jew who, willingly or not, laid the groundwork for the Christian tradition. So let me offer my own two denarii.

I find it difficult to imagine that a Jew who proclaimed Jesus as the resurrected Messiah, who expected his imminent return, and who preached this message at every opportunity, while writing letters not to synagogues but to a unique mix of Jewish and mostly Gentile gatherings; while collecting funds for the poor across the Mediterranean world; while crafting theological arguments that reinterpret Israel’s Scriptures as fulfilled in Jesus; and while making startling claims about the Law and his former life in Judaism, can still be described as “within” Judaism in any straightforward sense.

PwJers like to say that Paul _did_ change, but only in the way a Catholic might become Protestant or vice versa. But that analogy is far too mild. Paul’s transformation was more akin to a Presbyterian becoming a missionary overnight, or a long-standing Catholic suddenly reading Scripture daily.

It simply wasn’t something one did.
