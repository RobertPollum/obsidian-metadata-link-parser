I’ve moved more times than I would have liked to. Eleven apartments, four cities, two states in eleven years. With every move, I shed belongings, and my possessions shrink just a tad. But one thing I refuse to pare down is my book collection. Each book marks a chapter of my life. I remember who I was when I bought it, what I underlined, what struck me. Together, they chart my intellectual and spiritual journey. Sometimes I stumble across old favorites, _Crazy Love_, _Radical_, or _Blue Like Jazz_. I devoured those books, scrawling exclamations in the margins, highlighting on every page. These books marked the beginning of my spiritual life, when my faith became my own.

As I pursued greater theological depth, I turned to Tim Keller, John Piper, and John Mark Comer. Whenever I come across my copy of _The Reason for God_, I’m reminded of my freshman year in college, soaking in every page while riding the bus to my lifeguarding gig. By the time I reached Wayne Grudem’s _Systematic Theology_, I genuinely believed I had uncovered everything there was to know. I felt confident. I _knew_ the Scriptures, grasped key doctrines, and could reference Latin phrases like _Imago Dei_ and _Protoevangelium_. At that point in my life, it truly felt as if I had arrived. I had developed a fluency in speaking about God, the Bible, and the many theological debates surrounding them. But then I went to seminary…

In one of life’s great ironies, I went on to pursue seminary and doctoral studies to deepen my understanding of the Bible, only to come away feeling like I know less than ever. The sheer complexity of biblical scholarship is staggering. Scholars have spent decades debating a single case of a Greek genitive, and entire dissertations, hundreds of pages long, have been written on just half a sentence in Romans (e.g., [Preston Sprinkle](https://www.amazon.com/Law-Life-Interpretation-Wissenschaftliche-Untersuchungen/dp/3161495314)). By the time I completed all the coursework, I had learned so much that returning to resources like _Blue Like Jazz_, _Desiring God_, or even sitting through a sermon can feel difficult. To be clear: This isn’t to suggest that I’ve outgrown those resources or consider myself too smart for them. Rather, I’ve been exposed to so much information that it’s hard to hear broad claims about God or the Bible without immediately thinking of the counterarguments and differing perspectives. _It’s exhausting_.

This post is for those of us who feel that familiar tension deep in our gut, the longing for a strong, grounded Christian faith that fully embraces, rather than ignores, the wealth of information and data we now have about the Bible and Christianity. As I write, I do so as a fellow patient, not as a doctor. I’m still very much in the middle of it. But I want to explore the question: What can someone do when they’ve learned so much that it becomes difficult to approach God with a devotional and submissive heart? And how can one continue to seek Him on an academic level without losing the wonder, reverence, and intimacy of faith?

Below are my ideas and practices, but I’d love to hear yours.

## **The Critical and Devotional Mind(s)**

> _“Nothing is quite as deceitful as an Evangelical scholarly mind that thinks it is especially close to God because of its scholarship rather than because of Jesus.” - Don Carson_

When I first began my doctoral studies, I was a master of compartmentalization. I could spend hours in intense seminars on Greco-Roman history, Philo’s exegesis, or adoption practices in the Roman Empire. Then, the next morning, I’d open my Bible for devotions, switch off the academic side, and engage the text with a simple Observation–Interpretation–Application method. At first, it worked. But as my studies deepened, my reading widened, and the topics grew more obscure, the divide became harder to maintain. It began to feel like I was living a double life with Scripture, a devoted believer in the morning, a critical scholar by day and night. I was, in effect, a terrible double agent. And I knew something had to change. I needed a spiritual life that could hold both minds, critical and devotional, together. Easier said than done.

For example, I once spent an entire semester studying a single Greek accent mark in Philemon 9, where Paul writes, “yet for love’s sake I prefer to appeal to you. I, Paul, a _presbytēs_ and now a prisoner also for Christ Jesus.” Scholars debate whether Paul is calling himself an old man or an ambassador; the only difference lies in the accent (the correct answer is the former). But week after week, as I read articles, translated obscure Greco-Roman texts, and combed through ancient letters, I often wondered, “Does this even matter?” During one of our meetings, sensing my fatigue, my professor leaned back, gazed at the ceiling, and said, “If Paul did call himself an old man, then Philemon gives us a new view of aging in the Christian life. His authority didn’t come from age, but from love. In the Christian world, leadership isn’t marked by seniority, but by love.” My professor (in a secular institution, mind you) showed me the art of blending the critical mind and devotional mind.

While my experience with the Greek accent mark is quite minimal in terms of impact, questions like the existence of Q, the influence of Second Temple Judaism on Paul’s theology, or the Canaanite roots of Israel’s practices aren’t just academic; they have real implications for how we understand God, salvation, and Scripture. And there are thoughtful answers out there. But the real challenge isn’t just knowing about these issues, it’s learning to carry them from theory into practice, like being able to connect Greek accents to Christ-like love. Bridging academic study with spiritual formation takes intentional effort, and few take the time to do it. Yet doing so can profoundly shape a more integrated, mature faith.

## **Recapturing the Wonder**

> _“Wonder can’t be packaged, and it can’t be worked up. It requires some sense of being there and some sense of engagement.” - Eugene Peterson_

Each year, I try to follow the Robert Murray M’Cheyne Bible reading plan. And every year, I fall short, some years extremely short. The plan calls for four chapters a day, arguably more Scripture than one can meaningfully absorb at once. Before I can dive into commentaries on Ezra, the plan has already jumped to Acts. By the time I consider cracking open Craig Keener’s billion-page commentary, we’ve already moved on to Romans. I simply can’t keep up from an academic standpoint.

What I’ve realized, though, is that many of us assume the way to resolve confusion in Scripture is with more information. But that is almost never the answer. Sometimes what we need isn’t more depth, it’s more breadth. More reading, less mastering. As [Erik Lundeen](https://www.thegospelcoalition.org/article/keep-reading-bible-understand/) puts it, “Evangelical culture rightly values Bible _study_, but I suspect we need to value Bible reading more. We should allow proper space for uninterrupted, extensive _reading._”

While I’ve argued for bringing critical thinking into our devotional lives, we also need to make room for wonder. We should engage Scripture thoughtfully, but we must also accept that we’ll never master it. Before we search online for why King Cyrus is called a Messiah or debate the longer ending of Mark, let’s pause and be amazed: _we don’t know everything_. Let’s sit in awe of the vast history through which God has worked. No resource will ever be enough; there will always be more to learn, more sides to the debate, and another book by Eerdmans to read. And that’s okay, and it’s good.

## **Seminaries and the Suffering**

> _“You can read theology ten hours a day for forty years and not know God as beautiful and all-satisfying —as the highest treasure of your life. Who cares about knowing God the way the devil knows God? He hates everybody. His knowledge of God helps him hate people.” - John Piper_

There’s a well-known stereotype about someone fresh out of seminary, the kind of person whose favorite phrase quickly becomes, “Well, actually…” Churches tend to keep their distance, and few people seek them out for guidance. It’s not that they lack biblical knowledge; they can explain the crucifixion in its Roman context in great detail, but they often lack the ability to connect that knowledge to the realities of human suffering. They can outline amillennial theology with precision, yet struggle to offer real comfort to someone facing death or grieving a profound loss. This is where the old saying proves true: “Nobody cares how much you know until they know how much you care.”

I once gave a university lecture on the New Perspective on Paul. I walked students through the work of Krister Stendahl and Jimmy Dunn, unpacking the precise meaning of “works of the law.” I was in my element, and I loved every minute of it. That evening, my wife and I visited friends who had just lost their child to stillbirth. For two hours, we sat with them in their grief, crying, listening, and holding space for their heartbreak. As they poured out their raw questions, their theology of suffering, and their stubborn hope in the midst of devastation, it struck me with painful clarity: they knew God more deeply than I did. I knew the historical and Jewish background of grace. They _lived_ it.

Over the past five years, nothing has shaped my perspective or pierced my pride more than walking alongside the least, the lost, and the suffering. Academic study can sometimes tempt me to keep God at a safe, intellectual distance, to treat Him as a concept to analyze rather than a presence to trust. But suffering has a way of stripping away that illusion. God is not first an idea to master, but a refuge to hold onto. And in one of life’s quiet paradoxes, I’ve often learned the most about God, the Bible, and theology from those who’ve never attended a lecture, never read a commentary, just clung to Him with everything they had.

## **Find Your Mike Bird**

> _Theology is speaking about God while in the very presence of God. We are intimately engaged with the subject of our study. - Mike Bird_

In the academic world, we all have our people, the voices we trust and return to. For me I devour everything Mike Bird writes. He’s sharp, incredibly productive, and genuinely funny. But beyond that, I follow a group of scholars who not only know more than I do, but who also seem to love God more deeply. And that’s what draws me to them. My go-to group includes [Simon Gathercole](https://www.divinity.cam.ac.uk/directory/simon-gathercole), [Joey Dodson](https://denverseminary.edu/directory/dodson-phd-joseph-r/), [Mike Bird,](https://www.ridley.edu.au/team/mike-bird/) [Don Carson](https://www.tiu.edu/divinity/faculty/d-a-carson/), and [Preston Sprinkle](https://www.prestonsprinkle.com/about). I keep up with their academic work, but I also pay attention to how they’re serving the local church. What stands out is how much effort they put into making their scholarship matter for everyday Christian life.

Each of these steps is meant to remind us that we don’t know everything, and that perhaps the easiest trap to fall into is learning too much while neglecting our spiritual life. It’s all too common to use biblical knowledge and academic study as a way to control or master what was never meant to be mastered. While I fully support the pursuit of deeper understanding, knowledge alone won’t strengthen our relationship with the living God or secure our identity. Only God, through His Spirit, can do that.

Lord, I believe, help my unbelief.
