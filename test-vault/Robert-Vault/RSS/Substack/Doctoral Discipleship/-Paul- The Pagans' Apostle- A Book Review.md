**Paula Fredriksen,** _**[Paul: The Pagans’ Apostle](https://www.amazon.com/Paul-Pagans-Apostle-Paula-Fredriksen/dp/0300225881)**_ **Hardcover (New Haven, CT: Yale University Press, 2017) Pp. xii + 319. Paper $39.99 ISBN: 0300225881.**

What can we say definitively about Paul the Apostle? A basic biography usually goes something like this: Paul was a Jewish Pharisee (_disputed_), who also happened to be a tentmaker (_somewhat disputed_), turned Christian missionary (_mega disputed_), playing a crucial role in spreading Christianity among Gentiles (_heavily disputed_), and writing several (7? 8? 9?) New Testament letters that are the foundations of Christian theology (_eternally debated_). In other words, figuring out who Paul really was has been providing tenure for quite some time. However, for Paula Fredriksen, one thing is absolutely certain—Paul, often called the inventor of Christianity, was not, in fact, a Christian.

#### Summary

Chapter 1 examines three key figures, God, the nations, and Israel, within the Hebrew Bible and Second Temple literature. Fredriksen uses this survey to highlight the eschatological hope that shaped Jewish thought in Paul’s time, particularly the expectation that God’s redemptive Kingdom was imminent (p. 9). As she traces prophetic traditions through the various Jewish exiles, Fredriksen contends that Israel came to understand its own future redemption as something that would also extend to _all_ the nations (p. 26). In other words, when God’s Kingdom arrives, it will bring salvation to the entire world.

Chapter 2 examines the religiously pluralistic culture of the Greco-Roman world and how Jews fit within it. Fredriksen makes two key arguments in this chapter. First, she challenges the notion that Jews and their God were uniquely distinct in Mediterranean society. While Jews believed their God was supreme (henotheism), they were otherwise much like everyone else (p. 44). Second, just as Jews could be found in pagan spaces, pagans could also be found in Jewish ones. Fredriksen highlights evidence of non-Jews participating in Jewish worship, both in the Temple and synagogues. She argues that when a pagan “converted” to worshiping Israel’s God, it was as if they underwent an ethnic transformation, which led to the linguistic category of “Judaizing.” This means that when Paul refers to the “Judaizers” (Gal 2:14), it was not Jews who Judaized—only non-Jews could.

In Chapter 3, Fredriksen challenges the idea that Jews engaged in missionary activity in antiquity. While she acknowledges that there is evidence of Jews welcoming _prosēlytoi_ (Gentile adherents), she argues that “receiving is one thing; actively soliciting them is another” (p. 73). In other words, while Gentiles may have been present in Jewish communities, they were not the targets of an organized Jewish mission; it was more or less an accident (p. 146). Fredriksen then addresses the question: if pagans were present in Jewish spaces, what was expected of them? She concludes that while _prosēlytoi_ functioned within the community, they remained Gentiles and were not expected to fully adopt Jewish ancestral customs. As she puts it, “_inclusion is not ‘conversion_’” (_emphasis original_, p. 75). In this framework, Gentiles were saved _as_ Gentiles; they did not undergo an eschatological transformation into Jews.

Fredriksen turns to Paul and the Law in Chapter 4, focusing specifically on its relationship to Gentiles. She argues that Paul’s stance on Gentile observance of the Law is paradoxical and somewhat improvised (p. 146). On the one hand, Gentiles must turn from their gods and worship Israel’s God in Jewish ways, yet because they are not part of Israel, they _cannot_ fully become Jewish. As she puts it, “\[t\]hey are not responsible to and for the Jewish Law” (e.g., circumcision). “And yet, Paul insists they are” (p. 117). Though they are united in Christ (Gal. 3:28), they remain distinct by nature (p. 114). But if this is the case, why does Paul speak so harshly about the Law? According to Fredriksen, his critique is not directed against the Law itself but rather against Gentile observance of the Law _apart from Christ_. Thus, Fredriksen concludes, “Paul rejects (some forms of) Juda_izing_, not (all forms of) Juda_ism_” (_emphasis original_, p. 130).

In the final chapter, Fredriksen examines Paul’s understanding of the coming Kingdom of God, arguing that to grasp Paul’s eschatology, one must first understand his Christology. She urges readers to set aside later Nicene formulations of Christology and instead approach Paul’s most frequently cited Christological passage, Phil 2:6–11, on its own terms. According to Fredriksen, this passage makes one thing clear: “Jesus is not ‘God’” (p. 138). She contends that whatever degree of divinity Jesus may have had in Paul’s thought, Philippians 2 presents him in contrast to the God of Israel. Rather than emphasizing Jesus’ divinity, the passage (and other Pauline passages) focuses on his messianic role as the “eschatological redeemer” (p. 140). Therefore, Paul’s mission to the Gentiles was not a shift away from Jewish eschatology but was, in fact, the very framework through which Israel’s divinely destined identity would be fulfilled or, in other words, Paul was not a Christian.

#### Review

Fredriksen’s work is incredibly valuable. In this book, she provides a broad audience with insight into the _Paul within Judaism_ (PwJ) framework. More importantly, by situating Paul within this perspective, she offers readers a remarkably coherent interpretation of Paul that avoids the anti-Semitic readings that, unfortunately, still persist today. However, I do have two critiques. First, one of Fredriksen’s arguments against Gentile circumcision is that Paul adhered to the requirement that circumcision must take place on the eighth day of a child’s life and, therefore, was of “_no value for the adult male gentile_” (_emphasis original_, p. 129). I think Abraham, Moses, and the entire nation of Israel (Josh 5) would have disagreed. Second, her treatment of Paul’s Christology is problematic. While I won’t dwell on her assertion that concerns about preexistence are strictly a post-Nicene development (though I am tempted to), my main issue is her reading of Philippians 2. She argues that the RSV mistranslates the passage, which renders it as “though he was in the form of \[high/exalted\] God” (Phil. 2:6). Instead, she insists that the Greek is clear and should be read as “\[a\] god” (p. 138). I think the RSV gets the phrase “_hos en morphē theou hyparchōn_”, correct. In short, her interpretation of Philippians 2 is flimsy. Nevertheless, the book is a perfect starting point for people to be introduced to the ever-illusive man that is Paul the (Pagans’) Apostle.
