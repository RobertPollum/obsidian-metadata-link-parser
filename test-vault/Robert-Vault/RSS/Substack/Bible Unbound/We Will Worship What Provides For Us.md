We will worship what provides for us. You may immediately think of work—the worship of which author Gordon Dahl cleverly calls a work-ethic—providing financially. Or you may think of activities bringing immense pleasure like affogato, sex, sky-diving, or seeing the sunrise over Mount Parnassus from a Delphi balcony. For all unfamiliar with Greek mythology out there, that was an Apollo joke.

Ultimately, our desires are oriented toward the things that provide because we can control those things. We become increasingly uneasy toward the things we can’t control because we aren’t sure they will provide sufficiently for us. Therefore, we make sure we have a good job, get enough pleasure from vacation, and say just the right combination of words in prayer so that we know we are in good hands. Until we don’t.

I think this is why the fifth commandment is the first with a promise. “Honor your father and your mother, that your days may be long in the land that the Lord your God is giving you.” (Ex. 20:12, Eph 6:2). Our parents are the first step we take into a world of uncertain provision. If that first step proves reliable, we ought to begin trusting them more and more; wading deeper into the waters of their provision by honoring them with our trust. Our parents act as a sandbox, letting us test what we ought to worship within the confines of what they provide. Ideally, of course, our parent’s provision points us up, toward God. Often, however, our parent’s provision fails us and we must seek safety elsewhere.

We may say, then, that the job of the Holy Spirit and the process of discipleship is to lead us back, though slow as a crawl, to an unyielding trust that God—our true parent—will provide perfectly.

This process of trust _begins_ with the total forgiveness of sins leading to an astonishing humility. But this trust often _grows_ through affliction, where we find that God can seem silent. In this valley, the darkness can be a shroud and voices of hope are muffled.

Therefore, as a brief aside, let me provide three directions for the Christian crawling through the valley of darkness:

#### **The Bible**

The Bible calls us back into a foolish hope that God will provide perfectly. In fact, hope grows in direct proportion to our belief that a good God is in charge of everything. Thankfully, the Bible testifies to this truth in a multiphonic symphony of stories, poems, and letters. So much so, actually, that some theologians have walked away from the Bible as a book about this singular truth: God will provide.

#### **The Church**

The Church is designed to lead us to God. Through podcasts, books, and fellowship, we ought to lean into stories that proclaim God’s miraculous provision. If you don’t feel as though you have a community of believers who can help instill this joy within you, might I suggest a short little book called _He Leadeth Me_ by Walter Ciscek? The title doesn’t suggest that this is a book about a priest who was wrongfully imprisoned in a Siberian death camp during World War II and not only miraculously survived (5 years in solitary with one gun to his head) but further found a way to lead daily mass in the camps, saturated in atheist propaganda, for 23 years…but that is what the book is about! It’s been a comfort to both my wife and I in a particularly difficult season of life.

#### **The Creation**

Finally, it’s strange how often Jesus points to creation in both parable and sermon to tell us that God will provide. Famously, He does this in the Sermon on the Mount. “Look at the birds of the air,” He says, “they neither sow nor reap nor gather into barns, and yet your heavenly Father feeds them. Are you not of more value than they?” (Matt 6:26). The emphasis here is on how God consistently, tenderly, and joyously provides for His creation. Will He not do so for you? Perhaps this truth would hit home all the more if you sat in a park and watched the birds collect their things, realizing each branch and worm is a gift from God. You do not need to check your phone, get back to work, or produce anything from this experience. God will take care of all that needs to get done, He promises.

Of course, performing any of these liturgies will likely not lead you back to a full assurance of God’s provision. Yet they will each help get you there. Even if only for a day. For if we worship what provides for us, then we ought to try and turn our attention to the ways God provides. Even through death, we know that God provided a way. Freed from anxiety and shame, we come to the throne knowing that God gave His son for our salvation.

This trust, I believe, is the key to a good life. By this trust we will be offering up our bodies as a living sacrifice. We therefore end where we ought to have begun, in the whimsical worship of the true God.
