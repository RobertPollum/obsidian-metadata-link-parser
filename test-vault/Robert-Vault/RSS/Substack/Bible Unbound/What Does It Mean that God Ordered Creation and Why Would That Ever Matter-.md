The 1981 box office smash success and culturally iconic film, _Time Bandits,_ ends when God intervenes. Throughout the film, chaos reigns supreme as the Devil tries to race a band of dwarves to steal a famous treasure hidden in another era. Like I said, culturally iconic. Needless to say, God appears before the credits and commands the dwarves to clean up their temporal mess. The entire scene takes place in a collapsing interim universe. God, or the Supreme Being as he’s called, orders the dwarves to clean up the columns, sweep away the dust, and put everything back where they found it.

This God is punctual, coordinated, and (literally) wears a grey suit and tie. He has a clipboard. While still benevolent, he’s clearly meant to contrast the raggedy dwarves, the homely child, and the malevolent devil. This God is — for all intents and purposes — ordered. But when Christians speak of the orderliness of God, do we mean this sort of cosmic vision of maturity who orders us to sweep the house and repair the furniture?

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!zMmc!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F3f5a548d-fec9-4ec5-a293-39fddc13af9d_1126x399.webp)

Compare this to Wendell Berry’s novel Jayber Crow. The book is the fictional biography of a man who takes up the position of barber in the novel’s town of Port Williams. Throughout the book, defining order and chaos becomes a major theme. After all, the novel opens with Jayber looking out the window of his barber shop, taking in the quiet of the town. The town is quiet, of course, because all of the children are in school. Jayber is waiting. He waits daily for the, as he describes it, chaotic cacophony of joyful school children being breathed out by the school. This level of joy is the kind of chaotic order that Jayber longs for every day. In fact, this kind of order puts him face-to-face with Love.

This essay seeks to work out a Biblically theological vision of Order so that we rightly understand God and His orderliness. Ought we to picture the transcendent God (the Supreme Being) who rightfully cleans up the house after we screw up, structuring our lives; or do we mean to say that the order of God is more like a cacophony of rowdy school children bringing us joy? The vision we choose to behold will alter our perception of how the world operates. Therefore, our mental picture of God’s orderliness will affect how we act toward those around us.

### **Why Would This Ever Matter?**

In fact, let me illustrate the importance of this theological vision with an anecdote:

My wife and I are in the habit of taking a daily walk around our neighborhood in Milwaukee. One day on our stroll, we noticed someone’s $90,000 Mercedes parked on the street, with the hatch wide open, and several items of significant value sitting there for anyone who wanted. Now, I don’t know if you live in a city — particularly a city with an incredibly high auto and theft rate — but you probably don’t want to do this. It’s much cheaper to take 3 seconds, close your hatch, and come back out later than to replace your car. We waited for several minutes before seeing if they would return.

When they didn’t return, we figured the neighborly thing to do was to notify them. I knocked on the door. An older man answered. When I told him his conundrum, he was…furious? At me. He was distraught that I rushed him. He was _getting it all out of the car!_ He said with a whip of his tongue. He guffawed to himself as if to say _The nerve of some people!_

Why would a person respond this way? Is it out of embarrassment? Maybe overstimulation. Yes, and also, at the heart of it all, this man cannot fathom an orderly world. He relates to the world first and foremost as though it were disordered — chaotic, evil, even out to get him.

I say this, mind you, without an ounce of disrespect to this man. He is an archetype of us all. Wouldn’t you respond the same way if you were caught off guard, rushed, and stressed from unloading the car? Add in the embarrassment and fear of having your car stolen, and you get this response. Regardless, it is a response that emerges from our distrust in God’s ordering of our lives.

Instead, in Genesis, God invites us to relate to the world primarily as though it were ordered by God. Even after the Fall, the climactic verse of Genesis reads, “you meant evil against me, but God meant it for good” (Gen. 50:20) as a way to highlight how God is in the business of ordering His creation toward His goodness. From there, this theme of God turning things around for good becomes central to the biblical canon (c.f., Rom. 8:28).

But relating to the world this way is a difficult task. Therefore, we must break down biblical orderliness into smaller bits so that we can grasp how to relate to the world through order. Practically, relating to the world through order means [relating to the world through beauty](https://bibleunbound.substack.com/p/to-make-something-beautiful?r=1f0vew). Not because order is subservient to beauty. Precisely the opposite. Beauty serves to order rightly.

Furthermore, as Hans Urs von Balthasar makes clear, beauty draws us up into God’s glory. In fact, for Balthasar, beauty is the manifestation of God’s glory (Balthasar, The Glory of the Lord, vol. VII 12). Therefore, to envision the world as preeminently beautiful would order our lives and draw us into God’s glory.

### **Three Ways to Cultivate Beauty**

The pinnacle of the Goodness theme is the Christ-event. All other events preceding it point toward Him. All events succeeding it point back to Him. Therefore, we cultivate beauty first and foremost through Christ, united to the triune Godhead. As God works in us, the natural disposition of the Christian is pulled toward beauty, toward goodness, toward order.

Beyond our sanctified imagination being pulled toward beauty, we may conclude with Paul that we ought to strive to focus on beautiful things. In Philippians, Paul writes, “brothers and sisters, whatever is true, whatever is honorable, whatever is just, whatever is pure, whatever is pleasing, whatever is commendable, if there is any excellence and if there is anything worthy of praise, think about these things.” (Phil 4:8, NRSV). Paul here exhorts the Philippians to try to focus on what is beautiful and ordered in the world.

Finally, we can do beautiful things. The real benefits of spiritual exercises (prayer, giving, serving, etc.) are that they focus us on the Gospel. Spiritual disciplines remind us of what is beautiful. These actions rightly order our lives and call us into God’s glory.

### **So What Does it Mean That God is Orderly?**

We can now properly turn back to our original question: when biblical scholars speak of God ordering things, what does this mean? It means that God makes things beautiful.

Therefore, our two images of the Supreme Being at the beginning are not contradictory, but trying to emphasize two different ways in which God beautifies the lives of His people. On the one hand, He takes our messes and, with prompt execution, begins to sweep up. He often invites us in on this, too! But God also brings order to our lives by drawing us toward what is joyful, exciting, and freeing. For a God who enacts justice by sacrificing Himself for humanity, these two ways of ordering collide.

By thinking this way, the Christian’s life is properly immersed in grace. We are given peace beyond understanding and meaning amid chaos; we are given courage in the face of death. In fact, we are given the presence of God Himself. That is very good news.
