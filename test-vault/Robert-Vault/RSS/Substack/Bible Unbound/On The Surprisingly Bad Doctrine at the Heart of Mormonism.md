A recent conversation between Dr. Gavin Ortlund and Mormon apologist Jacob Hansen has me thinking about the correlation between belief and salvation. Their positions met in a surprising place that for me has made sense of a lot of alternative LDS beliefs and practices. That is, the doctrine of Creation. I encourage you to listen to the dialogical debate in full below:

My proposal is that the LDS doctrine of creation can lead to injustices and a distortion of Christian doctrine. This isn’t meant to be uncharitable in any way, nor straw-man the Mormon position. But I am not an LDS expert. Therefore, this article is not research-based. I merely want to defend the trinitarian view of Christianity as the most just option. Therefore, this article aims to clarify why the Latter Day Saints doctrine on Creation leads to an injustice on the created order—and ultimately, death.

But, let us start _in the beginning_ and walk through the LDS cosmology.

Imagine a beginning that was not created _ex-nihilo_ (out of nothing). This is the starting point of the Mormon doctrine on Creation. This starting point becomes a great one because the _ex-nihilo_ doctrine of creation is often taken for granted and thus not often deeply considered. Therefore, it does not necessarily follow that an _ex-materia_ (out of something) doctrine is unjust. However, when we take this idea out for a test-drive, we find ourselves in a world of wild and waste. Let’s explore.

Obviously, if you remove an ex-nihilo creation, you need to have a system that explains how everything came into existence. Hansen provides this explanation. I understood it this way: the material world of reality is always coming into existence via a (higher) spiritual plane of reality.

The implications of this _ex-materia_ creation is a hallmark of Mormon doctrine: everything is ontologically the _same_ as God—including (if not especially) you and I. Woohoo, I always knew I was more than just a meatbag. This is because everything is coming from the spiritual-material of God Himself.

However, this alone cannot account for sin (and why everything is so bad). Therefore, the LDS created a spectrum of God-ness. If you are fully unenlightened, then you are barely God at all. This spectrum gradates through stages of enlightenment. Until, of course, you share in the full glory of God Himself (the most enlightened being). A Christian way to put this would be: you become the fullness of God.

At this point, I am reminded of the TV show _Suits_. In the show, everyone is doing their best to become name partner of a law-firm. In this way, they will share in the glory of the company. That’s how I would understand the Mormon doctrine of exaltation: one day, if you are faithful enough, you too can become like the owner of the company, God, reigning in your own dominion. Until then, you’re entrusted with sovereignty over a cubicle of creation.

If this were true, you would come to expect a religion with a complex system of law-keeping. As your Mormon life continues you find, for instance, you are restricted from drinking caffeine. In this way, the religion becomes a guide to self-improvement.

Finally, imagine that you, as a Mormon, die. You get to Heaven and suddenly realize that you actually are a dirt-creature merely made in God’s image. Everything you worked so hard for in your life is absolutely meaningless. Every good deed you ever did, every cup of coffee you passed on, was all for nothing because God doesn’t care about the dirty rags that you call good deeds (Is. 64:6). You are ontologically different from God. I think you would go absolutely ballistic! You would try to overthrow God at every chance you got. Therefore, the _just_ thing for God to do is contain the death you so desperately wish to sow into His good (and safe) Kingdom.

The reason the Mormon doctrine of creation is unjust is because it’s logical conclusion is, what I believe smart people call, egotistic altruism. Or, as I like to say, selfish selflessness (cue: [](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QPXsYOPex4s)_[The Good Place](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QPXsYOPex4s)_). You only ever do good things because you think it will get you more glory and honor (God’s rightful glory and honor, at that). Therefore, I say unjust because it ultimately replaces God’s glory with self-glory.

Therefore, the Mormon worldview does not represent Christian Love. Love in the Bible is selflessness reflective of God’s own covenantal (triune) nature (c.f., Jn 15:13). Thus, you will never have known God who is Love (1 Jn 4:8) and will reject Him when you meet Him.

If you need evidence of this conclusion, I encourage you to listen to the dialogical debate linked above. North of the 30-minute mark, hear how often Hansen discusses the life of a human as a path toward self-improvement. The telos of Mormonism is not the triune God, but the best version of ourselves, gods.

It seems to me that the Mormon doctrine of creation was developed out of a denial of the Trinity as it is classically understood. _Ex-materia_ creation does not logically follow from the Genesis account. It also doesn’t follow from any other part of the Bible that echoes the pattern of creation. Yet creation _Ex-nihilo_ makes sense of both the text and reality.

Thus, we finally end where the conversation ought to have begun. Hansen is worried throughout the conversation that by failing a Christian Trinitarian “theology test,” he will be banished to Hell. But bad theology, in and of itself, isn’t why Hell exists. Truly bad (i.e., deceptive, false) theology can lead to injustices and unrighteousness. Those injustices lead to a cosmology where the self is at the center. When the self is central, we ultimately reject God’s centrality. Therefore, Hell exists because people reject God, which often results from bad theology.

Now, a whole new set of conversations can be had over Hell and salvation. I wish Dr. Ortlund and Jacob Hansen had dove into those concepts. But they didn’t. And I won’t either. Hopefully, we can begin to see that by stepping outside of fundamental Christian orthodoxy, we lose sight of God’s centrality and our proper place in the cosmos. But when we see those things rightly, we see very good news.
