Music deafens like suffering. Like suffering, music can stir our senses from outside and from within. So my family jokes that we are cursed. My life tends to go bad with uncanny desperation. When I find myself in the pit, I cannot listen to podcasts and audiobooks like normal. It is too much work. I can, however, put on music. Though it doesn’t get me out of the pit, I find it more spacious and comfortable.

I encourage you to pause and listen to the music linked throughout the article, if not for a moment. Notice how in the song above, the dissonance slowly gives way to harmony. As the harmony swirls together the choir becomes the formidable storm they seek. They seek the storm because the song is [a story about a desperate village](https://lyricstranslate.com/en/%D1%82%D1%8A%D0%BC%D0%BD%D0%B8-%D0%BE%D0%B1%D0%BB%D0%B0%D1%86%D0%B8-temen-oblak-dark-clouds.html) in a drought. To beseech the rain gods, they beg forgiveness, work together, and encourage one another. Whereas the village begins in disunity, they soon realize that justice, and harmony, is the only way to bring the rain.

Now, it may sound odd at first, but the connection between music and suffering is quite biblical. Turn to a page of great suffering in your Bible (I suggest Job 7). Likely, it will be written as poetry. The psalms sing their solemn Maskils. The prophets proclaim justices like thunderous operatics. Job’s tears turn to lamentations in the miry waters of his pain. You see, the Bible asks us to sing a song while in pain.

Why?

There are three reasons why the Bible might ask us to hear a melody amid our suffering. E.Y. Harburg, the man responsible for _Over the Rainbow_ said, “Words make you think a thought. Music makes you feel a feeling. A song makes you feel a thought.” Therein lies the musicality of suffering. Music is a wise companion through suffering, music invites us to feel beyond our intellectual capacities, and music lets us take the next step forward.

### **Music Is a Wise Companion**

Music is a good comforter. When I was a kid, my mom used to turn on the Godspell soundtrack to calm me down. We would sing Marc Broussard driving down the I-10 and The Beatles would accompany us home.

Unlike Job’s friends, music is a wise companion. Eliphaz derides Job. He sings,

> “Should a wise man answer with windy knowledge,  
> and fill his belly with the east wind?  
> Should he argue in unprofitable talk,  
> or in words with which he can do no good?” (Job 15:2-3)

But Job has done nothing wrong (Job 1:8). Whether intentionally or not, Job’s friends have accused him, teased him, and now scorn him amid his suffering. I am Job’s friends. When others suffer, I hardly say the right thing. I often make it worse. In response, Job laments, “I have heard many such things; _miserable comforters are you all._” (Job 16:2, emphasis added).

Music is not a miserable comforter. In fact, music does not ask anything of the sufferer. Music provides space. In that space, people can come to their own conclusions. Ideally, the right song will guide us to appropriate and healthy conclusions. In the Bible, this is also the task of a wise companion.

### **Music Invites us to Feel**

Music does not solve our problems, it invites us to feel them. In my favorite novel, _The Book of The Dunn Cow_, music inspires hope when words cannot. Evil has slithered onto the banks of the chicken coop and Chaunteclear, the head rooster, must inspire everyone to fight back. All of the animals have gathered. The moment arrives. But when Chaunteclear opens his mouth, his words fall to the ground like lead. It isn’t until his mourning wife, Pertelote raises her beak and sings that the moment is fully realized. Pertelote transforms her sorrow through a mournful song. Now the animals are ready to fight.

The reason is because music amalgamates what we know with what we feel and brings forth something new. It’s not always hope. Sometimes music makes us realize that we are truly angry. We know we should not be so mad, yet we feel furious. We ought to ask why we are so mad and bring forth healing. After all, music creates a safe environment in which to feel. It’s often better to work through the depths of our suffering when it won’t explode into the lives of those we love.

Feeling the inmost depths of our affliction is the opposite of what we want to do. The reason why we pose the problem of evil at all is because we would rather think our way through suffering. But if the Bible’s melodies are true then we are not given the right to think ourselves out of suffering.

Further, if we think our way out of our pain, we may confuse the final resolution of our suffering for our own strength. But if we feel our way through affliction we come to realize that any resolution to it is a gift not of our own making.

### **Music Leads us Forward**

Listening to music moves us. It is interesting that Oddysseus was moved by a Siren’s song. Even ancient ancestors knew the provocative power of music. Yet, beyond this enchantment, there is something powerful about the act of listening.

On days when our suffering becomes burdensome, the act of turning on music and listening to it can be provocative. Not only does this action move us outside of our own thoughts and into the world of another, but it also lets us do the “next right thing”. Alan Noble repeats this wisdom time and time again. In a [recent article](https://substack.com/@oalannoble/p-170635681), he wrote,

> “...Your task is faithfulness. Start crawling. Do the next thing.
> 
> And as I’ve stressed in my writing before, in _On Getting Out of Bed_ and in many articles, this must start practically. Getting up, eating, calling a friend or pastor or therapist or all of the above, going for a walk, showering, exercising (so I’ve heard), watching a favorite sitcom or movie, and so on. Taking action.”

I would add to this list, try listening to music. Music helps us to do the next thing by setting the bar appropriately: we must sit and listen. We must feel and not run. The Psalms ask us to do this (c.f., Psalm 121).

### **Music and the End of Time**

So the next time you read Isaiah or Nahum, consider singing it. You will feel what the prophets proclaim. You will know the depth of biblical sorrow. You will empathize with Christ’s cry from the cross.

_To the choirmaster: according to The Doe of the Dawn. A Psalm of David.  
My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?  
Why are you so far from saving me, from the words of my groaning?_ Psalm 22:1

It is fitting, therefore, that the final declaration to the end of suffering is proclaimed, not sung. In Revelation 21 God wipes away our tears like a period at the end of a sentence. It is poetic, certainly. But not done in poetic figures of speech. God actually took upon Himself our suffering. He burns that suffering away like dross through His Son (c.f., Rev. 1:5-7). By Him, our afflictions finally make sense.
