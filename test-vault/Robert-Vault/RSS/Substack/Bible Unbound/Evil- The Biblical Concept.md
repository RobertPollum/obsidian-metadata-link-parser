### **Diving Too Greedily and Too Deep.**

In JRR Tolkien’s The Lord of the Rings, we meet the dwarves. At first, the dwarves are relatively normal creatures, but overtaken by evil’s powerful grip, they develop one nasty vice: greed. They begin to dig deeper and deeper until their lust for gems uncovers an evil that the world is not ready to handle: the balrog.

To be honest, whenever I write on a topic like this, I become uncomfortable. For one, I am too stupid to understand these things. Similarly, I have a moment in my research where I know I plumb the depths of God too greedily and too deep. I worry that by thinking too long about things which we have too few answers, I will unleash a malevolence. Or worse yet, I will become consumed by the malevolence whose desire is to have me.

But if we believe that God created everything, and called it all very good, then we are not only obliged, but empowered to know rightly what evil is. For God defeated death. Therefore those united to Him can look death in the eyes and see its deteriorating impotence.

Finally, it is essential that Christians know what is evil. For, we must know what is good. Isaiah says, “Woe to those who call evil good and good evil, who put darkness for light and light for darkness, who put bitter for sweet and sweet for bitter!” (Is. 5:20).

This is a primer essay on the who, what, when, where, and why (though not in that order) of Evil.

Let’s explore.

### **What is Evil?**

We must know what evil is in order to grasp why evil exists. We find our answer in antiquity rather fast. Athanasius wrote in _On the Incarnation_, “Evil is non-being, the good is being, since it has come into being from the existing God,” (53). Augustine similarly concludes when speaking about evil, vice, and sin, "Consequently, to that nature which supremely is, and which created all else that exists, no nature is contrary save that which does not exist.” (_City of God,_ 382). Or, as Peter Kreeft summarizes, “Evil is real, but it is not a real thing. It is not subjective, but it is not an entity.” Evil is non-being as cold is non-heat or blind is non-sight or a hole is non-earth.

Imagine evil, not so much as a force that enters into our world, but as a vacuum that sucks life out of existence. Evil is the dementor’s breath. As though God were removing the boundaries of the created order and evil sucks all that is good right out. It is real, but it is not a real thing. A force that undoes what God is.

Therefore, Evil de-creates people. This is why ancient Christians saw irrationality as the result of evil (to be irrational, one must undo what accords with reality). When American conservatives demonize liberals for seeing people as groups; when American liberals hold the opinion that conservatives are evil for having other opinions, this is the irrationality that results from having our humanity corroded by evil. What we see around us today is the de-creating effect of evil. We allow our irrationality to get the better of us and we are becoming un-made because of it.

### **Where does Evil come from?**

Augustine launches this theory of non-being (called the theory of Privation) from Genesis 1:2. To be sure, he’s self-critical and humble about diving into these kinds of things. However, Tim Mackie has done a brilliant job detailing Privation via the Hebrew through [Bible Project’s Dragon series](https://bibleproject.com/podcasts/series/chaos-dragon/). Yet, Genesis 1:2 seems plain enough. The author writes, “The earth was _**without form**_ and _**void**_, and _**darkness**_ was over the face of _**the deep.**_ And the Spirit of God was hovering over the face of the _**waters.**_” (Gen. 1:2, emphasis mine). Notice the language used to describe what it is there before God. It is…no-thing. The author uses negative language to describe what it is because it is a non-entity. God truly creates _ex nihilo._

Augustine credits this no-thing to the source of evil, as does Mackie several centuries later. Without going into it too much, the “deep” and “waters” share linguistic roots with the sea and with the serpent that slithers up onto land. Therefore, we see the sea-serpent as an agent bearing non-being. Humanity falls for its illogical trickery and dives headlong into sin.

I imagine the Fall with the Duel of Fates theme playing in the background. You should play it, too. As Adam and Eve bite into the apple, God’s ordered creation begins to crack. Shards of creation shoot through the cosmos. Eyes are opened. Death wraps its sick tendrils around the throats of our heroes. Hope begins to die. A misty rain falls as Eve screams helplessly looking for her husband who has all but abandoned her.

### **Why is there evil?**

How long oh Lord? How long must we now endure the cracking creation (Ps 13:1)? Evil is not often dealt with in prose anymore. Through the Bible, we encounter the deepest suffering in poetry. Again, the likes of Athanasius, Augustine, and Origen, considered the byproduct of evil to be irrationality. We can no longer think our way through evil because, by its very nature, it causes us to be irrational. Though several answers through Christian history have been provided, none of them are sufficient to us in our suffering. Augustine says its beauty. He writes, “the beauty of the course of this world is achieved by the opposition of contraries…‘Good is set against evil, and life against death’” (_The City of God_ 362). Dr. Joshua Sijwaude continues an ancient tradition saying it is to make us better people. But armed with answers, we flounder all the more.

I might posit one other theory for evil. That is, revelation. Suffering is not the only means of revelation in the Bible, but suffering is always revelation. Suffering always reveals something about God, about us, about our world. Therefore, God pulls back the boundary of order, evil rushes in, so that we will know.

We see this from the Exodus. There, the Israelites have been robbed of agency, oppressed by slavery, and threatened to extinction by Pharoah. This is evil; the result is suffering. Yet God repeats this phrase throughout the Exodus narrative, “so that you may know that there is no one like Me in all the earth,” (Ex. 9:14). All this has come upon Israel and Egypt and all the world so that God may reveal His character.

### **Who is Evil?**

Further, everyone is evil. Humanity continues to break creation by driving our fingers into the cracks and pulling. Yes, it causes even the ground to groan from the pain with which creation is eviscerated. Now, that isn’t to say that everything everyone does is evil. This is to say that everyone is born into, and lives in, this state of corruption. Humanity allows more evil in the door through sin. Athanasius is crystal clear that evil is designed to draw us back to God.

Here, I’ll add a quick note on Satan (though he could have his own essay). Satan is the personalized entity ascribed to evil. If we amalgamate Heiser’s Divine Council theory with the book of Job, we get an interesting insight into who (or what) exactly this Satan is.

If you didn’t know, _satan_ is really a title, meaning Accuser. David has a satan, it’s Shamai in 2 Samuel 16 who opposes and curses David publicly. In the book of Job, one of God’s divine council members (some sort of spiritual being) is called the Satan. It stands up to accuse God that Job is really an unjust man. Satan is accusing God of injustice.

We may say that Satan is a member of God’s council, confined to chains in Hell, who maintains God’s sovereignty by watching over the non-existence directly opposed to God. Satan is a creature of the uncreated. By his nature, he de-creates; he steals, kills, and destroys (Jn 10:10). He undoes what is true (Jn 8:44). Satan is really evil. Often, Satan uses creation and humanity to bring about real suffering. But his malice cannot overpower God’s magnanimity.

### **When is Evil going to End?**

Evil begins to lose with the incarnation of the Word. Further, suffering always leads us back to Christ’s passion; suffering is always cruciform. Therefore, we begin to end with Him.

The cross is the climax of, and the response to, evil. The Word God’s crucifixion is the epitome of what evil is: brought into the world by humans to kill God. In the crucifixion, God has stood trial and been condemned.

This death leads to the nucleus of all revelation: the resurrection. The resurrection is God’s answer to evil: it loses. Death dies, loss is lost, and all that is evil will come undone on the final day of God’s judgment. God lives, therefore all that does not live retreats to nonexistence, never to reprise its role in God’s space again.

But the resurrection was only the beginning. A signpost for weary travelers on their way to the Kingdom. One day, God will not allow evil any longer. He will not pull back the veil of revelation.
