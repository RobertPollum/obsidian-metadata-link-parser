I know there’s a lot of advice out there but I’m pretty sure all our problems would be solved by a fuller, more frequent participation in the Lord’s Supper. Therefore, this article will seek to explain a fuller understanding of communion in order to cultivate a greater desire for the table.

#### **A Biblical Theology of Foodstuffs**

Have you noticed how food permeates an inordinate amount of scripture? So delicious. But to understand why, we ought to survey the entire thing as best we can. Then, we will have a multifaceted picture of biblical…eatinghood.

In the beginning, God gives humanity an entire world of food to eat (Gen. 1:29). Since we can’t suppose this to mean that Adam and Eve will singlehandedly consume God’s world, we can see this as a symbolic gesture, indicating God’s gift of superabundance. In other words, food, at creation, shows us that God will provide the proper nourishment in perfected lavishness.[1](#footnote-1)

However, there is one tree that our dynamic dirt duo (humanity) cannot eat from. That is, of course, the Tree of Knowing Good and Bad (Gen 2:17).

But why _eating_? In other Ancient Near Eastern literature, characters also gain the divine knowledge of Good and Evil. But not by eating, trust me. Enkidu, the unlikely friend of Gilgamesh, gains this knowledge by being tricked into intercourse by a prostitute. Therefore, biblical readers must allow a pregnant pause to think through why God chose to contain this knowledge in _food to be eaten_. Personally, given our other options, I guess I’m glad He did!

First, Genesis 2:5-24 replays Genesis 1:1-31 through two parallel sections of proper ordering. In other words, there are two distinct sections within Genesis 2 that replay God ordering creation out of the chaos. I marked up Genesis 2 below for your enjoyment:

Therefore we see that the Bible is communicating this truth: God provides a law that will rightly order humanity and move the world from disorder to order. Still, this doesn’t answer the question about why this truth is communicated in the form of eating. And my best guess is that eating is a daily practice.

The two trees were both situated centrally (Gen. 2:9). Therefore, each day, Adam and Eve needed to either trust God and His right provision, or count equality with God a thing to be seized. The reason this test doesn’t come in the form of a magic incantation or wily courtesan is because the ritual of right-ordering is performed frequently, daily in fact.

Food, here, thus comes to be significant for two reasons. (1) Food shows us God provides what we need and (2) Food plays out the ritual of trusting God, thus making us rightly ordered dirt-creatures. But this isn’t the end of the story.

Leviticus is similarly peppered with commands to eat in God’s presence (Lev. 7:16, 19:6). If we transplant our understanding of food from Genesis, we then realize food also contains _spiritual_ significance. Particularly, this spiritual significance is transported onto the food because of sacrifice.

The spiritual significance is this: specific foods revitalize worship through connection to the divine presence. As R.K. Harrison writes the sacrifice is eaten because it is “meant to promote the well-being of the worshipper.”[2](#footnote-2) Particularly, it is meant to evoke thanksgiving and praise.

However, what we find in communion is not that worshippers need to make a sacrifice to have a revitalized sense of worship, but worshippers need to remember that God made a sacrifice for this to happen. This takes us to John 6.

Many forget that John 6 is an incredibly long chapter. Therefore, the command to eat Jesus’ body and drink His blood (Jn 6:53) ought to come with the entirety of John 6 (indeed all of John) in mind.

The flow of John 6 is quite simple. Jesus is shown to command all of creation by providing an abundance of food for 5,000 people in a wilderness. Next, Jesus is shown to command the chaotic waters by walking over them. On the other side of the sea, the 5,000 swarm Him and Jesus give a command, echoing the Genesis 2:17 imperative: eat from the tree of life, not from your own understanding of good and evil. Well, what Jesus actually says is,

> “Whoever feeds on my flesh and drinks my blood abides in me, and I in him. As the living Father sent me, and I live because of the Father, so whoever feeds on me, he also will live because of me.” (Jn 6:56-57).

Of course by saying something so (let’s be honest) gross, a lot of people walk away! But Peter is insistent these are the “words of eternal life” (Jn 6:68).

It is as though Peter is giving us our line from backstage! We have seen how food shows us that God provides, plays out the ritual of trusting in God, and connects us spiritually to the divine presence thus making us rightly ordered dirt-creatures. Therefore, this perfect food from Heaven is what biblical eatinghood has been pointing us toward all along.

In other words, our understanding of food, both spiritual and physical, collide in John 6. Jesus will provide what His worshippers need to evoke such worship that it will cause their lives to change. And He will do this by providing food.

#### **A Vehicle of Faith**

By now, I’ve suggested that communion is more than a mere commemoration. Rather, it actually provides something and causes something, namely sustenance and worship respectively. But if it is more than commemoration then what exactly is the Lord’s Supper?

I would like to suggest that communion is a _vehicle of faith_. A vehicle is a mode of transport, and faith is a deep-rooted trust in God’s work. Therefore, the eucharist transports us to a deeper faith in Christ which causes a more joyous worship.

My favorite critique of this view on communion is something like “that’s stupid.” As if to say that _God would never use something so innocuous, so simple, to buttress a disciple’s faith_.

I just want you to read that back again slowly.

**“But God chose what is foolish in the world to shame the wise; God chose what is weak in the world to shame the strong;”** 1 Corinthians 1:27, bold emphasis mine in case you missed the irony.

It is actually precisely the means in which God _would_ strengthen the faith of His congregation. As we’ve seen, it tracks best across the biblical epic. And Paul makes this clear in 1 Corinthians 10:16 calling communion a _participation in_ the life of Christ. Without the biblical context, this participation seems merely commemorative, but packed in with the rest of the foodstuffs, we see clearly that this is a literal living out the life of Christ. Or, as Leon Morris comments, “Those who receive the cup rightly receive Christ. They are bound together in fellowship with Christ.”[3](#footnote-3) As Christians, the Holy Spirit uses communion to bring us into the divine presence and strengthen us.

Therefore, this communion is not to be mistaken for the source of our faith. It is a vehicle that moves us to deeper faith. Our faith comes from Christ as a gift (Rom 10:17, Eph 2:8-9). But Christ uses the table to move us into greater fellowship with Him and His body.

#### **Eating the Gospel**

When I was younger, I wanted to be an actor. I often got the same reply, “oh, you want to lie for a living?” I would respond by saying “only bad actors lie.” I used to be so clever.

In the same way, I have hesitated to use the word symbol throughout this article. Only bad symbols are ineffective. Indeed, communion is a symbol. But more akin to the sign-act of Ezekiel eating the scroll God gives him (Ez. 3:1-3).

In the story, God spreads out His hands before Ezekiel and commands him to eat what is provided. In place, God provides some paper (yuck). Ezekiel eats it and, to his surprise, the paper is sweet as honey. But the scroll contains judgment, right? It ought to have been bitter.

It is by this symbolic act that God’s words now reside in Ezekiel. We may say that after Ezekiel has accepted God’s call, he now begins to learn how to trust God. He does this by eating what God provides.

In the same way, the Holy Spirit literally works through the Lord’s Supper to provide faith and strengthen the hope of His people. From our understanding, this makes communion more than a symbol. That is, communion is a symbol that affects our actions.

If you’re familiar with _Avatar the Last Airbender_, you will no doubt recognize these symbols. They stand for the four warring nations of the series’ world. From top-left to bottom-right you may be able to guess that they are: Air, Water, Earth, and Fire. Each nation famously produces individuals with the superhuman ability to manipulate one of these elements. Oddly enough, these people come to live out the element they can control: Firebenders are hot-tempered; Earthbenders are strong and stubborn; Airbenders are flighty and childish—you get the point.

The symbolic world of the nation causes the actions of the individuals. This happens for us, too. It’s called advertising. You see a logo which triggers and emotion which causes you to buy Coca Cola. We live in a world of symbols that cause us to live out certain stories.

So why not eat the Gospel? That’s what communion truly becomes—a way to be animated by the work of Christ. Then, you will live further in a world where you are shaped by the story of the Gospel. Communion not only reminds us of Christ’s sacrifice but the Holy Spirit uses it to drive us to deeper faith. Therefore, we become people who trust fully in God’s provision. Then, we begin to see that all we have is a gift from God. And as Walter Ciszek says in his book, _He Leadeth Me,_ to trust God in this way “is to know peace, to discover a meaning to life, that surpasses all understanding.”[4](#footnote-4) And that is very good news.
