The ancient Dragon begets two beasts from his primordial sea of chaos. Slithering, they slide up from the depths and begin to curdle the cries of the children of God. They make war on the saints.

Daniel once saw these beasts in the sea. But they have grown. A frightful phantom to be sure, for Daniel was convinced that the beasts were political powers (Dan 7:17).

Now, John finds the beast animated by the Dragon. “It was allowed to make war on the saints and to conquer them. And authority was given it over every tribe and people and language and nation” (Rev 13:7). It seems the saints are in real trouble.

But the second beast is new. “It exercises all the authority of the first beast in its presence, and makes the earth and its inhabitants worship the first beast” (Rev. 13:12). It’s too easy for the beast. All it needs to do is give the people what they want! The second beast has some mysterious magical art. Instantaneous, effortless, power in the palms of the people’s hands. They fall and worship.

The saints must contend with two beasts.

### **The Dragon**

John identifies the Dragon for us. It is, “The great dragon…that ancient serpent, who is called the devil and Satan, the deceiver of the whole world” (Rev. 12:9). John paints the Dragon as an anti-Father. It archetypically bears all that is evil and disordered out into the world. Including two beasts from its own essence: the chaotic sea. The Dragon will manipulate the world into worshipping it using the two beasts of Revelation 13.

The end goal is always the same no matter the path: worship the Dragon.

### **The First Beast**

The first beast “was like a leopard; its feet were like a bear’s, and its mouth was like a lion’s mouth. And to it the dragon gave his power and his throne and great authority” (Rev 13:2). Daniel sees “four great beasts \[coming\] up out of the sea, different from one another. The first was like a lion…a second one, like a bear….and behold, another, like a leopard…and dominion was given to it” (Dan 7:3-6). John beholds a beast horrifyingly amalgamating Daniel’s.

Thankfully, since Daniel interpreted his vision for us, we can interpret John’s with confidence. Daniel says, “These four great beasts are four kings” (Dan 7:17). The first great beast of Revelation is a powerful political entity.

To be sure, the people of the world are tricked into worshipping the beast. But this worship is similarly difficult to discern. So John provides three hallmarks for what it looks like to worship the state:

1.  (Illusion) You marvel at the power of the state (3)
    
    1.  Here, you may believe the Justice, Order, and Safety can only be provided by politics
        
2.  (Hubris) You’re not bothered by the state interfering with other nations and peoples (7)
    
3.  (Anxiety) Your attention is captivated primarily by political problems (10)
    

**The Second Beast**

Unfortunately, this is not the end. Another beast rises out of the sulphuric waters. This beast has all the power of the first (13:12). Meaning that, in reality, this beast is a legislative power of its own. But it uses its power differently.

The powers of the second beast can be summarized easily: it generously doles out instantaneous effortless power — a lot like Mammon (i.e., money), which tricks people into thinking it’s beneficial. In turn, this empowers the first beast and leads to the worship of the Dragon.

What we have on our hands is a pseudo-religion that empowers the governing bodies. Fortunately, we have no direct parallel to this in our lifetime.

Of course, that is unless you consider the kind of power one has access to on the AI-era internet as instantaneous and effortless knowledge. In this sense, we ought to expect the internet would empower the State, exacerbating any vice that may be present in a society. If what John says is true, we may expect to see our modern society wrenched apart by political differences which get worse as our technology tailors this instantaneous power to us so that our worlds turn inward, and we dehumanize anyone who thinks differently than we do. Thankfully, there’s no parallel to John’s words to our day.

No, indeed, the saints must contend with two beasts. But the battle is within us as well as without. They are, indeed, the allure of power.

**How to Fight A Dragon**

We can push back against the hopelessness of the dark by planting the holiness of the Garden of Eden. Our world is broken. This desolation becomes a dense fog that makes discerning love difficult. But what Christ has done in us, gives us seeds to plant in this fallow ground. A seed, lit on fire by the Holy Spirit but does not burn; it glows in the darkness. You possess seeds from Tree of Life. Go and subdue the Earth because death will not overcome it.

Growing a garden does not require you to disconnect. It requires you to connect with an intentional community.

To that, we turn to Jesus. His words in Matthew 18:20 are probably more dense than we realize. He says, “For where two or three are gathered in my name, there am I among them.” In the broader Matthean context, it’s almost certain what Jesus has in mind here regards a spirit of humility. As if to say: when you gather, the King is among you. You ought to act accordingly (i.e., in humility). This humility directly opposes the kind of inward turn that our techno-political religion has promised.

Community allows us to slow down. Gardens grow slowly. The Kingdom of God arrives slowly (Lk 13:18-19). Slow helps us to form a foundation of wisdom before we respond to the world’s crises (Jm 1:19). So gather together and slow down! Read poetry together, sing and laugh together, fight and reconcile with one another. Live in an intentional community.

You can’t do this alone anyway. Actually, you can’t do it yourself at all — as Revelation continues, we find that out pretty quick! If you try to defeat the Dragon yourself, you’ll lose every time. Contrary to what you might expect, that is very good news because it pulls us out of our selfish, technology-induced daze, and points us upward to Christ (Col 3:2).
