The magicians of Pharaoh sat specters of themselves, strewn about the palace. The third plague had come upon them unexpectedly. The sheer number of bugs that sprang unnaturally from below was like the dust of Eden itself blowing in the wind. They had no time to prepare. Finally, they had to admit to the king of Egypt that "'This is the finger of God.'" (Ex. 8:19). Though this is the first step the magicians make toward God, it will take millennia before their story is complete.

Not only do magicians play an important role in the Exodus story, but they also play a role in other instances of prophetic rebukes to power. Whereas in Exodus, the magicians have the power of Creation, in Daniel, they have the power of the mind. They are the Wise Men. In the end, though, their wisdom proves to be a mere shadow of God's Wisdom, as do the magicians of Egypt, who find that their creative powers are merely a shadow of God's power.

However, it is possible that your first encounter with the dark arts was not from the Bible at all, but as your friends lined up to read the latest _Harry Potter_ book. Your parents might have prevented such an event because of the Bible's strong condemnation of witchcraft (Ex. 22:18, Lev. 20:27). While your parents were right to be wary of witchcraft, their worry over JK Rowling might have been misplaced.

If you really wanted to condemn witchcraft in its modern sense, you'd have to shut off the device you're reading this on. That is because technology is modern witchcraft. Artificial Intelligence is the most domineering form, and perhaps Elon Musk is the best example of Pharaoh's magician.

This idea may sound scary at first and easy to react against. To be fair, technology does find itself on a spectrum. On one side, technology can allow us to be more human (e.g., the scalpel of a surgeon); whereas on the other side, technology can denigrate what it means to be human (e.g., an addicting social media app). Andy Crouch articulates them best in his book _[The Life We're Looking For](https://www.christianbook.com/Christian/Books/product?event=AFF&amp;p=1227148&amp;item_no=237342)_; I recommend his book for deeper reflection_._ In this article, I would merely like to build on Crouch's argument.

Therefore, we might as well begin where Crouch himself begins, with Jesus and Mammon.

Famously, Jesus says in the Sermon on the Mount, "You cannot serve both God and _mammon."_ (Matt. 6:24, EHV). I chose this specific translation because amid the Greek words, Matthew uses this singular untranslated Aramaic word in the Sermon on the Mount. How odd it must have been to a Greek-speaking Roman to encounter a word in another language in Jesus' most famous κήρυγμα. How odd, indeed. Crouch points out the significance by saying, "Mammon is not simply money but the anti-God impetus that finds its power in money" (Crouch 75).

In other words, Crouch claims that Mammon is the demonic force that embodies what money does. Namely, money provides unlimited, effortless power. This type of self-dependent, effortless power runs entirely contrary to God. It's no wonder that alchemists famously tried to create gold. If they could poof money into existence, then the whole system of effort would be done away with!

In the same way, the magicians in the king's courts were there to provide effortless, impersonal power. Magic is malevolent because it causes us to rely on ourselves with no intention of trusting God or others. Regarding the condemnation of witches in Exodus 22:18, R. Alan Cole says, "We might say that to desire to know the future shows lack of faith, while to desire to control the future is even worse." We lose our ability to trust God and form meaningful relationships when we desire to control the future. If our culture were to rely entirely on technology, the same thing would happen.

As Crouch goes on, he makes the essential point that technology, like magic or money, is an instantaneous, effortless, impersonal power. Have a hankering for a hamburger? GrubHub. Want to become an expert in home repair? YouTube video! Dating, learning, and even baptism can all be done on demand and in your pajamas.

In the past, activities like writing would have taken skill, tact, research, and even a nominal grasp of the English language. Now, not only can I use Grammarly to be my editor (which I did), but I can have Chat GPT write the whole thing.

As an example, my own YouTube channel can act as a stand-in for all creators. Simply type in “[The Story of Paul](https://www.youtube.com/results?search_query=the+story+of+paul)” on YouTube. Not only do you get my video from Bible Unbound—which took dozens of hours worth of research, hundreds of hours of editing, years of experience, and thousands of dollars worth of equipment to pull off—but you also get the AI imitations, which were compiled in mere minutes. Generative AI is the most effortless and instantaneous power we have, making it the highest (perhaps darkest) form of magic in the modern era.

This is perhaps why it's nearly poetic that the man who designed the most technologically advanced automobile in decades is in the court of the king. To be clear, this is not a political post. You could say the same for Jeff Bezos, Bill Gates, or the late Steve Jobs—but no one's story has yet presented itself as poignantly as Musk. Put the pieces together, and life seems more enchanting: we (quite literally) have the expert of modern magic trying to make the United States government run more effortlessly, impersonally, and powerfully! This stuff writes itself. Elon Musk is the modern magician.

But don't get me wrong; I see the irony. I'm writing this on my laptop, streaming Friends in the background while organizing church meeting plans via text. So before we turn on phones faster than we turned on Rowling in the 1990s, let me continue telling the story of magicians in the Bible.

You see, there’s good news. Throughout the Bible magic and the magicians serve Babylon. In the same way, technologies are not Babylon, but they certainly serve Babylon. This is good news because we can be sure that God will use the technologies we build for His ultimate purposes and inevitably remove all that is opposed to Him (c.f., Prov. 21:1).

After the third plague, the magicians began to fear defeat. It won't be until the sixth plague that they give up altogether (Exodus 9:11). Magicians more or less fade into the background until the prophecy of Daniel. Tradition has it that when Daniel becomes the wisest man in Babylon, he begins a diasporic school of Jewish wisdom. This school continues for nearly 500 years before a star rises in the West, and these magicians travel back to the promised land in droves. They are convinced that the Jewish scriptures house an ancient wisdom that is manifest in the stars. In other words, they believe the messiah has come—not because God told them, but because the stars aligned. They disturb all of Jerusalem with this message. But in the end, the magicians find the messiah, greet Him with gifts, and bow down to worship Him (Matt 2:11).

Magicians and their magic will always end up at the feet of the messiah, gifts in hand, worshipping Him. God's purposes will always be victorious. Even though magic is designed to turn our eyes inward, God will always be brilliant enough to turn our hearts outward. The broad appeal to magic in the Bible is that it represents a cheap substitute for God's true power over our hearts and lives.

Once we are united to Christ, we are pulled to actively stand against these dark arts by forming meaningful communities. Embracing embodied community—and technologies that advance this embodied community—is the way we reject selfishness. Therefore, instead of throwing away our phones, we ought rather to go to church. In that way, we will find good news in our magical age.
