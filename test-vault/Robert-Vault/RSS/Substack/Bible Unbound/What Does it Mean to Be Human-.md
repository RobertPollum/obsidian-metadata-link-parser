### To Be Made As Storytellers of Stars

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!8gaT!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F437b4ff7-fe5c-4d0e-966e-2a08976220a0_1200x824.jpeg)



](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!8gaT!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F437b4ff7-fe5c-4d0e-966e-2a08976220a0_1200x824.jpeg)

_The Creation of Light_ John Martin

For a long time I was told that humans are the only animals that can sleep on their backs. I liked to imagine an ancient ancestor sleeping under the stars, making meaning from the constellations as they tried to fall asleep. These star-stories passed down from father to daughter, mother to nephew, until an entire tribe was itself a constellation of stories.

I always imagined that when God created Adam, Adam lay face up like he was sleeping on his back and God breathed into his nostrils like He was performing divine CPR. Sleeping on one’s back would’ve had many survival benefits. But ultimately it meant a human was faced toward the gods. They were invested in realities beyond themselves and when we look up at the stars, as we do when we lay on our backs, we find that we are not as important as we often think ourselves to be.

Of course, two things. One, plenty of animals sleep on their backs. My dog, Willow, for instance likes to sleep on her back with her paws stuck straight up in the air, stiff, as though she dreamed of being Krypto the Superdog. And also, when I sleep on my back, I have this nasty habit where I stop breathing or get sleep paralysis. So it’s typically stiff necks and back pain for me as a side-sleeper.

In fact, the list of things that sets humans apart from animals is surprisingly short—and getting shorter. For a while, it was believed humans were the only creatures that grieved, until scientists found similar grief-like patterns among whales. We’ve now witnessed elephants painting when given a paint brush, or orangutans opting to better a group over themselves in certain situations. We hear things sing and talk and laugh and it always all felt so pertinent to being human. Until now. Now, we scarcely know what it means to be human. And as technology advances, you often hear people say that it will one day be impossible to tell what is human and what is robot.

In every century after Christ, the Church has had to answer big questions. In the first century it might’ve been, how do the gentiles fit in? Then it was, what is the nature of the trinity? Then, what is the nature of Christ? The nature of the sacramanets? Of Grace? And in all of these, the question about what it means to be human has _come up…and been skirted around!_

I first heard Carl Trueman, author of _The Rise and Triumph of the Modern Self,_ bring this up on the Podcast for Pastor Theologians—but I’ve heard many more theologians and philosophers say it since. That is that the most important question for the Church to answer in our time is “What does it mean to be human?”

In many ways, a doctrine of anthropology (i.e., what does it mean to be human) has been largely neglected throughout church history. The nature of humanity has either been so taken for granted or been so difficult to define that it has often been defined in the negative. Or, what being human isn’t. For instance, the first treatise we really have on this topic is Irenaeus’ _Against Heresies_—disproving the gnostic idea that the body is evil. Since the incarnation, we have at least considered the body a good thing. But being a human is much more than being a body.

The idea of being human is at one time so simple that I think it can be captured in one sentence. Yet, at another time, so complex that we are going to take the rest of the article to unpack nearly each word in that sentence.

Previously, we have found that God is a magnificent king who created a brilliant kingdom. But after learning about who we are, we may begin to question who the King truly is, thus the story of the Bible is set in motion.

#### To put it simply, _**A Human is an individual created in God’s image with a body united to a soul.**_

Each word in that thesis is not only controversial but also loaded with meaning. However, the nature of being human is so complex that it needs to capture everything from babies in the womb to those bedridden at home and even those with physical, mental and emotional challenges that make them “useless” to society. While at the same time excluding robots, clones, questioning aliens, and even making sense of Christ!

Because of all that, this is Bible Unbound, let’s explore.

#### **A Human is an individual created in God’s image with a body united to a soul.**

### **To Be a Human**

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!jeyV!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F5062d8ab-96c5-4679-974e-a6d9047b15a5_640x410.jpeg)



](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!jeyV!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F5062d8ab-96c5-4679-974e-a6d9047b15a5_640x410.jpeg)

_The Creation_ Lawrence W. Ladd

**A human** is the thing we’re questioning today. Though, to be fair, the first half of our definition (A human is an individual) doesn’t quite set these guys apart in a meaningful way.

You see, in Genesis, these creatures are created on the sixth day along with the animals—actually, from the same stuff as the animals and then blessed—actually, actually, with the same blessing as the fish…

“God blessed them, saying “be fruitful and multiply and fill the waters in the seas and let birds multiply on the Earth.” (1:22) “And God said _let the Earth_ bring forth living creatures.” (1:24).

…When God says that, did you know: Humans haven’t been created yet! And aren’t created until Genesis 1:26. In connection with Genesis 2:7, we find that they are made out of the Earth and blessed to fill the Earth and multiply. In other words, humanity does share some commonalities with the beasts of the field and the birds of the air. Of course, as the story moves on we find that the more humanity looks like those beasts, the worse off they become. But we’re not quite there yet. We’ll look at that in a few minutes.

Further, _everything_ is given God’s breath of life, including the birds, fish, plants, beast and yes the humans according to Genesis 2:7. That is because God’s Spirit, His breath, is the thing that animates all reality. For all reality resides within Him. We may just say that if we were to examine the landscape of creation, without an eye toward God, we would conclude that humanity is in fact no different at all from the birds and the beasts. We are another created being.

### **To Be Created**

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!4qpI!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F358b8dbf-303c-4c68-a37e-adb3bc3d88d4_640x507.jpeg)



](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!4qpI!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F358b8dbf-303c-4c68-a37e-adb3bc3d88d4_640x507.jpeg)

_**Creation of Adam**_ **Jan Brueghel the Younger**

But that **humanity is created** is quite important. It means that we are a different being from God—or ontologically different from God—and that we are limited. God is eternal and noncreated. We have a physical beginning as individuals and as a collective whole. Therefore, we cannot do everything, we must rest. We must accept our limitations as part and parcel of how God created us. But being created also means that humanity—individually and collectively—has immense value. It means that God wanted us to exist. It means God wanted you to exist.

Now, I’ve had plenty of people ask me “why would God ever want me?” Not as an act of awe and contrition, but nearly as an act of defiance. As if to say, “my life has been hard enough and I don’t want to have been created!” To those people, I usually respond by asking them why does the bird sing, or the peach taste sweet? It is for joy and beauty. God created you out of love. A difficult enough proposition to believe, I know. But a true one nonetheless.

Being created also implies the existence of a purpose. All things made have a purpose—even if that purpose is to say nothing has a purpose. The reason we exist, therefore is, if nothing else, to be loved—and accept that love as true. Thankfully, we exist for many reasons besides.

If we have a purpose, we also have an intended function. That is, to be oriented toward God and to be God’s image out into the world. In short, we exist to glorify God and enjoy Him as He loves us.

### **To Be God’s Image Individually**

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Hgwv!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F70b31c3a-af16-4cab-a838-0429f7bb3887_999x1000.jpeg)



](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Hgwv!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F70b31c3a-af16-4cab-a838-0429f7bb3887_999x1000.jpeg)

_Let Us Create Man in Our_ _Image_ Jennifer Jones

But if our createdness gives us general value, our being **made in God’s image** gives us a unique value—a holy value, set apart from all the other creatures. For herein we find the very thing that makes us different from all others: we are made in God’s image. Theological scholars will agree with that premise, however, begin to deeply disagree on what it means to be made in God’s image.

Now, as a bit of context, the image of the gods was nothing new to the ancient imagination—but it was different from the biblical account. The image of a god was the giant statue found in their temple—it was the idol. It made the reverent aware that this deity’s presence was found in this temple. Smaller versions of these statues could be carved and placed in homes in order to represent a small portion of this god’s presence in the home. The image communicated something of the divine’s presence in a space.

The Bible, then, first introduces us to humanity with this very metaphor: that we are the image, or idol statue, of God. The story imagines that humanity communicates something of God’s character and presence. Theologians call these the communicable attributes of God. Communicable meaning shareable. They are primarily the moral characteristics of God. For instance, God did not share his omnipresence. We know this because of…how things are. I am not physically with you on your jog or in your chair while you read this. I am limited by time and space. However, I can communicate patiently and lovingly and kindly much like God can. Human beings have the capacity to reflect God’s character.

Theologians have broken this down further. These moral characteristics are conveyed by our rationality—or because we are capable of thinking, wisdom, and will. If I can imagine what it means to be kind, I am capable of being more kind. And by our relationships—I can only be kind in relation to other things whether that’s you or God or the part of myself in need of kindness. We are rational, relational creatures because we are made in God’s image. Thomas Aquinas put it best, if not more complexly. He said that being made in God’s image was to have the capacity for theological virtues. Or, virtues that are aimed toward and and given by God.

In the narrative arc of Genesis, to be made in God’s image is to do the things God does. In particular, to be made in God’s image is to have dominion over the Earth. _Consequently, to bear God’s image, as an office, is to be a rational, relational, ruler primarily through theological virtues._

This makes further sense if we think of being made in God’s triune image. Reinhold Niebuhr in his books _The Nature and Destiny of Man,_ claims that we are able to be individual humans within a collective humanity because we are made in God’s triune image. In other words, if God were three persons in one, then we would expect God to create both one human (Adam) and many people (humanity) in His image. Which He does over the course of Genesis 1-2.

The biblical authors wrote Genesis 1-2 with a wink and a nod. For we are introduced to humanity made in God’s image (Gen 1:26) and God proceeds to make an individual person (Gen. 2:7). This individuality is significant for many reasons but suffice it to say, being made as an individual means that you are uniquely crafted by God. If a person were to, say, re-make you in a tube, it would have serious theological repercussions.

Least of which is judgment and justice. If God can create us as individuals, then God can also judge us as individuals. If God can judge us, then we have the capacity to desire all of God’s image bearers to bear His image. Thus we desire the world to be a better place, and us to be better individuals.

All of that said: we have the unique capacity to desire virtue for ourselves and communities.

### **To Be A Body-Soul**

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!2HW6!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F56503633-fb3c-4897-b4ba-cee341d0d777_2000x1244.png)

_ADAMAH_ Jennifer Jones

Finally, a human is an image-bearer with **a body united to a soul**. Nowhere in the Bible is it said that a person is both body and spirit. But in Genesis 2:7 we find God breathing the breath of life into Adam and he becomes a living being. That phrase, living being, is actually what’s important for us today. _Hayah Nephesh_ in Hebrew or the Soul that is alive. Nephesh is the essence of the thing. The being-ness to being. So when God breathes His breath into Adam, he springs to life as someone who is now animated by God’s Spirit.

To be clear, the body is the material stuff humans are made of. Our bodies also make us limited to time and space, unlike God who is Spirit (Jn 4:24). A Spirit is the immaterial and immortal part of a creature. This is the whole self; the heart, soul, mind, and strength complex that Christ says we ought to love God (and consequently neighbors) with. The perfect unification of which is expressed most supremely in the incarnation and resurrection which unites all believers to Christ’s Holy Spirit (Jn 1:14).

### **To Be Un-Made in Sin**

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!HOC7!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F71baff28-3463-453d-961b-0faa142c5ec3_3597x2293.jpeg)



](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!HOC7!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F71baff28-3463-453d-961b-0faa142c5ec3_3597x2293.jpeg)

_The Destruction of Sodom And Gomorrah_ by John Martin

Now, of course, you may have already been thinking that to be a human is a tall order! I mean how are you supposed to be a rational relational ruler of the Earth and virtues when the dog won’t even listen to you to get off the couch? Well, this is where sin enters the picture.

After humanity eats from the Tree of the Knowledge of Good and Evil, sin actually inverts what it means to be human. Sin de-creates us by marring the image of God and forces us to reject a body-soul complex. The fall mars God’s image and breaks our unity with nature, with God and with others. Ultimately, the purest result of this de-creating effect is that it tricks us into thinking the center of control is within humanity and not God. Therefore, what happens at the Fall is not the giving of curses but the loss of graces.

For you see that Adam and Eve are not cursed at the Fall. No, the serpent, or evil itself, is cursed—ultiamtely to be made impotent. And the ground is cursed—ultimately making the abundance of Eden syphoned.

Humanity now merely lives in light of the curse of Evil and Eden, suffering the consequences of believing we are the center of the universe. To that end, carrying out the blessing of God in light of the curse means that making more humans will be painful and laborious and sustaining human life will be painful and laborious. What we need will be for someone to come and make more humans and sustain life eternally _for us._

As though the act of sleeping on our back, looking heavenward to forget ourselves, becomes impossible. We must now sleep on our proverbial sides. The kingship of humanity becomes marred by our perceived and actual inability to control anything. The goal then will be to restore humanity to their rightful place as rational relational rulers. But how do that without humanity living forever in this painful and impure state? How do you restore humanity back to something which they now by nature reject? We will need a king to lead us back through the treacherous valley of death. This king will need to be the perfected, uncreated image of God whose body is perfectly united to His soul. This king will need to receive more blessedness than Adam in order that we might be united to Him and become what we were made to be: God’s image bearers.
