_The Destruction of Pompeii and Herculaneum_ by John Martin

At the time, we were young missionaries, traveling across the country in a 15-passenger van. We stood in a Washington, DC art museum before a painting of an apocalypse: fire rained from Heaven as people fled naked across a cracked wasteland.

My friend and I sat and stared at the terrifying scene. When suddenly, a man approached. Noticing our focus he said, “Trust me, it’ll b’worse than that on J’dgement Day!” Startling us out of our reverence.

“Tha’s right,” he continued. “Mos’ people dn’t kn’w’it but bears’ll eat women and God’ll send ants to eat your children!”

We stared, stunned. Was this supposed to be an opportunity to share the gospel? He hobbled off before we could reply. My friend and I burst out laughing. What just happened?

My best guess is that we had met a man overwhelmed by injustice, seeking meaning in texts like Revelation or Daniel. When he read about God’s justice, he longed for dramatic acts, like bear-maulings and flesh-eating ants, to set things right.

After all, the central challenge that Christianity faces is the problem of evil: how can an all-powerful, wholly good God allow suffering? Unlike other worldviews, Christianity uniquely insists on both God’s omnipotence and omnibenevolence.

Theologians have posited theories for centuries about how God can be both just and allow evil. These theories are called theodicies. One such theodicy is known as the privation theory. This concerns the _nature_ of evil as a non-entity: a lack of reality rather than a reality itself.

In this sense, privation theories see evil as a force that sucks good out of reality. I have previously likened this to the dementors of Harry Potter. Therefore, God has the power to remove or replace the barrier that holds back this privational force.

Yet privation theories barely address _why_ a good God lets evil unmake His world. We know that God doesn’t want evil undoing His creation (Ez. 33:11, Ps 5:4). All that eventually happens is for God’s glory and our joy (Gen. 50:20, Rom 8:28). Still, He allows evil (Is 45:7, Job 1:12). You may even say that He allowed Adam and Eve to eat from the tree of knowledge in the first place— so, why?

I believe that an apocalyptic theodicy is essential to understand why God allows evil to persist. Though, I pray God have grace on me as I dive into the things of this nature. I am too stupid to know why God does anything. Yet, here we are.

Apocalypse is a genre found in the Bible. The word itself is Greek for revelation (ehem, that’s where we get the name of the book! If you squint and tilt, you can kind of see it: αποκαλυψς …eh maybe not). This word is a contraction with the prefix απο (_apo-)_ “from” and καλυπτος (-kaluptos) “covering”. Literally, a revelation is the _uncovering_ of an event. The apocalypse reveals what’s really going on behind the veil.

Therefore, every apocalypse in the Bible contains the following: a revelation of God’s space that points the reader to the end times. In this way, Daniel, Revelation, parts of Ezekiel and Isaiah are all apocalyptic stories.

Oddly, each book is written in the context deep suffering and evil. The revelation of the prophets is this: God confronts evil and establishes justice. Justice becomes a framework from which His kingdom grows.

Therefore, we can add Job to the list of apocalyptic works. It is the Bible’s seminal theodicy, whose meaning has long been debated. But the climax of Job’s suffering is when God reveals how His space operates. This revelation then becomes the trellis that rebuilds Job’s life.

To say it plainly, evil and suffering exist for the purpose of revelation. Evil that causes us personal suffering reveals something about who we are, who God is, and ultimately, what is to come. These revelations reshape us and (re)orient us toward God’s larger purposes.

This change can be made in two directions, though. Proverbs 10:25 says, “When the tempest passes, the wicked is no more, but the righteous is established forever.” As though God allows evil and suffering to persist so that His people can flourish in their newfound revelation of Him. After all, Job was a righteous man (Job 1:1).

To be sure, this is not the same as an exemplarist model of theodicy. Exemplarist theories state that evil exists to make us better people. Since I don’t find this consistent with Christ’s life in particular, I am shifting the focus somewhat.

In contrast, this Apocalyptic Privation Theodicy holds that evil and suffering exist to reveal who God is, how He works in history, and/or who we are in light of Him. Christ’s suffering is the ultimate revelation (Rom 5:8, 2 Cor 5:15, 1 Pt 3:18).

In this way, our suffering always points us upward to where He is seated at the right hand of the Father. Our suffering always points others back toward the cross. Evil and suffering become ways for the gospel to be proclaimed in time and space. Suffering brings us and others to Christ.

_Christ the Intercessor_ by Josh

We see this truth reflected in baptism. There, the person is drenched in the chaotic waters. But they rise with Christ. This resurrection reveals the baptized as a person united with Christ, and proclaims it to the spiritual realm (c.f., 1 Pt 3:18-22). In the same way, when we suffer, the Christian is revealed to be united to Christ through their perseverance which is proclaimed to the principalities.

Therefore, all suffering is eschatological. Suffering causes us to yearn for God’s kingdom to come; to long for that malevolent dragon to be slain. Ultimately, suffering reveals a God who is bringing the end to fruition.

This eschatological hope is what that odd man in the museum had, however misguided (I hope). He knew that one day all would be made right again. That is the foundational revelation of suffering. That the God of all grace and peace is in charge (Job 42:2).
