For all intents and purposes, this video, released about 7 months ago, was a flop. I know 18k views is not something to scoff at. But it took nearly six months to create and hundreds of hours of research. More than that, it has much of my heart baked into the core.

I also anticipate criticisms about the introduction being too slow and the middle dragging – I get it. Yet, if this article feels thin, it's because I have already proven much of what I am saying in the video. You may want to watch that.

The problem is that many people missed a significant component of the story, even if they decided to watch. So, let me try to spell it out a bit more clearly.

Christians need to change their relationship with politics as the religiosity of politics changes.

Let’s explore! 🚀

Often, we think of our relationship to politics as stagnant. However, my proposal here is that we ought to think of it more like our relationship to money, alcohol, or anger: prowling around and in flux. Of course, if you've watched the above video, you know that this sentiment is not explicit. This is purposeful. I want my videos to immerse us in the world of biblical storytelling. In those waters, we are formed into citizens of God's Kingdom.

Intentionally, the video drops the audience into various points throughout the biblical epic to paint a picture of who Babylon is in the Bible. As Leon Morris writes, "The name \[Babylon\] thus stands for the pride of mankind…the symbol of mankind in community opposed to the things of God.” As the video concludes from this, the spirit of Babylon creates a self-seeking desire for preservation.

In the Bible, this desire is always threatening God's position. Though I concede, as Paul says, "I speak in a human way." (Rom 3:5). Our kingdoms are never real threats to God (Ps 2:1). Still, we try to overthrow Him because we think we would make better rulers than Him.

Because humans are moving toward this end, the political systems we set up are also constantly moving toward this coupe-de-tete. To be clear, they seldom begin this way in the Bible. Pharaoh welcomed Abraham, Joseph had a good relationship with Egypt, and Daniel was amicable to Nebuchadnezzar. But the stories also rarely end this way. Abraham is run out of town; a new pharaoh rises who does not know Joseph; Nebuchadnezzar reveals he is truly a beast of a man. From Babel, we get Rome.

You see, the narrative pull of biblical empires is always toward religiosity. In other words, politics has the tendency to become religion in the Bible. When politics becomes religious, Christians need to reevaluate their relationship to political systems. For some, this proposition is satisfactory enough to end the article, though I suspect it is not for most. This creates our first major problem: definitions.

Though I am not an expert in either of these categories, we must now turn to the daunting task of defining politics and religion. I am going to dive in headlong!

By politics, I mean the activities associated with governance. This is very broad. I want to keep it as broad as possible for two reasons. First, it should give us pause since much of American life falls into this category. Two, on its own, politics is not a bad thing; it provides order and resources. I am still an Augustinian; even an evil government is better than anarchy!

By religion, I mean an individual's trust in a higher power. Religion is notoriously difficult to define, to be sure. But here, I would not focus on the activities of religion but on the essence of belief itself.

Put together, politics becomes religion when activities associated with governance become the higher power in which we put our trust. When we rely on political leaders to keep us safe through legislation and warfare, we know we are taking one step into the throes of political idolatry. Like a swift current, you are sure to be sucked under faster than you realize.

To state the obvious, the alternative to trusting our government to provide safety is trusting in God. This is how Babylon will fall: God compels us to refuse a self-seeking desire for safety. This courage begets Moses, David, and the heroes of the biblical epic.

If I were to guess, had you made it this far without being upset, then you would readily agree with the above. But for many, this is a hard saying (c.f., Jn 6:52-60). To that person, let me propose (first) Mordecai as an example to follow and (second), a reminder of your freedom in Christ.

In the third chapter of Esther, we meet Haman the Agagite on the first day of a new job. When Haman walked into the office that day, everyone bowed down to him (typical). "But Mordecai did not bow down or pay homage" (Es 3:3). In other words, Haman did not even have to do anything, good or bad, to warrant Mordecai's protestation of worship. Mordecai did not bow down to Haman at all because Mordecai trusted God. The consequences were dire, and safety was stripped away. But through trust in God, the Hebrews were saved. Mordecai is then elevated for his unrelenting trust (6:8-10) and even made second in command like Joseph (10:3).

In other words, there should be no reason to stand up and back away when we find ourselves replacing God with politics. We do not need to give an inch of our trust or hope to those in charge. It is in God we trust. As we notice our temptations to trust in policy over the Provider, then we have the freedom to repent. Christians have an opportunity to adjust how we relate to politics before it devours us.

That is very good news.
