The clearing of rose bushes was the perfect place for my young imagination to run wild. It was set back into the woods just far enough that I was sure no one would find me. Plus, you had to crawl under a nasty bramble of branches to reach me.

As a boy, I was fascinated by the potential for magic I knew the wide world was hiding. I figured there were certainly dragons — after all, we hadn’t explored every cave in the East. Venture through the Mongolian Steppes, and you are bound to find a mysterious house with a warlock. The South American jungles were teeming with ancient magical gemstones, granting their possessors tremendous strength to beat up bullies.

The glade of rose bushes behind our old house was a step closer to this magic. It was abuzz with life! Literally, the rose bushes hummed with such strength, the sound went right through me…Strange? Why would-

Hundreds of bees began to swarm out of hives hidden around the rose bushes.

I ran like hell, scratching up the sides of my arms and legs, going through that bramble on the way in.

I never went back to my hiding place ever again.

This type of thing happens as we grow older, actually. It can feel as though when we find something beautiful, hundreds of bees pour out of nowhere to attack us. As life goes on, we wisen up. We learn not to stay too long in an clearing of rose bushes. Soon, we stop noticing the roses altogether. We can be hurt by our expectations for how the world should be.

### A Theology of Imagination

Beyond thinking damp caves house red dragons, _imagination_ broadly refers to the way things should be. A moral imagination is the capacity to which we can envision a moral world. A social imagination is the way our social world operates beyond our conscious awareness. A gospel imagination is the degree that we envision God’s kingdom shaping our world.

Unfortunately, unmet expectations are precisely what disparage our imagination. But as imagination fades, so does our whimsy and, finally, our ability to enjoy anything.

That may sound dramatic. But I’ve seen it happen to plenty of people. I see it happening to all of us online. So the urgent question becomes: how do we manufacture imagination, and is that even possible?

**Well, to generate imagination, we must first cultivate beauty.**

### **A Theology of Beauty**

I have spoken of this [elsewhere,](https://bibleunbound.substack.com/p/to-make-something-beautiful?r=1f0vew) but it’s always good to restate it. Beauty is the orderliness of chaos. A mountainscape may take our breath away because the peaks are ordered where we expect chaos. A person, too, is beautiful by the same thinking. But so is literature, or a particularly satisfying conversation. To the degree that something is ordered correctly, that is the degree to which we will think it’s beautiful.

A clearing of rose bushes is beautiful. Amid the wild and waste of the North Florida swamps, one would never expect to find a perfectly preserved clearing. Better yet, it represented a solitude I often sought. There, my imagination could run freely, intoxicated by the smell of a southern spring sweetness. It was beautiful.

### **But Then the Bees**

But sometimes the chaos crashes back in on the things we find beautiful. Chaos is the de-ordering effect of sin. When the chaos comes, we can be disoriented and uncomfortable. Our brains make the logical leap and alert us never to return there again.

So, when I was twelve, and I experienced my first taste of death, my imagination began to fade. In an instant, the world stopped being beautiful and became precarious. We often find ourselves in a world that seems forsaken by hope. Whimsy can feel a treacherous ally in unstable times.

### **You Must Run From the Bees**

One crucial mistake is that we stop and surround ourselves with bees (more often the metaphorical variety) of this sort. You will get stung. Maybe in the eyes, rendering you unable to run much farther.

Bees are the chaos crashing back in. This can be our addiction to social media, or politics, or, as Paul says, “foolish controversies, genealogies, dissensions, and quarrels about the law, for they are unprofitable and worthless.” (Titus 3:9). The longer we decide to dwell among the bees, the longer we are formed in the image of chaos.

Both individuals and societies can be formed in the image of chaos. When that happens, our imaginations dull and we cannot see the world for what it ought to be. We will have no answers to social problems because we lack the creative capacity to produce them. Truth will become a tool to gain power over others. Our neighbors will bear the weight of our oppressive, domineering nature because we will no longer have the capacity for whimsy.

We must cultivate an imagination beyond the chaos.

### **Cultivating an Imagination**

To spark your childlike imagination, I have a simple suggestion: tend to beauty. As said before, beauty can be amphibious, adapting to the environment you find yourself in. But you must seek it out regardless.

This will often require three things from you:

1.  Seek God. This is the first and foundational step. God’s word, prayer, and people provide the kind of structure our hearts desire when we need beauty. In them, we find hope, rhythms, and belonging. Seek Him in them, and soon we will be able to slow down.
    
2.  Slow down. When we begin to notice God in the details of scripture, or the words of His people, we will notice Him in other details with greater ease. By seeing God in the details of life, we cultivate hearts of gratitude, compassion, and selflessness.
    
3.  Search for beauty. Set time aside to go for walks in the woods, stare at art, read good books, know someone intimately, and all the other things that rightly order us as humans. Don’t just stumble across it, but actively seek it out.
    

When done well, you will see the world as it once was. Suddenly, new possibilities will spring forth from the ancient depths. These habits will tell new stories, and those stories will be a childlike gift of whimsy.
