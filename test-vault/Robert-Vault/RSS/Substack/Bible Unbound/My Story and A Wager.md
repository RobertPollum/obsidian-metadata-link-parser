I was born in fourth grade when I made my archnemesis cry in front of the entire school. Of course, I was not physically born in the fourth grade. That'd be nonsense. But this was the moment that catalyzed my life. We had been competing in a fierce county-wide storytelling competition, and I had won, beating out my opponent. But as soon as I won, whispers began to blow through the auditorium that I had cheated.

For context, my childhood was built by the swampy charm of the North Florida Panhandle. Every morning, my dad, two brothers, and I drove one hour to the K-12 school that we all attended. Naturally, the entire school thought I had cheated, aided by my father, who taught storytelling classes at our school.

This was, of course, untrue; I didn't cheat! As true storytellers, my brothers and I were always responsible for writing our tales. Dad was certainly involved, though. Our father perfected our stories in two ways. One, quite literally through the sheer force of his love for us; and two, the intense work ethic he instilled within me and my siblings – often practicing, memorizing, and reciting late into the night. I can still feel the hot tears upon hearing the words from the top: we started reciting all over again.

Nonetheless, my dad was an incredible man. Still, to this day, he was one of the most selfless people I had ever met. He was constantly giving away some inexplicable source of joy that seemed to come from nowhere but some depth of his inner being.

He used to say that stories keep us alive, like the drum beat of a song or the heartbeat of a man. Therefore he'd say all stories were true — even if some were more true than others. Fiction or nonfiction, all stories meet us and tell us what it means to be human.

Then, on September 1st, 2009, my dad died suddenly. That day, the bottom fell out from under a small 12-year-old boy. A boy who would only be the same in one respect: I would love my father by loving stories as he did. To carry on his legacy meant telling good stories. Several years passed, and I was no more than a punk kid too awkward to admit it. I was hyperactive, cunning, and angry. But when I found solace in the free food at a youth group, I came to a place to land.

I was raptured into the biblical story. I immediately picked up on this epic tale about angels and demons, God and humanity, Christ and redemption. I joined Christ's story and became a Christian later that summer. But, while my conversion was genuine, I did not understand my motivations yet. I had become a Christian a lot like a man who buys a good luck charm. But good luck never came. Only a lot of confusion.

To make a long story short, it hit me all at once, several years afterward, and in a fury of grace. Author JK Rowling describes finding the Harry Potter story; as though the idea were floating through the air, and she happened to be in its way. This is what it felt like when I finally understood the grace of the gospel—a whisper floating through the air.

Then, I began to see the gospel on every page of the Bible. There were seven days of creation and seven days of Passion Week; the Day of Atonement took away sins, and Christ's atonement takes away sin. I began to hear this good news in a thousand ways from dozens of authors spanning millennia. If this were true, then the implications would be massive. After all, stories shape who we are. The Bible thus shapes our actions by tumbling us around in the Gospel. The heart of Bible Unbound began to beat in those early days of understanding.

The problem was that when I began to tell others of this revelation, they looked at me like I was insane! The Bible, they said, was not a story. It was meant to tell us how to live. They would go to church and be told that the five smooth stones David picks up represent five ways to be a better person, and I would die a little more in my chair.

This is why, during the pandemic, I began creating podcasts and videos for a YouTube channel called Bible Unbound. I wanted to prove to others that the Gospel is everywhere in the Bible. I wanted to show how the story of the Bible is the very thing that kept Christianity alive. It is the drumbeat of our faith. And others all over the world resonated with this vision to discover the Bible by uncovering the Gospel in every biblical story. It was as though the biblical epic was a massive black hole waiting to pull you in. You realize that the mounting weight of God's grace is difficult to ignore once you begin to fall. The Bible's beauty through the Gospel's lens is boundless—unbound. When people watch _The Story of Paul_ or _The Story of Lydia_, they get pulled into this wild world, and it changes them.

And it is awesome! Awesome to see others enter into this chasm of grace. Just look at what this viewer wrote on the Story of Jacob. They said, ​​_"I could never focus on reading the bible to better understand it as I have \[to\] maintain focus when reading but this video made it so much easier to understand as I want to bring my family and myself closer to Christ."_

My passion became exploring the Word of God to find how the gospel shows up and then presenting that to others. This adventure led me to a Bachelor's degree in Biblical Studies. Soon, I will begin at Western Theological Seminary for a Master's in Biblical Studies to continue pursuing God's word.

But it is my deep belief that nothing I produce at Bible Unbound should cost a dime for anyone. The gospel should be free. Access to biblical literacy should not be gate-kept. So now I am reaching out, trusting that God will inspire generosity within others that overflows into ministry funding.
