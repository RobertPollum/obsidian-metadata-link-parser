The next total solar eclipse visible in North America is taking place on April 8, with the path of totality passing over 13 states and several major metropolitan areas, including Mazatlan, Dallas, Indianapolis, Cleveland, and Montreal. [More than 31 million people](https://www.greatamericaneclipse.com/april-8-2024 "open in a new window") live inside the path already, and [several million more](https://themessenger.com/news/solar-eclipse-2024-total-viewing-best-cities-travel "open in a new window") are expected to travel to view the spectacle.

There are a handful of [highly scenic parks and remote places](https://www.space.com/10-best-scenic-locations-watch-total-solar-eclipse-2024-national-parks-state-parks "open in a new window") within or near the 115-mile wide path of totality that would offer epic viewing—think Lake Placid and Niagara Falls in New York or Hot Springs National Park in Arkansas—as well as both big cities and small towns [from Mexico to Maine](https://www.astronomy.com/observing/20-of-the-best-places-to-view-the-2024-great-north-american-eclipse/ "open in a new window").

However, many of these locations are [already reporting](https://www.wgrz.com/article/news/world/solar-eclipse/buffalo-business-first-solar-eclipse-hotels-wny-stays-money/71-f6b088aa-e6ce-4ab3-be6f-aec9f3573d43 "open in a new window") sold-out hotels and exorbitant per-night prices for what little lodging remains. Unless you have family or friends who live locally or the time and money to spend a few days on your eclipse-themed trip, you may need to consider some alternatives if you are set on seeing it.

## Choose a home base just outside the path of totality

The most impressive experience is obviously within the path of totality, but that's where everyone else is going to be. Consider traveling to areas that [will experience near-totality](https://science.nasa.gov/eclipses/future-eclipses/eclipse-2024/where-when/ "open in a new window") and are close enough to get within the path if conditions warrant the effort. Possibilities include Houston, Texas; Shreveport, Louisana; and Cincinnati, Ohio.

You only need to be within the 115-mile width of the path to experience totality—being right in the center will simply increase how long it lasts.

## Stay in a big city

While major cities within or on the edge of the path of totality are sure to be extremely crowded—and you may prefer the view from a more remote area—you likely have more options for lodging and easy viewing without needing to drive anywhere. Smaller cities and towns are likely to be even more overwhelmed with the influx of eclipse visitors.

You can also plan to sleep in the city but head out of town for the eclipse itself if you leave early and are willing to sit in some traffic.

## Have multiple options

Don't get too attached to one viewing spot, as you may find that it's difficult to access, too crowded, takes too much effort with congested roads, or is clouded over on eclipse day. Again, if you haven't already arranged a home base inside the path, map out a few options with interstate access (and try the one with the best weather forecast).

## Assess probable cloud cover

One reason Texas is expected to be such a popular ([and crowded](https://www.space.com/most-crowded-places-for-total-solar-eclipse-april-2024 "open in a new window")) destination for eclipse viewing is the relatively low likelihood of cloud cover during totality, with the best prospects of clear skies expected on the north side of the path [according to Eclipsophile](https://eclipsophile.com/2024tse/ "open in a new window"). While it's hard to predict weather patterns this far out, you can use forecasts in the days leading up to the event to determine how much effort is worthwhile to not be disappointed.

## Check for closures

Do research on your route before you go, as some roads may be closed for traffic control throughout eclipse day.

## Be prepared to drive—a lot

You'll just need to accept that you're likely to spend many hours in the car on eclipse day. Traffic is sure to be terrible, and if you're arriving and departing on April 8, you can expect it to take much longer than the same route on a regular day. Plus, you'll spend extra time if you have to drive around to find the best location. Try to arrive early and consider hanging around for a while after the eclipse ends.

## Pack the car with supplies

Per the above, make sure you have plenty of food, water, and entertainment with you for those long hours on the road. You don't want to get stuck in a standstill with no sustenance and nothing to do. For the eclipse itself, pack your eclipse glasses and a chair for hanging out at your viewing spot in the hours leading up to the event.
