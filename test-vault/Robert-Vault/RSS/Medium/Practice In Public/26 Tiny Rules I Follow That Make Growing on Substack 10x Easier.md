#### The game plan I use to grow by 300+ subs each month

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*iIjc_8ozlD_dB-mbxn0sFw.png)

Image by author

Most of you overcomplicate Substack.

You’re looking for:

-   algorithm cheat codes
-   the right time of day to post
-   and God knows what else.

You don’t need any of that crap if you want to grow your Substack by 300 subs a month like me.

Steal my rules:

### 1\. Don’t try to become a bestseller

The math is broken.

At 5% conversion and $8/month, you need 27,000 free subscribers to make $5,000/month.

That’s a rat race you’ll lose.

### 2\. Keep all your posts free

The more you paywall, the slower you grow.

Free posts get shared and spread. Paywalled posts die behind a wall where nobody can discover them. Hiding your content behind a paywall also makes you overthink every time you publish. “Should this be free or paid?” Screw that.

Keep everything free, build fast, and monetize on the backend with real products.

### 3\. Get people off Substack

Go and get a real autoresponder. Coz Substack ain’t one. Now you can start writing emails that sell stuff without [risking getting your account suspended](https://medium.com/write-a-catalyst/substack-banned-my-newsletter-without-warning-heres-what-you-should-know-9793aa8045fe).

### 4\. Sell this instead

The same 5% conversion on a $200 course with 1,000 subscribers makes you $10,000. You just made your goal with 26x fewer subscribers.

### 5\. Being nicheless is a scam

Most fellows here get no traction because they’re too broad. They confuse everyone: themselves, their readers, and the algorithm.

What works better?

Picking a specific audience with a specific problem and writing for them only.

### 6\. How many Notes to post every day

Notes are how you get discovered.

I like posting at least 3 times so I can recycle the winners, test, and see what works.

### 7\. What about long-form posts?

Long-form is your foundation.

But you don’t need to write War and Peace every other day. When you’re just getting started, 1–2 articles per week are enough.

You can also repurpose them on Medium (with no shame.)

### 8\. Write articles you can finish in one sitting

I write 400–700-word articles in 30–60 minutes. Short beats long when people are reading on their phones between meetings. It’s also faster for you to write.

### 9\. Get more juice out of your brain

I sit down once and write all my Notes in one session. Your brain needs warm-up time, so batch the work and free up the rest of your week.

### 10\. Recycle winning Notes like a b….d

Check your analytics to see which Notes drove subscribers.

Then rewrite the same idea in 5–10 different ways and milk it dry. I use the [StackSweller](https://stacksweller.com/?utm_source=mattgiaro&utm_medium=medium-article-26-tiny-rules) dashboard because it allows me to see all my metrics and sort the notes by the number of subs it brought in.

![Stacksweller dashboard with analytics — Image by author](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/0*Bk57Hg7neqtDsupE.png)

Stacksweller dashboard with analytics — Image by author

Then, I simply give these winners a new spin. Either by rewriting them manually, or with the AI backed into StackSweller.

### 11\. Post & Ghost is broken

Substack = Social media.

So you need to interact with other writers on this platform. That’s how the algorithm understands who you want to attract. And that’s how you get other humans to know you.

### 12\. Swap recommendations

Over the past 90 days, 43% of my subscribers came from recommendations.

![Substack dashboard with analytics — Image by author](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/0*opW_eCntMXnfyRv5.png)

Substack dashboard with analytics — Image by author

So make sure you interact, and then partner up with other creators in your niche.

### 13\. Look at your damn analytics

If you gauge the popularity of your content by vanity metrics… you’re screwed.

76% of my Notes don’t drive subscribers, and that’s fine.

I double down on the 24% that actually move the needle.

### 14\. Track which content brings buyers, not just eyeballs

Viral posts with 10,000 views mean nothing if they attract tourists. I optimize for subscribers who open emails and buy products.

And these subscribers need the right bait.

### 15\. Plug something in every single email

Every email I send with my autoresponder ends with a soft pitch to something people can pay for. If you shy away from selling, you’ll hate your email list and only keep attracting freebie seekers.

### 16\. Schedule Notes

Substack has no native scheduling. The problem is that I don’t want to manually copy/paste notes. I tried and failed.

So I’m using [StackSweller](https://stacksweller.com/?utm_source=mattgiaro&utm_medium=medium-article-26-tiny-rules) instead.

There are other tools out there but they all have a major flaw: they require you to keep your computer on like it’s 1999.

Since I’m using it, I’m on a pretty good streak:

### 17\. Save your ideas (but don’t organize them)

Capture ideas the moment they hit during walks, showers, drives. I talk out my brain farts with [Letterly](https://mattgiaro.com/letterly) or save them straight into [Bear](https://bear.app/).

### 18\. Don’t plan topics beyond this week

Content calendars look good until you wake up one morning and don’t feel inspired about what to write.

I brain dump ideas immediately, but I don’t organize them until I’m ready to write.

Planning 3+ months ahead kills motivation because your interests change and you’ll resent following a rigid calendar.

### 19\. Use templates for everything

I use templates for everything. They improve my first drafts with AI and allow me to write good content on the bad days.

### 20\. Use AI

The purists writers on this platform will hate me for this one.

But AI saves me hours of back pain when writing first drafts.

But I’m not using it like a dumbass. I prompt it with my style + ideas. I also edit everything myself.

### 21\. Never write and edit

Writing and editing at the same time is like trying to look right and left at the same time. Once you’ve written your first draft, let it breathe for a day. Your editing will be faster and sharper.

### 22\. Pay yourself first

I work on my Substack 45 minutes in the morning when my brain is sharp.

Then, I close my laptop and give the orange platform the finger.

### 23\. Find your one thing

I write down one must-do task every morning.

Some days it’s an article. Some days it’s batching notes. Some days it’s editing. If I only get that done, the day is a win.

Everything else is a bonus.

### 24\. Be obsessed & ignore the rest

I mute anyone who tries to distract me with “the next big platform.”

Substack is my focus, and I’m ignoring everything else until it stops working.

### 25\. Ship imperfect content

My newsletters have typos. Some ideas fall flat. Nobody cares because they’re reading emails on the toilet, not grading my grammar.

### 26\. Dumb down

Nobody clicks or shares content they don’t understand.

Write like you’re explaining to a friend over coffee. Make your headlines stupidly easy to understand. I save the poetry for the novel I’ll never write.
