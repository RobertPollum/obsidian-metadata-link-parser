### The Domesticated King

I can't think of a single person at my American evangelical church who'd insist they would *not* have stood with Martin Luther King Jr. What a good and righteous man he was!

Oddly, however, they would equally insist that today's social justice movements are divisive, unbiblical, or "woke."

Both claims, I'd say, cannot be true.

If we are honest, much of contemporary evangelical resistance to social justice activism rests on the comforting fiction that the civil rights movement was widely loved, orderly, and morally unambiguous — and that Martin Luther King Jr. was a gentle unifier rather than a destabilizing force. That version of King allows us to honor him without allowing his critique to interrogate us.

The King most Christians celebrate today is a domesticated one: a man frozen at the Lincoln Memorial, stripped of his later writings, political theology, and growing radicalism. We quote *"*I Have a Dream*"* while ignoring *Beyond Vietnam.* We praise his call to love while bracketing his insistence that love demands structural change. In doing so, we transform a prophet into a mascot.

But the historical record tells a different story.

> King was not merely controversial; he was widely despised. He was surveilled by the FBI, roundly denounced by Christian clergy, condemned by politicians, and portrayed as a dangerous agitator undermining social order.

By the end of his life, King could not book a hotel without federal surveillance or a church without protest. He was not simply opposed; he was hunted.

The King America honors today is not the King America rejected when he was alive — and that dissonance matters, especially for Christians who now invoke his name to oppose the very kinds of movements he embodied.

### "You're Being Divisive": Then and Now

Many people today — Christians included — are deeply troubled by state violence: family separations, mass incarceration, aggressive immigration enforcement. At the same time, they are uncomfortable with the tactics of activists who confront these realities. I have heard this expressed nostalgically: *Why can't protests be more like they were in the civil rights era?* More peaceful. More respectable. More docile.

This memory is historically delusional — and emotionally convenient.

Even the most peaceful civil rights tactics were overwhelmingly disapproved of when they occurred. Contemporaneous Gallup polling from the early 1960s tells a sobering story. In 1961, roughly 61 percent of Americans viewed the Freedom Riders unfavorably. In 1963, after the Birmingham Campaign, Gallup found that about 60 percent of Americans disapproved of the protests — even as they approved of racial equality in the abstract. Around 57 percent said sit-ins hurt the cause of integration. The March on Washington itself carried only about 36 percent approval.

Martin Luther King Jr. was not spared this rejection. By 1966, approximately 63 percent of Americans viewed him unfavorably. He was not widely regarded as a moral hero; he was seen as a force that was tearing at the moral fabric of Western civilization.

> What later generations praise as "peaceful protest" was experienced in real time as a terrifying force of disorder and extremism. To use today's lingo, MLK was the woke of his time.

This is why the charge of being "divisive" appears so consistently across history. It functions as a way to discipline resistance while leaving injustice intact. King named this clearly in *Letter from Birmingham Jail*: *"*For years now I have heard the word 'Wait!' … This 'Wait' has almost always meant 'Never.'" Appeals to patience, order, and unity have long served as mechanisms for maintaining unjust arrangements.

Christians often reached for Scripture to justify this posture. Romans 13 was invoked then, just as it is now, to warn against civil disobedience and protest. What went unexamined was how such readings sanctified the status quo while ignoring the biblical tradition of prophetic disruption.

Today's activists are judged by the same impossible standard: produce change without disruption; pursue justice without cost; expose injustice without making anyone uncomfortable. King failed that test in his time — and was condemned for it.

### What King Actually Believed (and Why It Terrified People)

One reason King is so easily whitewashed is that many Christians have never engaged his writings beyond a handful of quotations. His actual theology and political economy, stated plainly and repeatedly in his own words, remain deeply unsettling.

In *Where Do We Go from Here: Chaos or Community?* (1967), King insists that racism cannot be reduced to individual prejudice: "Racism is a philosophy based on a contempt for life. It is the arrogant assertion that one race is the center of value and object of devotion, before which other races must kneel in submission." Racism, for King, was embedded in structures, policies, and economic arrangements — not merely in individual hearts.

He names the interlocking nature of injustice with remarkable clarity: "We must see now that the evils of racism, economic exploitation, and militarism are all tied together." Because these evils are systemic, King argues, they require systemic remedies. This leads him to his most threatening conclusion: "**A radical redistribution of economic and political power" would be necessary if America were serious about justice.**

King's *Letter from Birmingham Jail* presses the point further. Justice delayed is justice denied not because individuals are impatient, but because unjust systems perpetuate harm: "Injustice anywhere is a threat to justice everywhere." He indicts the white moderate — not for cruelty, but for prioritizing order over justice, tranquility over transformation.

In sermons collected in *Strength to Love*, King develops a theology of social sin. Love, he insists, is not sentimental feeling but disciplined action aimed at dismantling oppression. And in *The Trumpet of Conscience*, particularly his Vietnam speech, he names America as "the greatest purveyor of violence in the world today," collapsing the sacred-secular divide many Christians used to justify silence.

Across these works, King articulates a consistent vision: racism is structural; responsibility is collective; repentance must be communal; and justice requires coordinated action. This vision terrified white moderates precisely because it implicated systems, not just attitudes, and demanded more than private virtue.

### In the Long Line of the Prophets: Sin, Liberation, and the Cost of Justice

King did not invent this way of seeing. He stood squarely within a long Christian tradition of resistance theology that names collective sin and calls for collective liberation.

The biblical story begins not with individual moral failure but with structural bondage. Moses confronts Pharaoh not over private immorality but over an economic system built on exploitation. The prophets repeatedly indict Israel for shared sin: unjust weights, crushed laborers, hollow worship divorced from justice. Jesus confronts religious systems that sanctified domination while neglecting mercy.

In Scripture, sin is often corporate, social, and institutional. Repentance, therefore, cannot be reduced to personal remorse; it must involve reordering communal life. King's critique of "cheap justice" echoes the prophets' condemnation of empty ritual: "Peace, peace," they warned, "where there is no peace."

Modern reform efforts often seek justice without disruption. Representation is substituted for transformation. Power is diversified while remaining intact. As Gerald Torres and Lani Guinier observe, systems discipline newcomers, requiring them to exercise power in familiar ways. The hierarchy remains; only its face changes.

Frederick Douglass named the deeper truth long before King: "Power concedes nothing without a demand." This is not merely a political insight; it is a theological one. Fallen human beings cling to advantage. Oppression is normalized. Resistance is recast as disorder. King understood this — and refused to accommodate it.

### Why They Killed Him — and Why This Still Matters

By the late 1960s, King was not drifting toward irrelevance; he was becoming more dangerous. His opposition to the Vietnam War, his focus on economic justice, and his leadership of the Poor People's Campaign sharpened his critique of American power at home and abroad. His unpopularity tracked precisely with this radicalization.

His assassination was not an aberration. It was the foreseeable outcome of sustained rejection. America did not kill a dreamer; it killed a threat to its social order.

If King were alive today, he would not be celebrated. He would be called Marxist. Anti-American. Divisive. And Christians would be among those urging him to be more patient, more orderly, more "biblical."

This is why we should be grateful for today's protesters. As we see repeatedly — through video after video — they are placing their bodies on the line for the moral health of the nation. They are doing what prophets have always done: refusing to make peace with injustice.

**Honoring Martin Luther King Jr. does not mean quoting him selectively or celebrating him safely. It means refusing the fantasy of cost-free justice.** MLK Day is not about nostalgia or comfort. It is about truth, courage, and unfinished work.
