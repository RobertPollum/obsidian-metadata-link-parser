# [Genesis: A Brilliant Mythology | by Peter Mansfield | in Backyard Church](https://freedium-mirror.cfd/https://medium.com/backyard-theology/genesis-a-brilliant-mythology-bb35d109c587?source=rss----a86f2e1baca9---4)

#### A journey through the scriptures

On the inside cover of my Bible, there is a scrawled date:

> 13/9/87

On that day, 13 September 1987, which was predictably a Sunday, I began to read the Bible, all of it. I started with "*In the beginning, God created the heavens and the earth*" and, 17 months later, I turned the final page and completed the task with Revelation chapter 22, verse 21. I was an older, and possibly wiser man.

Since then, I have read the Bible three or four times more, but I have not done so for many years. As such, I surprised myself when I woke up on 1 January 2026 and thought:

> "Wouldn't it be fun to read the whole Bible again? After all, my faith is in a very different place now and I wonder if I will see things differently? When I was young I was taught to apply everything to me. What does the story of Abraham have to say to ME? What can I learn about MYSELF from the story of Jacob wrestling with God? Back then, I naïvely assumed that the Bible had been written for the benefit of ME, a young man in the UK in the late 20th century. But I don't do that any more. Or not so much. I just want to read the stories and see what unfolds."

So my aim is to read the whole Bible and write 66 blogs, one for each book. I may even read the apocrypha for the first time. That'll be interesting.

I have no idea how long this will take. And, in truth, I accept that I will probably fail, defeated along the way by 2 Kings or Lamentations. But here goes with the first blog on Genesis.

#### In the beginning…

I love Genesis.

It is, quite simply, a cracking good read. When people say "*Oh, the Bible is boring*", they surely cannot have read Genesis. Genesis is many things, but it is not boring.

It has money, sex and power; stories of love, loss and deception; it has sacrifice and betrayal, sibling rivalries and marital indiscretions; it has unexpected virtue and shocking vice. It speaks about creation and destruction, purpose and disappointment, and through it all, bleeding like an unhealed wound, there is the story of God and humanity.

#### A very potted summary of Genesis

1.  God created the heavens and the earth.
2.  This included humans. However, humans ate from the Tree of the Knowledge of Good and Evil, and everything started to go downhill rapidly.
3.  The first two humans, Adam and Eve, were exiled. Their son Cain killed his brother Abel, and was then also exiled. In fact, he was exiled from his exile, which takes some doing.
4.  Everything became so awful that God sent a flood to kill everyone other than Noah and his family. However, this did not have the desired effect. Things remained awful.
5.  So God chose Abram/Abraham to be the father of a great nation to bless all peoples on earth. You certainly can't fault God for his optimism.
6.  Abraham had a son Isaac, who had a son Jacob, later renamed Israel. Israel had 12 sons, who were destined to father the 12 tribes of the nation of Israel.
7.  Jacob/Israel and his family were forced from their land in Canaan because of a famine, and ended up in Egypt, which sets the scene for the next book in the Bible, Exodus.

#### A long book of short stories

Many Bibles nowadays are written in very small print. As such, it is difficult to gain a sense of their length when compared to a modern-day novel.

In my NIV Bible, the 50 chapters of Genesis cover just over 51 pages, which doesn't sound like very much. However, in her book '*Reading Genesis*', Marilynn Robinson includes the King James version of Genesis and it fills 110 pages of a standard paperback. As such, Genesis is the length of a reasonably long novella.

It is, though, split into bite-sized chunks, many of which are no more than a page or two. As such, it feels more like a book of interlinked short stories, or a TV series that has separate episodes but with an overarching story arc. In some ways, Genesis was the first bingeable box set, its stories perhaps passed orally from generation to generation until it was eventually written down (possibly by Moses, possibly later, possibly during the period of the Exile) and then honed through regular editing.

And Genesis climaxes with the most perfectly crafted short story, detailing the rise, fall and rise again of a young man named Joseph, and his struggles within a family more dysfunctional than the Sopranos or the Roys. If you have never read the story of Joseph, please do, starting at Genesis chapter 37. There is surely no better tale told in history. If Netflix ever serialises the Bible story, this would be a brilliant Season One finale.

But there is one problem with it.

#### Joseph was an arse

As a child I was told the story many times. My church even put on a performance of *Joseph and His Amazing Technicolour Dreamcoat* and I knew all the lyrics to all the songs. And I was always told that Joseph was a great guy, someone who remained true to his values despite his family's provocations, and was raised up by God to achieve great things.

However, as I was reading it last week, what struck me was that Joseph started out as an arse, and largely continued to be an arse throughout his life.

1.  His first appearance in the story is as someone who, whilst sleeping, has a dream that some day he will be in authority over his brothers and that they, together with his father and mother, would bow down to him. (Interestingly, his mother never did bow to him because she had died by the time the brothers visited Joseph in Egypt.) Rather than keep these dreams to himself, as any non-arse would do, Joseph shared them with his family, and unsurprisingly he annoyed everyone to such an extent that his brothers wanted to kill him. Arse.
2.  Then, much later, when he was in charge of the grain supplies in Egypt, and was visited by his brothers, what did he do? He used his position of authority to play games with them. In my Sunday school, this was always presented as a reasonable thing for him to do, because Joseph was entitled to know whether his brothers had changed and were now trustworthy. But to me it feels far more like Joseph toying with them in the way a sadistic cat goads a terrified mouse. Why did he do it? Because he could. Arse.
3.  And, then, as the famine extended into a fifth, sixth and seventh year, Joseph used his bargaining power to take the Egyptian people's wealth, all of it, in return for grain. Then he took their livestock, and finally he placed the people themselves into bondage. "*Joseph reduced the people to servitude, from one end of Egypt to the other*" (Genesis 47:21). Arse.
4.  At all times, the brothers knew he was an arse. When their father died, their first thought was "*What if Joseph holds a grudge against us and pays us back for all the wrongs we did to him?*" As it turned out, Joseph did not do that, but the fact that the brothers thought he might was indicative of their opinion of him. When Joseph died, there is no sense that anyone grieved too much. Arse.

#### In fact, they were all arses

Perhaps it is unfair to describe Joseph as an arse. Perhaps it is better simply to say that he had flaws. And, in that respect, he was not alone. None of the 'heroes' in Genesis is presented as a perfect human being. They were all deeply fallible. Not one of them comes across as being particularly likeable.

1.  Noah cursed his son for the crime of accidentally seeing Noah naked, despite the fault lying entirely with Noah, who had stripped after a heavy 'wine tasting' evening.
2.  Abram/Abraham pretended his wife Sarai/Sarah was his sister and left her vulnerable to the sexual advances of others, simply because his desire for self-preservation was greater than his desire to protect her.
3.  Despite being treated as a chattel herself, Sarai/Sarah was cruel to her servant Hagar, who is initially described as being a 'maidservant', but is eventually dismissed as a 'slave'.
4.  Jacob's name means 'he deceives', and it was apt, because he fooled and tricked both his brother and his father. When he was in turn fooled and tricked by Laban, no-one was too upset. What goes around comes around.

And so on.

Having said that, I quite like Reuben. He seems like a nice fella. And it seems very harsh that his father Jacob, on his deathbed, prophesied to Reuben: "\[You are\] *turbulent as the waters, you will no longer excel*." Dear son, you will never be better than average.

Indeed, the only people who are presented with any morality are counter-intuitively the ones outside the familial tribe. For example, when it was discovered that Abram/Abraham had falsely presented Sarai/Sarah as his sister, first, Pharoah, and then Abimelech, criticised Abraham for his deception, saying: "*You have done things to me that should not be done*".

And then there is Isaac.

#### Isaac

I had always believed that Isaac was a key player in the story of Genesis. But in reality he acts solely as a bridge between the two main story arcs of Abraham and Jacob. If Genesis were about the Tudor monarchs, Isaac would be the forgettable bit in the middle between Henry VIII and Elizabeth I.

Not only that, but he is presented as a passive character. Things are constantly done to him, rather than being done by him:

1.  He is taken to a mountain by Abraham to be sacrificed.
2.  His wife, Rebekah, is chosen for him by a servant.
3.  When he is old, he is fooled by a combination of Rebekah and Jacob.

There is also some weird Freudian stuff going on with him. When he first meets Rebekah, he has sex with her in "*the tent of his mother Sarah*", and as a result he is "*comforted after his mother's death*". So, Rebekah seems to have been a mummy-replacement, which makes him a tad Norman Bates-ish.

Isaac is one of those people who is defined by his relationship to others, rather than as an individual in his own right. Who is Isaac? Oh, he is Abraham's son; or Jacob's father; or Rebekah's husband.

I feel sorry for Isaac.

#### Comedy gold

I have far less sympathy for Jacob. That said, he is involved in a genuinely funny scene in chapters 29 and 30, a scene that doubles as a sex comedy.

Jacob had two wives: Leah, whom he did not love (he had been tricked into marrying her); and Rachel, whom he did love. The added twist is that Rachel and Leah were sisters, and it becomes obvious the sibling rivalry ran very deep.

The story begins with Leah having four children with Jacob. As a result, Rachel, who had difficulty conceiving, became envious. She told Jacob to have sex with a surrogate, her maidservant Bilhah. Bilhah became pregnant and gave birth to a son. Bilhah then had a second child, whom Rachel named Naphtali, meaning 'my struggle', because "*I have had a great struggle with my sister, and I HAVE WON!*"

It all then kicks off.

In retaliation, Leah tells Jacob to have sex with *her* maidservant Zilpah, who then has two children with him. By now, Jacob is presented as having very little agency. He just does what his two wives, both strong women, tell him to do.

We now reach the climax of the story.

In purely mathematical terms, Leah was winning 6–2 against Rachel, six sons to two. Despite that, she remains very angry and, when Rachel innocently asks Leah one day for some mandrake roots, Leah explodes:

> "Wasn't it enough that you took away my husband? Will you take my son's mandrakes too?"

Of course, underlying this statement is the tragedy of an unloved wife, but how can you not smile at the absurdity of that line?

And then the story takes an even more absurd turn, because the mandrakes are traded for sex. In return for the roots, Rachel offers Jacob to Leah. As such, when Jacob returns that evening, and before he has even taken off his shoes, Leah tells him: "*You must sleep with me.*" Leah becomes pregnant again, and then again, and then again.

In this story, the comedy is generated by the rivalry between two siblings. And this is a constant theme throughout Genesis. Whether it is Cain and Abel, Isaac and Ishmael, Jacob and Esau, or, of course, Joseph and all eleven of his brothers, siblings are simply incapable of rubbing along together happily.

#### The role of women

But there is a darker side to the Leah and Rachel story. Whilst we watch the comedy play out between them, two other women become unwittingly caught up in their melodrama.

Two maidservants, Bilhah and Zilpah, are ordered to have sex with Jacob. To what extent was this consensual on their part? Probably not at all. Their role as sex slaves (also seen with Abraham and Hagar) is uncomfortably glossed over in the narrative.

Furthermore, despite the important roles of Sarah, Rebekah, Leah and Rachel in the story, it is evident throughout Genesis that women are treated as chattels. In chapter 19, Lot offers his two virgin daughters to a sex-crazed mob with the words, "*You can do what you like with them.*" Then, in chapter 34, Dinah, Jacob's daughter, is raped by Shechem, with deadly consequences, as Dinah's brothers take bloody revenge.

Of course, Genesis was written at a time when very different views on gender equality prevailed. This is just one reason why my earlier approach to scripture ("*what is in it for me?*") is so problematic. There are times when you have to read the Biblical stories in horror, and say, "*This is wrong*". And never is this more evident than with the stories that do not seem to fit the main narrative.

#### The odd stories

We have already mentioned the horrific story of Dinah's rape, which seems to be completely unnecessary, in the sense that it does not drive the story forwards. If it was removed from Genesis, the story arc would remain unaffected, so why is it there?

But it is not the only story like that. Indeed, you could argue that the whole of the sub-plot with Lot adds nothing to the story.

> Lot was Abram's nephew and they travelled together for a while before going their separate ways. Subsequently, when Lot was captured by an invading army, Abram heard about it, chased after the army, and heroically saved his nephew. After being thus liberated, Lot settled down in Sodom with his wife and two daughters. Two angels then warned him to flee the city, which he did with his family. As soon as they had departed, Sodom was destroyed with burning sulphur. Unsurprisingly, Lot's wife was tempted to look back on the devastation, but in so doing was transformed into a pillar of salt. Thereafter Lot and his two daughters (the two he had offered to the baying sex-mob) lived in a cave. The two daughters, now fearing they would remain childless, get their father drunk, have sex with him, and bear his children.

From beginning to end, the story of Lot is an unsavoury one, and it makes me wonder why it is there, because it is not needed.

Having said that, I can guess why it is there. After all, don't we all love salacious tales? Aren't these exactly the types of stories humans have shared for centuries? Shakespeare regularly included bawdy jokes and sub-plots within his plays. If a story is going to survive telling and re-telling, it has to grab the attention of its listeners and there is nothing that does that better than sex.

And there is, in addition, a wonderful twist at the very end of the story of Lot. The two sons drunkenly fathered by Lot eventually become the fathers of two of Israel's enemies, the Moabites and the Ammonites! You can imagine the laughter as this punchline was first revealed to an eager crowd of listeners around the campfire.

#### Brilliant mythology

A comparison can be made between Genesis and the gruesome tales of the Greek myths. Both use memorable stories to achieve the same thing, namely the creation of an origin story that explains and reaffirms a current identity. In effect, Genesis is telling the Jewish reader: "*this is who you are; this is your story*".

In arguing this, I am not saying that the stories in Genesis are not based on real events. They may have been, although I have no idea personally whether there was a real Eve, Nimrod, Abraham, Rebekah or Potiphar's wife, and I suspect no-one else does either. However, even if they were real, the stories are simplified for effect. They are mythologised.

For example, there is no infant mortality in Genesis. According to the narrative, Jacob has 13 children, all of whom survive into adulthood. Of course, that is possible, but it would have been statistically very unlikely. Far more likely is that Jacob had other children who did not survive infancy. Genesis ignores these, and other, painful realities of everyday life. The one exception is in chapter 35, when Rachel dies in childbirth.

And what do we make of the likes of Adam living 930 years and Methuselah dying just 31 years short of his 1,000th birthday? I have seen some writers who take this literally. Personally, I am more persuaded by the argument that the ages are examples of numerology, where numbers are used symbolically. Genesis chapter 5, which lists the ages of Adam's descendants, includes 28 numbers, but none of them end in 1, 3, 4, 6 or 8. This suggests that the numbers are not random, and have been chosen for a purpose, possibly a numerological one.

Of course, to describe Genesis as myth is not to dismiss its importance. Mythology is how we all make sense of our identities.

I remember virtually nothing of my own personal history. In my first 18 years, I experienced almost 6,600 days, but I can remember no more than a handful. My memory of myself is based on very little. I can tell you now that I started reading Genesis on 13 September 1987, because I wrote it down, but I can remember nothing else about that day. And the little I do remember from my childhood is likely to be inaccurate and incomplete. My own history is heavily mythologised, yet it forms the basis of who I am, and who I believe myself to be. Myth is a reminder that truth takes many forms, most of them incomplete.

It is said that Genesis (and other books of the Old Testament) are theologised narrative. They are stories told with a theological purpose. The authors and editors of Genesis were telling the story of Israel and its God by means of a series of brilliant vignettes, vivid and vital, starting with the Garden of Eden through to the story of Joseph.

These stories enabled the Jewish exiles in Babylon to remind themselves of what united them and made them different. In the stories of Genesis, they could gasp at Cain, laugh at Jacob, cry with Dinah, scorn the Amorites, dream of the unfulfilled promises to Abraham, and call upon their God to restore them.

Above all, though, they could read Genesis and think: "*These people may not be perfect; they may not even be admirable, but they are us.*"

In achieving that, we can safely say that Genesis is a work of genius.
