# [Love as a Practice, Creation as Motion, God as a Verb | by Becca ∞ | in Backyard Church](https://freedium-mirror.cfd/https://medium.com/backyard-theology/love-as-a-practice-creation-as-motion-god-as-a-verb-a26960bc4615?source=rss----a86f2e1baca9---4)

For much of human history, religion functioned less as belief in God and more as a container for relationships.

People gathered regularly. They shared time, stories, obligations, and limits. They were reminded — sometimes gently, sometimes forcefully — that their lives were bound to one another and to forces larger than individual preference. Whether belief was strong or not, the practice remained. There were rhythms of care, accountability, and return.

When older, land-based spiritual languages were stripped away — pagan rituals, seasonal cycles, embodied reverence — the container adapted. With the rise of industrial life, religion compressed itself into Sundays. You could work all week, produce all week, compete all week, and then pause. Even in its reduced form, it still held something essential: a shared reminder that humans are not only economic units. That care matters. That relationship matters.

What we have done since is mistake the container for the content — and then discard both.

Modern logic, correctly, dismantled the idea of God as a fixed noun: a supernatural authority who demands belief, obedience, and certainty. That conception has caused real harm. Dogma hardens. Certainty becomes violent. Identity turns sacred ideas into weapons. The critique was necessary.

But in removing the noun, we quietly removed the practice.

We argued about belief while abandoning shared devotion. We dismantled metaphysics and assumed ethics would survive on their own. We treated community as optional, care as spontaneous, meaning as privately generated. We forgot that love, if not practiced, weakens. We forgot that responsibility, if not shared, becomes unbearable. We forgot that humans are shaped less by what they believe than by what they repeatedly do together.

What is missing in our culture is not belief in God. What is missing is love and care as a collective practice.

When God is treated as a noun, belief becomes the gate. Who agrees? Who belongs? Who is right? When God is treated as a verb, the question shifts. Participation replaces belief. What matters is not what you assent to, but how you act. How you show up. How you tend what has been entrusted to you.

Love, in this frame, is not a feeling or a value. It is a practice. It is repetition under constraint. Repair instead of withdrawal. Attention instead of indifference. Responsibility instead of convenience. Love is not something you possess; it is something you enact — again and again, especially when it would be easier not to.

Creation, likewise, is not an outcome reserved for the gifted or inspired. It is movement. It is the refusal to remain frozen. It is the willingness to participate in bringing something new into being: a conversation, a repair, a structure that supports life rather than drains it. Creation as movement does not require certainty. It requires contact.

Seen this way, the argument between belief and disbelief collapses. We do not need agreement on metaphysics to agree on what must be done. We need shared responsibility. Shared devotion. Shared practices that make care real rather than aspirational.

The earth already understands this. Gardens do not grow because we believe in them. They grow because we tend them. Soil responds to rhythm, restraint, attention, and repair. So do people. So do communities.

"Love as practice, creation as movement" is not a spiritual compromise or a secular slogan. It is a reorientation. It flips the problem on its head. It says that what sustains humanity has never been belief alone, but participation. Not doctrine, but devotion enacted in daily life.

> This is not optional. Not if we want to remain human.

Without shared practices of care, we fracture into isolated individuals arguing over meanings while losing the conditions that make meaning possible. Without shared movement toward one another and toward the world we depend on, we decay — politically, psychologically, ecologically.

We do not need to resurrect old gods. We need to remember what they once organized us to do.

Love must be practiced. Creation must stay in motion. And community must be tended — not believed in, not argued over, but lived.

That is the work.
