# [The Most Misunderstood Word in the New Testament | by Dan Foster | in Backyard Church](https://freedium-mirror.cfd/https://medium.com/backyard-theology/the-most-misunderstood-word-in-the-new-testament-30f70dc631bb?source=rss----a86f2e1baca9---4)

Growing up in an evangelical church, I was exposed to endless speculation about who was "saved" and who was not "saved."

By salvation, I mean coming to a point in your life where you agreed with the core tenets of evangelical Christianity on an intellectual level and were willing to "ask Jesus into your heart" so you would be saved from eternal separation from God.

Hell, in other words.

So you can understand why, in the church's eyes, "getting saved" was pretty darn important. Certainly, the majority of the church's time and resources were put into trying to *"get people saved."*

I remember an occasion where a young man known in our community but not part of the church tragically died after coming off his motorbike. The pastor of my church got up and shared the news with the congregation and saw fit to add these words: *"Unfortunately, this young man was not saved. We can only hope in the split-second before his head hit the concrete that he accepted Jesus as his Lord and savior."*

Yes, salvation mattered so much that such grotesquely insensitive comments were met with nods of approval by the average pew-dweller.

But the thing is, the Greek word commonly translated as "salvation" or "saved" in the New Testament doesn't mean what most Christians think it does. In fact, when we take a look at what the Bible really says about "salvation," it puts an entirely different spin on things. And, in fact, it presents a much more hopeful and life-giving message than the usual, *"You're going to Hell unless you get saved."*

Buckle up for a brief lesson in Biblical interpretation that might just challenge your perspective, especially if you've been tethered to a system where salvation is synonymous with escaping the threat of Hell.

### Salvation 101

The Greek word frequently translated as "salvation" or "saved" in the New Testament is the word "soteria" (noun) or "sozo" (verb). When you understand its true meaning, it enables you to move beyond the narrow confines of a fiery pit.

Unlike the popular narrative that reduces salvation to a rescue mission from eternal damnation, Biblical scholars, like [William Barclay](https://www.preceptaustin.org/salvation-soteria_greek_word_study#wbnt), emphasize that in everyday Koine Greek, the language of the New Testament, salvation is often related to bodily health, wellness, or well-being.

That's right! Salvation or "Soteria" meant "wholeness," and being saved was more about healing, restoration, and liberation. You could picture a dutiful son inquiring about the "soteria" of his parents. He's not seeking intel on their eternal security but rather their general well-being and health.

Even more intriguing is the fact that the New Testament, predominantly written in Koine Greek, was rooted in the everyday language of the people. Koine Greek was used for pretty much everything from negotiating the terms and conditions of borrowing the neighbor's donkey to writing in your Mom's birthday card. Therefore, we have plenty of information on what certain words usually meant, thanks to these extra-Biblical sources as well. When we see the use of the word "Soteria" outside of a religious context, we discover most often, salvation was connected to wholeness, healing, and restoration.

Not hell.

When the Bible was being translated to English, translators did not use the common, everyday use of "Soteria" (wholeness and healing) but chose instead to use a less common meaning for "Soteria," referring to being rescued from disaster or released from prison.

Why did they choose that meaning?

It probably wasn't a secret plan to scare people into obedience. It was more likely the result of generations of theology that had already trained people to see God as a judge and salvation as a way out of trouble.

### Saving Salvation

Armed with our new insight about the word "Salvation," let's take a second look at some well-worn Bible verses. How about [John 3:17](https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=John+3%3A17&version=NIV):

*"For God did not send his Son into the world to condemn the world, but to save ('sozo') the world through him." (NIV)*

Usually, when we hear this verse, we think of sinners dangling over a fiery abyss, only to be snatched away to safety by Jesus Christ. However, this interpretation wasn't part of the Koine Greek worldview. In fact, belief in a literal "Hell" like this didn't come until much later.

Now that we understand that when people in Jesus's day spoke of salvation, the most common meaning was related to wholeness and healing, perhaps we can reasonably conclude that our salvation isn't about dodging hellfire but rather being rescued from the wreckage of our own lives, or being liberated from our own self-imposed prisons.

So, let's take a look at John 3:17 through a different lens and go with the most common meaning of "sozo" here:

*"For God did not send his Son into the world to condemn the world, but to* ***heal*** *('sozo') the world through him."*

Mind. Blown.

### The Last Word

When we limit salvation to a binary outcome, we overlook the myriad ways it unfolds in our lives. Salvation, in the typical evangelical understanding, is a moment in time. It's an event, a line you cross, a box you tick.

But "Salvation," in the true, Biblical sense of the word, is not merely a ticket to escape hell but a process — a continuous journey towards wholeness.

As William Barclay *[explains](https://www.preceptaustin.org/salvation-soteria_greek_word_study#wbnt)*, *"The New Testament knows nothing of an angry God who has to be pacified into forgiving men. It knows nothing of a God whose attitude to men has somehow to be changed from wrath to mercy. In the New Testament, the whole initiative of soteria is with God. God has not appointed us to wrath but to obtain "soteria" (wholeness, healing, freedom)"*

Therefore, the goal of the Christian message isn't to dangle someone over the fires of hell but to elevate them to the joys of heaven.

Now, that's what I call good news.
