# [What I No Longer Say to People Who Are Suffering (And Why) | by Dan Foster | in Backyard Church](https://freedium-mirror.cfd/https://medium.com/backyard-theology/what-i-no-longer-say-to-people-who-are-suffering-and-why-9fe269ec785f?source=rss----a86f2e1baca9---4)

For the first thirty years of my life, suffering was mostly theoretical.

I grew up in a stable home. Mum and Dad were steady, reliable, and kind. I genuinely cannot remember them ever having a proper fight. My dad was a pastor. My mum stayed home with us. There was food on the table, routine in the house, and a quiet sense that things would probably work out.

And they did.

I did well at school. I said the right prayers. I ticked the right boxes. I got married, bought a house, had kids, and kept climbing the ladder at work. From the outside, and mostly from the inside too, my life looked like the reward section of a well-lived faith.

So when people suffered, I had answers.

Not cruel ones. Not flippant ones. I had *good* answers. Pastoral answers. Bible-shaped answers. The kind you learn when pain is something you minister *to*, not something you have to carry home with you.

I didn't know it at the time, but most of what I said to suffering people wasn't about helping them endure pain. It was about protecting my belief that life made sense.

That belief didn't survive contact with real suffering.

### How Life Took the Training Wheels Off

What does someone who has never suffered have to say to the suffering? Not all that much, really.

For a long time, my life insulated me from that reality. Then it didn't.

The first crack came through burnout. For years, I worked in church ministry. It is a demanding profession and it slowly hollowed me out, and after being mistreated by the church I had given much of my adult life to, I resigned. I did not walk away from faith, but I did walk away from a system that was costing me more than it was giving back.

What I was not prepared for was what followed. The church told its people to cut me off.

People I had shared meals and history with disappeared. There were no difficult conversations and no attempts at repair. Just silence. In losing the role, I also lost community, belonging, and the comforting belief that love was unconditional.

That was my first real encounter with grief.

But over time, life began to stabilise. New work. A healthier rhythm. The sense that perhaps the worst was behind us and that things were slowly finding their place again.

Then my wife was diagnosed with cancer.

Not a scare or an early-stage interruption, but stage four. Incurable. The kind of news that collapses the future into a series of short horizons and forces you to relearn how to inhabit each day.

Whatever confidence I had left in my understanding of suffering did not survive that moment.

I do not believe these experiences were punishments or lessons handed down from God. But I do believe they changed the way I show up with people who are hurting. They stripped away the urge to explain, fix, or theologise pain too quickly.

They taught me restraint.

And that is why there are certain things I no longer say to people who are suffering. Here they are.

### The Things I No Longer Say

#### **"Everything happens for a reason."**

I understand why people say this. When something terrible happens, the mind reaches for meaning because meaning feels safer than randomness. A reason suggests order. It suggests that this isn't just chaos, that there is something here we might eventually understand.

I used to say it too.

I stopped because, in the middle of real suffering, this sentence does not land as comfort. It lands as pressure. It asks people to interpret their pain before they have even had time to survive it. It turns an experience that is already overwhelming into a problem that needs to be solved.

For someone who is hurting, "everything happens for a reason" can sound less like reassurance and more like a demand. A demand to find the lesson. A demand to grow. A demand to make the pain worthwhile.

I no longer believe suffering needs to justify itself.

Some things happen that are simply devastating. They do not arrive carrying insight. They do not need to be redeemed in the moment to be taken seriously.

So now I resist the urge to explain. I name what is real. I acknowledge how unfair and disorienting this can be. I let people feel what they feel without rushing them toward meaning.

If there is meaning to be found, it can wait.

#### "At Least…"

At least they caught it early. At least you still have your job. It could be worse.

This sentence usually arrives with good intentions. It is an attempt to offer perspective, to soften the blow, to remind someone that not everything has been lost.

I used to say it without thinking.

I stopped because comparison does not actually comfort people who are in pain. It shrinks their experience at the very moment they are trying to name it. Instead of being met where they are, they are subtly asked to move somewhere else, to feel better, to be grateful sooner than they are ready.

"At least" often sounds like "don't feel this so much."

Pain does not work that way. It does not respond well to being relativised. Knowing that someone else has it worse does not make this hurt less. It just makes people quieter about it.

So I no longer lead with silver linings. I do not rush people toward gratitude or resilience or optimism. Those things may come later, but they cannot be forced.

Sure, perspective has its place. It just rarely belongs at the beginning.

#### "You're So Strong."

This is usually meant as encouragement. It recognises endurance, steadiness, the way someone keeps showing up when life has given them more than they asked for.

I used to say it too.

I stopped because it often lands as a requirement rather than a kindness. Being told you are strong can quietly remove your permission to fall apart. It can turn survival into a performance, something you are expected to keep doing well.

Strength becomes the standard. Collapse becomes failure.

For people who are hurting, this can be isolating. It suggests that the most acceptable version of suffering is the one that is managed, controlled, and impressive from the outside.

But real pain just isn't like that. It has days when strength is simply not available.

So I no longer praise people for holding it together. I make room for the moments when they cannot. Sometimes the bravest thing a person can do is stop being strong long enough to be honest.

#### "Let Me Know If You Need Anything."

This sounds generous. It signals care without intrusion and leaves the door open.

I used to say it automatically.

I stopped because it quietly hands responsibility back to the person who is already overwhelmed. It assumes they have the clarity, energy, and emotional bandwidth to identify what they need and then ask for it.

Most people who are suffering do not.

They are tired. They are foggy. They are trying to keep their head above water. Turning need into a request can feel like another task they do not have the capacity for.

So instead of offering availability in the abstract, I try to be concrete. I offer something specific. "Can we bring you a meal?" or "Can we pick up the kids from school for you?" or "Would you like me to grab some groceries?"

Care works best when it is specific and does not require the hurting person to manage it.

#### "I Know Exactly How You Feel."

This sentence usually comes from a good place. Shared experience feels like a bridge, a way of saying you're not alone and I recognise this terrain.

The problem is that pain is not interchangeable.

Even when two people walk through similar events, the experience is shaped by different bodies, histories, relationships, and fears. What looks the same on the outside often feels entirely different on the inside.

Saying "I know exactly how you feel" can quietly shut a door. It replaces listening with assumption and turns attention away from the person in front of you and toward a story you already know.

Most people who are suffering do not need to be mirrored. They need to be met. So now I leave more room. I let people tell their story without rushing to connect it to my own. I resist the urge to prove empathy by comparison and choose curiosity instead.

#### "Stay Positive."

This one often sounds like encouragement. It carries the hope that attitude might soften the blow, that optimism might keep despair at bay.

But positivity, when it arrives too early, tells people which emotions are acceptable and which ones are not. Sadness is understandable for a moment. Anger should pass quickly. Fear needs to be managed. Grief should not linger too long or make others uncomfortable.

"Stay positive" rarely makes pain lighter. It just makes it lonelier.

People who are suffering already feel they are expected to cope well. They feel the pressure to reassure others, to keep the mood steady, to prove they are handling things. Forced positivity adds another layer of work to an already heavy load.

So I no longer ask people to feel better than they do.

I make room for the full emotional weather of what they are facing, including the parts that are messy, repetitive, and unresolved. I remind myself that despair is not a failure of mindset and that honesty is not the enemy of hope.

### What Actually Helped

When my wife was diagnosed with stage four cancer, the rug was pulled out from under our lives in a single conversation. Everything that had felt solid became provisional overnight. Plans, assumptions, even the language we used to talk about the future no longer fit.

In those early days, what helped was not insight or reassurance. It was not people trying to put the situation into perspective or offering words that were meant to steady us. What helped was far simpler and far quieter.

It helped when people stayed without trying to manage our emotions. It helped when they allowed the fear and shock to sit in the room without attempting to dilute it. It helped when no one rushed us toward meaning or resolution, and when our grief was not treated as a problem that needed to be improved.

What helped was the absence of pressure. The absence of explanation. The absence of expectation that we would handle this well.

I learned quickly that suffering does not ask to be understood. It asks to be accompanied. The people who mattered most were the ones who did not try to take the pain away, but who refused to leave us alone with it.

That experience changed me. It changed how I listen, how I speak, and how careful I am with words when someone is hurting. I no longer trust language that moves too quickly, and I am wary of anything that tries to make pain productive or meaningful before it has even been acknowledged.

In the end, what carried us was not someone saying the right thing. It was people being willing to remain present when there was nothing right to say.
