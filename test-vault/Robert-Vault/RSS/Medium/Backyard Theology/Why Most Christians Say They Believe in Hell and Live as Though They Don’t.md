# [Why Most Christians Say They Believe in Hell and Live as Though They Don't | by Dan Foster | in Backyard Church](https://freedium-mirror.cfd/https://medium.com/backyard-theology/why-most-christians-say-they-believe-in-hell-and-live-as-though-they-dont-b61428ca59a0?source=rss----a86f2e1baca9---4)

There I was, sitting in another small group Bible study when the topic of salvation came up. It seems Christians are always speculating who will be saved from the fires of Hell and who won't be, and how exactly God will decide.

I suppose that there is something in us that wants to rest assured that we don't end up on the wrong side of the ledger, and so people of faith — and maybe even people of no faith — hypothesize and ruminate on this issue.

In a [TV interview](https://www.news.com.au/entertainment/tv/current-affairs/do-you-believe-homosexuals-go-to-hell-australian-christian-lobby-boss-grilled-on-the-sunday-project/news-story/658fa138683b792a2715bf240425895a), Martin Isles, the former head of the Australian Christian Lobby, stated that the mainstream Christian belief is that all people are destined for Hell from birth unless they find salvation through Jesus. This was undoubtedly the belief that I was taught as fact growing up in the evangelical Church.

In fact, the prevailing strategy that many churches used to get people on board with Christianity was to sell it as a kind of fire insurance to protect you from ending up in the bowels of Hell. Simply pray this prayer, and hey presto! You're saved!

But is belief in Hell really 'mainstream,' as Martin Isles suggests? Remarkably, the answer is still a resounding "Yes!"

### Belief in Hell

It might be out of fashion to believe in Hell, but research indicates that most people in the US still hold to the idea that there will be some kind of posthumous place of eternal torture.

In fact, a survey from [Pew Research](https://www.pewresearch.org/religion/religious-landscape-study/belief-in-hell/) showed that almost 60% of people in the USA believe in Hell, with belief being highest amongst the Evangelical Christian demographic, at around 82%.

What is more, according to Pew Research, "Among Christians, 48 percent of Protestants and 56 percent of evangelicals believe [Christianity is the only path to eternal life](http://www.pewforum.org/2015/11/03/chapter-1-importance-of-religion-and-religious-beliefs/#paths-to-eternal-life)."

Yep, about half of all Evangelicals believe it's their way or the highway to Hell.

### They might say they believe in Hell, but…

Let me level with you. Despite the fact that most Christians say they believe in Hell, their behavior betrays an altogether different set of beliefs.

Why do I say this?

Well, belief in Hell has some clear-cut implications for behavior here on Earth. Let me explain:

If you believed that a friend or family member was in imminent danger of being killed, logic suggests that you would immediately and decisively act to prevent that from happening. I do not doubt that if my child were about to be hit by a bus, I would attempt to push them out of the way. In fact, I would probably even help a stranger who was about to be hit by a bus.

It would be instinctive.

Now, imagine that the imminent danger was not physical death but a kind of spiritual death that you believe has further-reaching consequences than any physical death — since this spiritual death you believe in lasts for all eternity. Physical death is over in an instant. But Hell is eternal — or at least that's what many Christians believe.

It makes sense that if I genuinely believed in this imminent and horrible spiritual death — Hell, that is — that I would do everything I could to "save people." I would actually shout it from the rooftops, stop strangers on the street, and be in my family's faces all the time — and unapologetically so.

Now, you might know Christians who do these kinds of things — God knows they are certainly out there. But the majority of Christians don't do this. That's a fact, and research supports it.

According to [Barna](https://www.barna.com/research/millennials-oppose-evangelism/), *"Almost all practicing Christians believe that part of their faith means being a witness about Jesus (ranging from 95% to 97% among all generational groups) and that the best thing that could ever happen to someone is for them to know Jesus (94% to 97%)."*

So, virtually all Christians believe that sharing their faith is one of the highest goals of Christianity, and 80% of them believe in Hell. So, you would expect a whole lot of evangelistic fervor, right? You would expect that pretty much all Christians would be sharing their faith because A) They believe that they have the greatest message of all and B) They believe that message is a life-or-death message with eternal consequences.

But, it turns out, 90% of Christians don't.

According to [Lifeway Research](https://research.lifeway.com/2019/04/23/evangelism-more-prayed-for-than-practiced-by-churchgoers/), less than half of all Christians have shared with someone in the past six months how to become a Christian (45%). Of those who have spoken to someone about becoming a Christian, most had only done so once or twice (24%). Only around 1 in 10 churchgoers (10%) average at least one evangelistic conversation a month.

### What are we to conclude from this?

Isn't it a bit puzzling that many Christians consider knowing Jesus the absolute best thing in the world, but then you notice that a lot of them don't really go out of their way to share that incredible news with others?

Or, perhaps a more pertinent question is why do most Christians believe that those who don't believe in Jesus are destined for a horrible, eternal spiritual death in the fires of Hell, but they don't bother to warn people?

There are a few potential conclusions that we could make from these statistical anomalies. Firstly, you could conclude that perhaps Christians don't care about their family and friends who are supposedly perishing. However, despite what you might think of Christians, there is no good reason to believe or suggest that they care any less about people than those who are not Christians.

So, that leads us to a second, more likely, conclusion. Perhaps, despite what they say they believe, even Christians struggle with the idea that an all-loving God would send people to Hell for all eternity, and that is why the task of "warning others" is statistically not a priority. Could it be that the mental gymnastics required to maintain the belief that God will punish most of humanity in eternal conscious torment is too great a leap for even most Christians?

It's easy enough to believe in God. I mean, most people have done that for most of human history.

But it's hard to believe in Hell.

You might hate those fundamentalist Christian types who are always harping on about Hell and trying to get people "saved." But, the fact of the matter is that these are the ***true believers*** — at least in the evangelical version of Christianity. In a strange way, their consistency exposes the tension many other Christians quietly live with.

### If it matters to Jesus…

Speaking of "true believers," one Twitter user, *TexanQueenBee*, recently explained that love, properly understood, means trying to spare someone from spending eternity in hell.

Source: [Twitter](https://twitter.com/TexanQueenB/status/1748061318945362351)

In her framework, love looks like telling people they are going to burn forever unless they listen to her.

I always thought love was something more concrete than that. Feeding the hungry. Giving the thirsty something to drink. Standing with those who are pushed to the margins. Apparently, I misunderstood.

Jokes aside, people like TexanQueenBee are often keen to present themselves as the ones who truly take the Bible seriously. Yet when pressed to show a clear biblical command instructing Christians to warn people about hell, things get surprisingly vague.

If the central goal of Christianity were to save people from hell, you would reasonably expect Jesus to have made that explicit.

But did he?

In the four biographies of Jesus — the Gospels — there are forty-nine direct commands attributed to him. Not once does Jesus instruct his followers to warn people about hell, to frighten them into belief, or to tell them they are destined for eternal punishment unless they comply.

What he does command is unmistakable: love your enemies. Do not judge. Treat others as you would want to be treated. Feed the hungry. Care for the poor. Make disciples.

But hell?

Jesus is silent.

He never tells his followers to save people from hell. He never commissions them to threaten people with it. He never suggests that warning others about eternal torment is an expression of love.

Make of that what you will.

### It's hard to believe in Hell

Personally, I think a big reason — perhaps the biggest reason — why many people don't share their Christian faith is that the Christian faith has become so enmeshed with the doctrines of sin, punishment, and hell that it's simply hard to speak about it as "Good News." In fact, to hold to the idea that it is "Good News" that anyone who does not receive Christ is destined for an eternal future in fiery torment requires a certain level of denial of what would appear to be common sense.

I have [written much](https://medium.com/backyard-theology/why-i-cant-believe-in-a-god-who-sends-people-to-hell-d21f7b111328) about my own personal struggles with the idea of Hell. But it seems that many Christians have similar struggles, though fewer are willing to admit it.

Therefore, perhaps the most compelling case against the existence of Hell is the fact that very few people — Christian or not — actually seemed to be bothered by it. Sure, Christians might say they believe in Hell, but their lack of action tells a different story.

We are simply not hardwired to believe in a loving God that punishes people for eternity.
