#### Coming Home to a Love and Heart-Based Faith

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*FZcoa0sDLPYHLXzaMmTqeg.jpeg)

Photo by [Gilbert Ng](https://unsplash.com/@zhekai_39?utm_source=unsplash&utm_medium=referral&utm_content=creditCopyText) on [Unsplash](https://unsplash.com/photos/a-black-and-yellow-sign-hanging-from-a-ceiling-WO2Pfxc-24U?utm_source=unsplash&utm_medium=referral&utm_content=creditCopyText)

Under a full moon at a Sunday school camp in Zambia, Africa, six twelve-year-olds sat around a campfire. After a ‘preachy’ pitch by our adult leader, we bowed our heads and prayed the sinners’ prayer. That ritual marked, in evangelical terms, the moment I became a Christian and secured my ticket to heaven.

Two boys, one path. Until my early twenties as a third year student at a Baptist seminary when Sandy, one of the campers, questioned the validity of “giving his heart to Jesus.” I tried with every ounce of my theology-filled brain to persuade him to come back to the fold. By then, devoid of dogma, he firmly challenged me, “Do you think that a 12-year-old’s prayer is a matter of life and death? There are other ways to know God.”

Thirty years later, I left my childhood evangelical community and embarked on a spiritual journey beyond my childhood church tradition. My burning question became “where now?”

I did not escape my evangelical Tribe as a _refugee_. No-one kicked me out. I arrived at my current spirituality as an _immigrant._ I consciously chose this path with a huge assist from Divine prompting.

My ‘where next’ came from a nagging attraction to a dogma-less and heart-generated spirituality. I’d also had hints of a love way beyond what I’d experienced in my former faith tradition.

A decade ago, Sandy died from a heart attack while running on a beach in Melbourne, Australia. Recently, while reflecting on our childhood years, I felt his presence in the room. I experienced a tingling from head to toes as I basked in his affirmation from the other side that I was on the right path.

I’d found the expulsive power of a new affection. Here are some of the life lessons learned as I stumbled my way towards a heart-bound faith.

#### Shift from the head to the heart

My longest faith journey has been from my head to my heart. (As one of my readers put it, “men want facts and avoid feelings.”)

My struggle with tribal beliefs and mores began to expose a heart-based longing to feel rather than think about my religion. I started searching for the experience Jesus articulated when he said, “Blessed are the pure in heart, for they shall see God” (Matt 5:8). At first, I could not put my finger on the longing in my heart to see or experience the Divine. In her book _Centering Prayer and Inner Awakening,_ Cynthia Bourgeault identifies this inner, or “still small,” voice as “God’s positioning system.” She describes it as our “magnetic center” or “organ of spiritual perception,” aligning us with God. Such direct inward spiritual experience eventually became a new direction for my faith.

I now identify a heart-based quest for what Rabbi Rachel Timoner, in an interview with Laura Esther Wolfson, described as “a strong belief that there is one God in the universe — one unifying presence that connects all life,” which I experience through dreams, dance, visions, ancestor encounters, and nature. All validated my spiritual journey beyond traditional doctrines.

The more I seek a direct connection to presence, the more I begin to distance myself from a culture that focuses on intellectual assent to a list of beliefs. Such cognitive rigidity or textbook belief often resulted in an immature and hateful religion. Amidst the swirl of dogma, my knife of doubt started to cut, one strand at a time, through a thick rope of tribal certitude.

#### Don’t give doubt a bad name

To me, doubt is not just a mix of anger and skepticism. It’s a vital part of a healthy faith journey, helping us feel understood and supported amidst our thorny questions.

Yes, I trampled on many toes when I refused to dance to the tune of tribal beliefs and mores.

As I explored my doubt, I confronted many of the tribe’s head-based arguments by asking inconvenient questions that empowered me to question dogma without fear.

_What if we humans are not inherently flawed but good to the bone?_

_What if questions about divinity take us into a pure mystery that defies simple rational formulas?_

_What if the Scriptures are not the “norm” for every aspect of life?_

Once, I expressed my curiosity about reincarnation to an evangelical pastor who abruptly declared, “I believe in the resurrection.” I saw his mind snap shut like a mousetrap, and his clear message was, “Don’t mess with my certainties!”

But those days of arm wrestling with my former tribe and inner self are fading into the past. However, I still question what I see and feel on this new leg of my path back home.

_Was the Divine speaking to me through those trees around our home? Is this a case of “I talk to the trees, that’s why they put me away?”_

_Am I pushing my luck in asking Spirit for a sign of its presence? When the answer comes (chills up and down my spine), I remind myself that these prompts are valid guides, not just confirmation bias, helping me trust my inner experiences._

_Did my dog communicate God’s unconditional love to me in ways different from my old Bible study sessions? At least, I felt my dog’s love._

I’ve concluded that skepticism is warranted. I don’t want to become like a pawnshop that takes anything in. I‘m disinclined to commit intellectual suicide. But I don’t want my healthy skepticism to become hardwired into cynicism which is a dead end spiritually.

As a life long seeker I‘m open to countless indicators of Presence, or as Rumi wrote, “What I’m seeking is seeking me.”

I’ve written again and again about ways ‘Spirit’ downloaded wisdom, gave me warnings, and assured me that I’m greatly loved. So my current inclination is to pull the loose threads from these promptings and see where they take me. Instead of becoming paranoid about jumping into a pool of heresy, I tell myself, “That’s interesting. Let’s see where this takes me.”

I’ve sure had some exciting and mysterious rides on the teeter-totter of faith.

#### Search for love beyond criticism

In a world riddled with judgment (fat shaming, gay bashing, heresy hunting), it’s balm for a wounded soul to experience the Divine (an ocean of consciousness) directly. In this space we feel unconditional love without judgment.

-   Since childhood, in my South African Evangelical background, I had been taught that the Communion ceremony is restricted to the chosen few who had “given their lives to Jesus.”
-   If you were a person of color, someone would quietly encourage you to attend the church’s afternoon service in Zulu, the local African language.
-   If you were gay, the Tribe would accuse you of living in sin and suggest “conversion” therapy.
-   If you were divorced, you’d be met with raised eyebrows, though your chances of acceptance improved if your spouse had left you (especially for adultery) rather than the other way around.

So instead of viewing “misbehaving” folks (by Tribal standards) as bad, evil, or back sliders, we view them with compassion as people with deep wounds and vast potential for good. It’s then that we are positioned to

-   Adopt the faith of my dog that loves me unconditionally.
-   Reflect on reports of life reviews by those who crossed the veil and returned to tell their story. Most all discovered that they are not sinners in the hands of an angry God, but bathed the absolute love of Presence.

We begin to grow in true spirituality when we realize that by virtue of us being in the image of the Divine, we are love at our very core.

#### Reflection

What will it take for former evangelicals like myself to get over being enslaved by a toxic religion, cross our equivalent of the river Jordan, move through the desert of residual pain, and enter the promised land of our true spiritual home? It’s there that we bask in the sunshine of an eternal love that declares “_This my child who once was lost, now is found?”_

#### Welcome home beautiful soul.
