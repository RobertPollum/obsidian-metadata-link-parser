Last year, my wife and 1 finally visited a British visitor attraction that had been on our "must visit" list for a few years, namely Beamish Open Air Museum.

This museum in County Durham vividly brings Northern English history to life with large-scale reconstructions. And travelling between the various historical eras on vintage trams and buses was a cool experience.

Exploring the recreated early 20th-century industrial village, I took the photo at the top of this post. There were even more rules on the original; I trimmed the image to keep it roughly square.

It's incredible to think that not so long ago — when my grandparents were alive — poor people were forced to live under these harsh rules.

Among the rules about waking times, meal etiquette ("No waste should be made at meal times. Everything will be eaten."), and prohibitions against swearing or fighting, there's one that really jumps out: "Those who neglect to attend church will go without their next meal."

Look at that sign again. It wasn't a suggestion; it was a non-negotiable command, enforced by threatening to withdraw the most basic human need — food! It reminds me of the harsh schoolmaster in Pink Floyd's 1984 hit, *Another Brick in the Wall, part 2*, where a Scottish voice intones, "If you don't eat your meat, you can't have your pudding…"

This sign is more than an interesting relic of a long-gone historic era. It reflects the power and widespread influence the Church wielded in UK society a hundred years ago.

Fast forward to 2026, and the idea of anyone being forced to attend church, let alone being starved for not doing so, is hard to imagine, given the complete change in the spiritual landscape.

I want to suggest that, to a large extent, the church has failed to recognise and respond to the seismic shifts of the past 100 years — a failure that lies at the heart of its struggle for relevance today. But before we examine this issue further, let's follow the example of visitors to Beamish and hop on a vintage bus, journeying together from the Victorian era to the present day.

#### Hankering after "the good old days"

Church attendance was once about social status, not just faith. The lower classes attended to show they were deserving of charity, while the middle and upper classes viewed it as a social duty that affirmed their place in society.

When I was a humble curate (vicar in training), I served in a parish with a church dating back to the Norman conquest. There were box pews for the common folk, but a special "Lord of the Manor's" pew in a completely separate area of the church, which not only physically separated them but also made it impossible for ordinary worshippers to see their elders and betters. Even the priest leading the service couldn't see the Lord and his family from his stall!

Of course, in those days, the local vicar or priest wasn't only there to lead Sunday worship and occasional offices (baptisms, weddings, and funerals). They were also key community leaders who influenced everything from children's education to village disputes. Regardless of their spiritual maturity, the priest or minister carried authority just because he (and it was only a "he" in those days) was running around with a dog collar and cassock.

Before coffee shops and smartphones were everywhere, the church was a centre for social life and neighbourhood solidarity. Baptisms, weddings, and funerals were major community events. Vicars who refused to baptise notable community members faced consequences. (trust me on this one, I've been there!)

#### The slow transformation of Christian ministry

When I first attended Bible College in the late 1970s, my vicar lent me his copy of a handbook he had received and read when he first started college, which covered the "nuts and bolts" of parish ministry.

The book, probably dating back to the 1950s, already felt outdated by the late 1970s. Its advice would seem almost comical in 2026, a time when the church was an expected, unyielding part of society with regular attendees.

The author described how people from the local community (not churchgoers) would phone to arrange an appointment with the vicar to discuss personal matters related to their spiritual and material well-being.

I was ordained in 1984, and those days were almost over. Rarely, if ever, did anyone from the local community phone me or come to see me for such advice. As the years passed, even church members relied on friends, counsellors, or other professionals for anything beyond specific church business.

Funeral and baptism families rarely expected to have conversations about spiritual matters, and I'm sure I'd have been told to mind my own business had I ever offered marriage guidance to couples who came to book a wedding.

The decline of this kind of church role in society didn't happen overnight. It happened over a long period, starting after the Second World War and gradually accelerating in our increasingly fragmented, social media-savvy age.

The decline of church authority occurred gradually after the Second World War and accelerated in our fragmented, social media-driven age.

As scientific knowledge grew and censuses routinely showed people becoming more secular, the idea of a single, all-encompassing religious authority has become impossible to justify. There was a time when society relied on the church for education, welfare, and healthcare. Those days are long gone, and those matters are now almost exclusively the purview of the state.

People now reject — and, frankly, resent — the church's interference in private lives, favouring individual choice, self-expression, and autonomy. Being told what to believe or where to go on Sundays conflicts with present-day norms.

I know, from skipping church a few times lately (yeah, I know), that our local high street, shopping malls, and sports clubs are rammed on Sunday mornings.

Most of these folks would no sooner think of dropping into their local church on a Sunday than I would think of visiting the local massage parlour or strip club…

For the avoidance of doubt, I can assure you I'm not…

And then we can't ignore "the elephant in the room" of the repeated sexual abuse scandals. It's no surprise that these have eroded confidence in the church, accelerating the trend of many moving away from it.

There are now far more appealing Sunday activities for most people. From what I've observed, people fill their Sundays with enjoyable, worthwhile pursuits — not sitting home with nothing to do.

#### When the Church is no longer an obligation

As we sit here and view the spiritual terrain in 2026, it is obvious that the church can no longer depend on social obligation, inherited tradition, or even the threat of a missed meal to fill its pews.

This change has forced churches to rethink their purpose and approach. It's resulted in some very positive changes, despite presenting some challenges.

People are less keen on being told what to think and more interested in exploring questions of meaning, purpose, and spirituality in a safe, non-judgmental space. So what would it look like for churches to become authentic, vulnerable, and deeply committed to the well-being of their local community, rather than merely upholding dogma?

My observation is that too much emphasis is placed on making the church "cool" and "funky" to attract people. The latest songs, the best worship musicians, throwing money at staging, lights and set dressing. Is that helping, or is it actually hindering?

Have you been to one of these churches that try to make themselves "relevant" by replacing the tried-and-true "opening hymn" with two or more "worship songs"? I'll be honest, my heart sinks when I'm invited to stand at the start of the Sunday gathering and be told, "We're going to sing three songs together."

No, thank you…

It doesn't feel genuine; rather, it seems (to me) like something we do because we heard somewhere that it's what growing, thriving churches do. (Spoiler alert: loads of churches that have that kind of worship aren't growing *or* thriving.)

Perhaps we should start trying something more interactive. Can we create a space where people can come along and share their doubts, joys and fears in a warm, welcoming and supportive community? Would that be more compelling than sitting in rows listening to an upfront "expert"?

Of course, this transformation isn't easy. Many traditional churches find it challenging to adapt, struggling with dwindling congregations, ageing buildings, and a longing for the "good old days." Believe me, I get it. I've got the T-shirt (and the scars) from contending with this in reluctant-to-change churches.

Without relying on coercion or tradition, the church can only thrive by facing change head-on — becoming more genuine, loving, service-driven, and focused on its core mission. This isn't an option in today's world. It's a necessity.

Ultimately, the future of the church lies in authentic invitation, real community, together with a pledge to make a meaningful difference. While the world has changed, the need for a positive, Jesus-centred spiritual community endures. The challenge — and the opportunity — is to meet this need with openness, integrity, and real interaction, offering something authentically relevant and life-giving in our time.

That workhouse rules sign from Beamish serves as an indication of how much the world has changed over the years. Today, the church operates in a society where it must earn its place rather than being "the done thing" on Sundays. And you know what, perhaps, just perhaps, that's a far more powerful and meaningful way to fulfil its purpose.
