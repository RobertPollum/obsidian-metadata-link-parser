# [How the Church Turned Paul Into a Moral Enforcer | by Evan LeBlanc | in Backyard Church](https://freedium-mirror.cfd/https://medium.com/backyard-theology/how-the-church-turned-paul-into-a-moral-enforcer-3dcc31338632?source=rss----a86f2e1baca9---4)

> "A grumpy, ornery old guy without many friends railing at his churches."

Above is a quote from a New Testament scholar about the way that Paul has been perceived by many Christians, especially those who are deconstructing. It creates this mental image of a curmudgeon that goes on long rants.

Paul does get mad at people and maybe he does have a mental health struggle or two (especially in 2 Corinthians, for example), but I don't know that this picture really captures the complexity of this author.

It is also possible that some of this really comes from the attitude of people who try to use Paul as a source for the kind of certainty that they tend to prefer in their religious expressions within churches.

Paul tends to be viewed as a preacher in many modern American churches, as well as someone who wasn't afraid to use harsh verbiage to get across points and often called people to task for their inconsistencies.

This perception makes sense for one major reason.

**Misunderstanding Paul**

The main reason that this perception makes sense is that many tend to want to return back to the teachings of Jesus, as Paul's teaching has tended to dominate a lot of conservative American churches.

As a result, it feels like going back to the teachings of Jesus is getting back towards a more pure and less judgmental version of the faith that does not look towards others who are outside the church in quite the same way.

Yes, the teachings of Paul have often been used to justify judging the lifestyle of others in the American church, clearly differentiating between those who live in a way that shows they obey God and those who don't.

Is this fair to Paul? Let's take an example:

> "I wrote to you in my letter not to associate with sexually immoral persons, not at all meaning the sexually immoral of this world, or the greedy and swindlers, or idolaters, since you would then need to go out of the world. But now I am writing to you not to associate with anyone who bears the name of brother or sister who is sexually immoral or greedy or an idolater, reviler, drunkard, or swindler. Do not even eat with such a one. For what have I to do with judging those outside? Are you not judges of those who are inside? God will judge those outside. "Drive out the wicked person from among you." 1 Corinthians 5:9–13 NRSVue

I have definitely heard that passage preached to encourage people to stay far away from sin and live a clean lifestyle and obey God instead of doing whatever you want and living a wild life (whatever that means).

However, the passage actually has more to do with those who are within the church that you should not associate with. It additionally says that God will judge those who are outside of the church.

This is something that a lot of churches don't seem to be picking up. They absolutely judge those who are outside the church and they specifically judge those who have left the church and the reasons why they leave.

The ironic thing for many who find themselves deconstructing and are sometimes outside of church circles is that they might not have paid as much attention to the language about outsiders as they do now that the rhetoric is applied to them.

It is definitely disappointing that churches operate this way, but it is a way of ensuring that a church can stay together in the event that a group of people from within the church leaves (especially if it is due to conflict).

Another important point here is that, even though this is not discussed in this way in church, most of the reasons to judge someone relate not to an inward attitude (even if they sound that way), but to behavior that will bring the church some level of public shame and embarrassment.

You may be wondering why I made this distinction. The reason for me boils down to the tendency that some people have to think that they can judge the inward attitudes and intentions of people due to their own personal discernment.

That is far from a guarantee. If we are honest with ourselves, most of us have had the experience of thinking that someone in the church is a certain way, only to find out later that we had misjudged them (whether positively or negatively).

In any case, suffice it to say that the attitude of judgment that was typically ascribed to Paul may be more of a product of the people who are teaching about Paul than it might be an "authentic" teaching of his.

I will give the caveat that the "authentic" perspective of Paul is not something that is completely recoverable in my view, even in light of our best attempts to figure out all we can possibly know about him.

If Paul wasn't a judgmental and angry curmudgeon, then what was he? Well, one aspect of Paul that I think that the church may be missing, I think that he is calling for the liberation of all creation.

**Liberation of Creation**

> "For the creation waits with eager longing for the revealing of the children of God, for the creation was subjected to futility, not of its own will, but by the will of the one who subjected it, in hope that the creation itself will be set free from its enslavement to decay and will obtain the freedom of the glory of the children of God. We know that the whole creation has been groaning together as it suffers together the pains of labor." Romans 8:19–22 NRSVue

For all of the talk in Romans 8 about the effect that the resurrection will have on humans, as well as the normal Pauline themes of justification and the role of the Spirit, as well as a popular favorite verse ("nothing can separate us from the love of God"), one could easily forget about what is said above about the world (creation).

Paul here speaks about creation as an agent with its own will and also longing for freedom from what he calls "enslavement to decay". This language is pretty striking and more than a bit interesting.

The world here, is suffering and it is oppressed to an extent, unable to stop the changes that are occurring to it, changes that amount to its decay and eventual potential replacement (depending on what you think Paul's view of heaven is, but that is a topic for another article).

Is creation, then, going to be liberated at the eschaton in the same way that believers are going to be resurrected? That seems to be a fair and interesting question to ask given this particular passage.

It is fair enough to claim that Paul had a more expansive view of who was going to be restored to God in the end than we do and did not view creation as entirely about humanity in the way we do today.

But it is also true that in places Paul seems to hint at the idea that all people will eventually be liberated and they will inherit eternal life because that is the kind of God that they serve and this is the effect of Jesus's death.

It is fair to say that this liberative move is limited by most churches, who do not want salvation to be available to everyone, but only to those who formally commit to a given process of salvation.

This process, in its American evangelical form, usually involves a confession, saying a prayer, accepting Jesus into one's heart and (depending on the denomination) getting baptized subsequently.

> It is important, though, to note that this does not represent the entire viewpoint of the writers of the New Testament nor the early church fathers on this particular topic. They hoped (possibly even more than some do today) that God would eventually save everyone.

The form of religion that allows for all people to be involved is a difficult sell for the faithful in conservative American evangelical churches, however, because the "real Christians" are not supposed to stray from the straight and narrow path at all.

**Conclusion**

In summary, Paul might be a bit less inclined towards judgment than we might think. Our tendency is to write our own preferences onto a Biblical character and then run with it before considering whether it is reasonable to do so.

In Paul's case, I think we should try to be fair to him when it comes to issues like judgment if possible. The conservative evangelical American church has perhaps shown itself to be harsher than even the roughest statements of Paul could hope to be.

It is entirely understandable that people do not want to hear any more Paul in their deconstruction process after the way that he was taught to them in their respective church traditions.

I wrote a review of the New Testament scholar Nijay Gupta's book on Paul and love on Medium and I do think that book represented a chance for people to view Paul from a different perspective.

I understand that Paul is very important for people; he was arguably the most influential character in the history of the early movement. However, every statement that he makes does not have to be taken dead seriously.

We make run the risk of taking Paul more seriously than he takes himself.
