# [Living in Motion | by Becca ∞ | in Backyard Church](https://freedium-mirror.cfd/https://medium.com/backyard-theology/living-in-motion-9b51496eda41?source=rss----a86f2e1baca9---4)

When I say God is a verb, I'm not being poetic. I'm being precise.

Consider forgiveness. Not the performance of it, not the strained smile, the dutiful reconciliation but the actual phenomenon. Forgiveness only makes sense if time remains open. If the past were truly fixed, sealed, immutable in the way we've been taught to imagine it, then forgiveness would be irrational. There would be nothing to forgive because there would be no room for revision. The harm would be permanent, the relationship determined, the story finished.

But we forgive anyway. And when we do, something shifts in the structure of time itself.

This is not mystical thinking. It's observational. Forgiveness operates as though meaning is not exhausted by a single temporal pass. As though what happened can be re-contextualized, re-metabolized, re-entered from a different angle. The event remains — the body remembers, the facts don't change — but its gravitational pull on the present loosens. That's what forgiveness does: it reopens a process where noun-based thinking insists on closure.

God-as-noun stabilizes narrative*. "This is what it is. This is what happened. This is who you are."* It provides certainty, fixed points, endings you can point to and say, *there, it's done*. And there's a comfort in that. A noun-based theology tells you the story is already written; you just have to find your place in it, accept your role, and submit to the plot.

But lived reality contradicts this daily.

Every parent knows it. Every person who has watched someone they love change in ways they couldn't have predicted knows it. Every dancer mid-movement, every therapist in session, every moment you've felt time stretch or compress or double back on itself, these experiences don't fit inside noun-logic. They require a God who moves.

God is *loving*, not love. The difference matters.

Love-as-noun can be possessed, achieved, lost, defended. It becomes an object you hold or fail to hold. Loving-as-verb is directional, relational, ongoing. It implies movement between, toward, through. It doesn't ask "do you have it?" but "are you doing it?" And doing implies choice, implies presence, implies that the outcome isn't predetermined.

This is why forgiveness becomes thinkable. Because if God is loving, if divinity itself is motion rather than monument then time must be structured to allow for that motion. Not infinite in some abstract mystical sense, but non-finite in its meaning-making capacity. The past doesn't disappear, but neither does it have the final word.

Our stories are built around God-as-noun, and it shows. Western theology especially has favored the fixed point: God as Father, King, Judge, Law. These are stabilizing metaphors. They provide hierarchy, order, authority you can appeal to when chaos threatens. They make the universe legible by making it static.

But static systems deny what the body knows.

Stand still long enough and you'll start to sway. That's not weakness — that's physiology. The body doesn't do stasis. It oscillates, adjusts, responds. Blood moves. Breath cycles. Cells replace themselves. Even bone, which feels permanent, is constantly remodeling. To be alive is to be in motion.

So when theology demands you believe in a God who doesn't move, who decided everything before time began, who exists outside the process of becoming, your body resists. Not consciously necessarily. But in the gap between what you're told to believe and what you experience as true, something fractures. You learn not to trust your own knowledge. You learn that the body lies, that feeling is unreliable, that truth must come from outside yourself because your insides are too chaotic, too changeable, too much in motion.

This is not accidental. It's structural. A noun-based God produces subjects who distrust their own aliveness.

***The Discipline of Dance***

I learned God-as-verb in dance before I had language for it.

Ballet is unforgiving about timing. You can't think your way through a pirouette. The preparation matters — the plié, the spotting, the core engagement — but once you're turning, thought interrupts. The body has to know the sequence so completely that consciousness can step back and let the verb complete itself.

That's not mindlessness. It's a different order of intelligence. Somatic knowing. Pre-linguistic information that arrives as sensation, as rhythm, as the felt sense of rightness or wrongness in your alignment. The mind learns the form so the body can inhabit the motion. And in that motion, there's no separation between doing and being. You don't *have* balance; you *are* balancing. Continuous present tense. Verbs all the way down.

When I say God is movement, I mean it the way a dancer means it. Not that God moves *through* space like an object traveling, but that God *is* the moving itself. The relationship between preparation and execution. The conversation between gravity and intention. The way meaning forms in the doing, not before it.

This is why these moments don't last, and why that's not a problem.

A dance that never ended would stop being a dance. It would become a position, a freeze, a death. The impermanence is not a flaw in the experience — it's the structure that makes the experience possible. Relationship that never changes becomes control. Presence that tries to hold itself permanently becomes performance. Even love, if you tried to make it static, would calcify into obligation.

Verbs don't last. They occur.

And in their occurring, they create the conditions for the next occurrence. This is not loss. This is rhythm.

What I'm describing has political implications, though I didn't start there.

If God is a verb — if divinity is fundamentally relational, temporal, processual — then authority cannot be located in fixed texts, unchanging laws, or institutional hierarchies that claim to speak for the divine once and for all. Authority would have to be something else. Not something you possess but something you enact. Not power over but directionality within.

Love as divine authority, in this frame, means love as the force that orients action. Not love as achievement, not love as feeling, but loving as the practice that shapes how you move through time and relation. You can't own it. You can't arrive at it and stop. You can only keep doing it, or stop doing it, and notice what changes.

This terrifies systems that need you to believe the ending is already written.

Because if time is still open — if meaning can be revised, if forgiveness is structurally possible, if your participation actually matters — then you have responsibility. Not guilt. Responsibility. The ability to respond. The requirement to respond. You can't outsource that to a God who already decided, or a text that already answered, or an algorithm that already optimized.

You have to be present. You have to choose. You have to move.

And that means you could choose wrongly. You could miss the timing. You could fall out of rhythm. The verb-universe doesn't promise safety. It promises consequence. Which is another way of saying it promises that what you do matters.

This is hope, but not the cheap kind.

Not the hope that everything will work out, that God has a plan and you just have to trust it. That's noun-hope. That's the hope that stabilizes by removing your agency.

Verb-hope is harder. It's the hope that the future is not yet written. That process remains open. That forgiveness is possible because time is not finished with us. That your participation — however small, however local, however limited — contributes to what comes next.

That's terrifying. And it's also the only hope worth having.

So when I say God is a verb, I'm saying: divinity is what happens when we participate in motion that exceeds us without erasing us. When we feel ourselves held by something larger — not dominated, not absorbed, but *accompanied*. When we move in rhythm with forces we didn't create and can't control, but can learn to dance with.

This is not belief. Belief is what you hold in your head, the proposition you agree to. This is relationship. Relationship with time, with consequence, with the body's knowledge, with the fact that meaning forms in doing and cannot be fully pre-determined.

You cannot have a relationship with a noun. You can only worship it, obey it, rebel against it, or ignore it.

But a verb? A verb invites you in. It says: participate. Move with me. Let's see what becomes possible.

That's the God I know. Not because I believe it, but because I've lived it.

In the moments when forgiveness arrived unbidden. In the pirouette that completed itself. In the conversation that changed everything. In the love that kept moving even when I wanted it to be still.

God is loving. Present tense. Continuous action.

And if that's true, then time is not finished with any of us yet.
