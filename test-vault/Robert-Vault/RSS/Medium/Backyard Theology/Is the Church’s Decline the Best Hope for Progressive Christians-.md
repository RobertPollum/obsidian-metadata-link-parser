# [Is the Church's Decline the Best Hope for Progressive Christians? | by Eric Sentell | in Backyard Church](https://freedium-mirror.cfd/https://medium.com/backyard-theology/is-the-churchs-decline-the-best-hope-for-progressive-christians-71d5a650591a?source=rss----a86f2e1baca9---4)

Political scientist Ryan Burge wrote about ["the God Gap"](https://www.graphsaboutreligion.com/p/how-big-the-god-gap-really-is-on?) recently for his Substack, "[Graphs About Religion](https://www.graphsaboutreligion.com/)," and the implications of his analysis really dishearten this progressive Christian.

The best hope for progressive politics seems to be the continued decline of church attendance.

The God Gap refers to the tendency for Christians to be Republicans and for non-Christians to be Democrats. Burge uses a recent survey of 68,000 college students to look at the God Gap among young people. He breaks the data down by church attendance, political affiliation, and religion.

For simplicity's sake, I'm going to zoom in on church attendance and political affiliation in the survey.

Among those college students who attend religious services "never," 33% self-describe as "very liberal," 32% identify as "somewhat liberal," and 12% claim "slightly liberal." Three of four "never-attenders" lean left. The numbers decrease for people who attend once a year or a few times per year, but they follow the same leftward trend.

For students who attend religious services "several times a week," 20% say they're "very conservative," 29% self-describe as "somewhat conservative," and 12% identify as "slightly conservative," for a total of 61% of frequent worship attenders claiming conservative politics. Again, the numbers decrease for weekly attenders but follow the same rightward trend.

Burge concludes:

> For those who don't go to church that much, they are 20 points more likely to be a liberal Democrat than a conservative Republican. Among the weekly attenders, it's exactly the opposite.

Here's the kicker. Two-thirds of the sample were "never-attenders." Only 3% attended multiple religious services each week.

As a political progressive, I suppose I should hope for the continued decline of U.S. church attendance. Heck, I should actively keep college kids from ever becoming adults who go to church.

[According to Gallup](https://news.gallup.com/poll/642548/church-attendance-declined-religious-groups.aspx), in 2004, 42% of Americans attended a religious service weekly. By 2024, only 30% attended religious services each week or almost every week. The decline has been driven by what Burge calls "the rise of the nones," those who do not claim any religious affiliation.

If they were a U.S. denomination, the "nones" would be the largest by far.

Facing the "rise of the nones," aging memberships, and rising expenses, some people predict that [100,000 churches will close](https://medium.com/backyard-theology/100-000-u-s-churches-may-close-by-2050-what-can-be-done-11242ca0df6d) within the next couple decades. Entire mainline protestant denominations may disappear as their aging members pass away without being replaced by younger members.

As a progressive Christian, I can't help but lament what's lost when a church closes. Churches are often important centers of their communities, especially in rural areas. Their ministries serve the poor and needy, provide connection for the elderly and young, and show the love of Christ.

Talk about a dilemma. More "never-attenders" likely means more liberals, which means electing more progressive candidates, passing more progressive ballot initiatives, and so on. But it also means fewer churches in communities that could benefit from them.

Viewed from the other direction, more church attendance means more conservatives who vote for candidates with whom I profoundly disagree, who implement policies that demonstrably make everyone's lives worse and disproportionately affect certain groups.

So, I lament.

I lament that Democrats cannot attract more religious people and that religion cannot attract more liberals.

I lament that Dems fail to address any issue of religious significance with a compelling vision. Although they champion the social safety net, they shy away from talking about any religious motivation for caring for the poor.

I lament that churches fail to disciple people into the kind of Christians who take seriously the Bible's thousands of verses about caring for the poor, the marginalized, and the vulnerable.

I lament that both these trends were decades in the making and will require decades to change for the better, if that's even possible.

But I also hope.

For Jesus' Kingdom is not of this world.

For Jesus' Kingdom cannot be defeated by political trends.

For as long as Christians strive to follow Jesus, they will discover new and vital ways to bring God's Kingdom to earth as it is in heaven.

The American church may look very different in a few decades, but it will still exist in some form. There may be fewer churches but more houses full of believers gathering together. There may be fewer denominations but more networks of people with differing beliefs yet the same love for God and neighbor.

There will be fewer people in the pews partly because some of them will find community online, such as The Backyard Church group on Mighty Networks. I've witnessed community and growth there comparable to my church.

There will certainly be more "nones" who exhibit as much fruit of the spirit in their day-to-day lives as any weekly-attending Christian.

For the church is not a building; it is a people.
