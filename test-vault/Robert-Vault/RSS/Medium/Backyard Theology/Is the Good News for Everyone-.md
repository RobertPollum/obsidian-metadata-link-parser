# [Is the Good News for Everyone? | by Evan LeBlanc | in Backyard Church](https://freedium-mirror.cfd/https://medium.com/backyard-theology/is-the-good-news-for-everyone-b2606dd44bd8?source=rss----a86f2e1baca9---4)

"For a group that used a church sign that said, 'all are welcome', we were pretty darn exclusive."

Above is a quote from a New Testament scholar. She is acknowledging a phenomenon that is familiar for many American Christians, the reality of an exclusive approach towards outsiders while simultaneously adhering to an "evangelistic" approach.

I became familiar with this idea in my first semester of attending seminary. There were many students who enjoyed making groups based on theological distinctions usually relating to questions of eschatology or the role of the Holy Spirit or baptism.

It was so bad on one of the campuses that I attended that I eventually decided that I would try the main campus instead. It was a good decision because the conversations did not end, but they didn't pervade the language classes that I was interested in as much.

This is not to say that I am not interested in theology, but I do begin to lose interest in certain questions and I definitely have no interest in determining who is or isn't "saved" based on their theological beliefs.

I did see the appeal of this, though, because the more specific that one's belief becomes, the more convinced that you are that you have found the "true" meaning of a passage and that others are simply incorrect in their understanding.

From there, it tends to evolve into the importance of having "true" theology and then the need to make sure that others understand what the "real" meaning of these things are, so as to help them along in their faith.

Combine that with exclusivistic language relating to the truth of God's word and the absolute nature of one's theological opinions and you are almost at the point where you need to be to introduce the idea of salvation.

You then come to talk about how "true Christians" value this or that theological idea, or that "real Christians" would never dare believe this if they knew what has become so clear, obvious and evident.

Finally, you think that your opinion lines up perfectly with what God actually said and what God really wants from us and voila, you have gotten to the point where you consider your viewpoint to be essential for salvation.

**Insider Language**

The concept of insider language is not too hard to imagine for many Christians, who use a particular term that I will not recreate here (it sounds rather insensitive, if people know what I mean) to refer to language that doesn't make sense outside of Christian circles.

Suffice it to say that there are many ways that a Christian can signal to another that they are part of a particular group and let them know that they acknowledge the tradition from which they emerged.

When I started going to church (not having been raised in the church), I realized that my inability to recognize this language left me not understanding what was being said around me and what these words or terms meant.

I found that it was fruitless to even bother asking about some of this terminology because of the fact that I did try and it really did not lead to much clarity on the topic, making me think that such clarity was not available.

For example, people used to say all the time in the first church I attended that God would overwhelm us with his grace. I asked a leader in the church what they meant by that and why they wanted God to overwhelm them.

They said that grace was "like this thing that God gives us but it's really not about a thing, it's really about an attitude and also it is an intense feeling that comes to you in your chest and possibly also your stomach."

Unfortunately, one has to be discerning about who you ask questions to in a church. Otherwise, you end up with answers like this, which exist somewhere in an existential plain between a nothing burger and a word salad.

Have you ever had the misfortune of asking the wrong person in church a question like "what does it mean to be saved" or "what does forgiveness mean?" It ends in quite the disaster of confused speech.

> You could not be blamed if you simply concluded, as I did, that the system was not made for you and that you will never quite understand how to fit into the church community that insists on putting up such barriers.

I eventually reflected on these barriers and I asked what they were for. What was their purpose and how could someone try to overcome these barriers and feel like they belonged? One creative way I thought of was by realizing that the good news could be for me.

**The Good News**

One could hardly be blamed for thinking it was possible that the "good news" wasn't altogether that, well, good. After all, the gospel in American churches is often characterized as the idea that we are all sinners and God in his mercy saved us from hell.

On the other hand, some go more in the direction of the fact that the good news is actually just the saving part, which I guess is kind of like someone asking you whether you want the good news or the bad news first. You eventually get both.

However, I, like many before me, had watched the Charlie Brown Christmas special. Unlike the recent "war on Christmas" and "keep Christ in Christmas" campaigns, it presents a version of the good news that I hardly ever heard in church:

> "Now in that same region there were shepherds living in the fields, keeping watch over their flock by night. Then an angel of the Lord stood before them, and the glory of the Lord shone around them, and they were terrified. But the angel said to them, "Do not be afraid, for see, I am bringing you good news of great joy for all the people: to you is born this day in the city of David a Savior, who is the Messiah, the Lord. This will be a sign for you: you will find a child wrapped in bands of cloth and lying in a manger." And suddenly there was with the angel a multitude of the heavenly host, praising God and saying,

> "Glory to God in the highest heaven, and on earth peace among those whom he favors!"

This represents a vision of the good news that we might not have heard in church, namely, that the good news is for "all the people" and not just those who have shown themselves to be especially faithful Christians.

> It is good news for everyone in the world, not just those who believe in God. And it is something that doesn't fit into the normal definition of the good news. It shows that, at least for this writer, the good news really benefits everyone.

This is a theme in Luke/Acts, where all people can benefit from the coming of Jesus and the ministry of Jesus, in which people are treated justly, and the roles of power are reversed in the world of the time.

Does that fit with an exclusive message? It doesn't seem so to me.

Now, I will note that there are absolutely places in both the Hebrew Bible and the New Testament where an exclusive group is thought to be the true followers of God over and against others.

But this is where it is up to Christians to choose whether they want to see that kind of exclusivism embodied or not. Do you want to judge the actions and hearts of other people to determine whether or not they are "real Christians"?

Do you think that you should worry about whether other Christians might think that you are real Christians? It is something that everyone should decide for themselves. Not every iteration of Christianity is equally appealing to everyone.

For me, I didn't like the idea of deciding whether or not someone's Christianity is authentic. Yet, many reading my articles may think that I am saying that everyone who does not agree with me is mistaken or isn't a real Christian.

It seems to me that I found more significance in the idea that the good news is for everyone than from leaning into the exclusivism of certain forms of Christianity. Some of you may agree with me on that point.

However, some of it may have naturally resulted from knowing how it feels to have others tell you that you are not a real Christian just because you do not embody the beliefs that they have on certain topics.

In this time of rising Christian nationalism in the United States, do we think it is a good idea to keep an exclusive outlook on things? After all, there are more than enough Christians willing to exclude everyone from liberals to immigrants to people from the LGBTQIA+ community.

The list of people who are not welcome is very long. Yet, the signs outside many of the houses of worship in the country insist that all are welcome. If all are welcome, then the good news is for everyone. If the good news is not for all people, then all are not welcome.

**Conclusion**

The decision of whether the good news is for everyone remains up to Christians. There are of course good arguments on both sides of the question and the line between the sides is anything but clear.

We don't often understand how much of these decisions that we make as Christians are dictated by us and come down to our own way of seeing things. Who receives the good news is one of these issues.

Many of the readers who responded to my previous articles about the LGBTQIA+ community spoke of the importance of choosing love. The expansion of the good news to all people is perhaps a similar choice of love.

One doubts whether the shepherds in Luke 2 made a formal confession of their faith. Yet they are included in what many would term the most significant and discussed birth in the history of the world.

Should they be excluded on the basis of their possibly shaky faiths? Or maybe we should inquire about their theology, or whether or not their life is in line with where it needs to be in order to welcome Jesus?

To me, it is possible that the shepherds understand something about life that I may never come to. One need only to reach out to someone who is quite different than us to see the value of a life that is potentially beautiful.
