# [Grief Is Not Linear; It Comes In Waves | by Paddy Murphy | in Backyard Church](https://freedium-mirror.cfd/https://medium.com/backyard-theology/grief-is-not-linear-it-comes-in-waves-18775ecefcad?source=rss----a86f2e1baca9---4)

#### A flurry of funerals

Over the last 13 months, I've been to a funeral roughly every seven weeks. To some people, that'll sound like boatloads. To others, they will be thinking, what's this paddy complaining about?

I'm not complaining, though. I'm Irish, after all, and we have a saying: *better a good funeral than a bad wedding*.

There are a thousand thousand things to be grateful for if you were raised in Ireland, and one of them is our relationship with death and funerals. It doesn't necessarily make it any easier when someone close to you dies, but because of the way we treat funerals back home, we make an effort to give the dead a proper send-off, and to celebrate the life of the departed as best we can.

That can mean swapping funny stories, remembering the person's great successes, even their failures, and what was learned from them. The church service is always sad, and the burial brings the finality of it down on your head like an ogham-etched standing stone.

But the wake, whether it's held in the family home or a funeral home, offers real solace to the family. And then there is usually that final send-off back at the house, or in a hotel function room, or in the local bar, where the person is remembered in full voice.

It's still sad, but often happy, too. Pure bittersweet. A fleeting community of people drawn together because they all had this one person in common. Just like a wedding, though a wedding concerns two people, of course, that coming together to show support to the family, and to both mourn and celebrate the life of the departed, is, to me at least, why funerals matter.

After all the funerals I attended last year, starting with my grandmother back in Ireland, I hoped that 2026 wouldn't bring so much death close to me. But we were only a few snowy January days in when a pal texted to say that another friend's father had passed away.

Now, I will say this clearly: I'm not fishing for sympathy. I've written plenty over the years about the whole hero/victim routine, and that's not what I'm doing here. The vast majority of the funerals I attended were not for people related to me. They were neighbors or friends' family members, apart from my grandmother and, hardest of all, my father-in-law.

But you do what you do, and you attend. I had never met that man, but you go to show support for your pal. It's just what you do. Driving the two hours up with two other pals was part of it too, catching up, and learning that one of the lads would be popping two cherries: his first Catholic funeral and his first Polish funeral.

#### Death, distance, and the calls that change a day

Being an immigrant who left Ireland's windy, wet shores about 20 years ago, I cannot always make it home for funerals.

My dad's best pal died last year after a short illness, one he did his best to keep to himself, though he let the cat out of the bag when he told my auld lad, the last time they met in person, that he was not long for the world.

And then last night, I had a heartbreaking call from my mother. She had just been told that her best friend has about three weeks left to live.

To state the bloody obvious, none of us are getting out of here alive.

There are a few truths in life. Donald Trump is a scumbag, Vladimir Putin is a maniac, and another is that you are going to pop your clogs. Hopefully, it won't be any time soon, but it's not like you get much of a vote in the cosmic matter. And most of us will be touched by death this year, one way or another.

We are pattern-making, meaning-making machines, and when someone dies, we do what humans have always done. We rally. We gather. We stand awkwardly at the back of a church hall with a lukewarm cup of milky tea, saying the same strange sentences people have been saying for centuries, because the sentences aren't really the point. Our presence is.

We show up for the people closest to the one who is gone. And we find whatever ways we can to honor the dead, and to celebrate the lives that went before ours.

We owe them that much. We keep living as best we can because it's what they would want for us, and because staying alive isn't just biology; it's loyalty to our loved ones and ourselves.

#### Amor fati and the grim mathematics of being alive

When I'm chewing on the big existential questions, I usually end up back in Germany with its most famous philosopher and his famously theatrical mustache, Friedrich Nietzsche, and his idea of *amor fati*, learning to love fate.

On the surface, it sounds fairly simple enough: accept what happens, and when it happens, welcome it. Easier said than done, obviously.

It's one thing to accept your fate when the sun is shining, and the bees and the birds are riding like bejaysus, and your auld knees are working as designed, and the bills have all been paid. It's another thing to accept it when the phone rings at night and your stomach knows something awful has happened before your ears do.

What always fascinated me is how Nietzsche tried to force the question into the body, not as a motivational quote, but as a test you can't wriggle out of.

He had this thought experiment called *the eternal recurrence*: what if you had to live your life again, exactly as it was, every detail, every embarrassment, every small kindness, every pointless workplace Zoom meeting, every cheap laugh, every bad date, every good date, and every grief, over and over and over and over, forever?

If that were true, what would you do with your life as it is?

You would either curse existence itself, or you would learn, slowly and painfully, to say yes to every single bit of it. Because if you cannot say yes, then you are stuck in permanent revolt against reality, and, y'know what, lads and lasses, reality doesn't lose those fights.

Aye, I know Nietzsche lost his marbles in the end, but that wasn't because he was a time-traveling mystic with a portal gun for hopping dimensions; the poor lad had syphilis. But the point stands: *the eternal recurrence* is a way of asking whether you can affirm your life without demanding it be different first.

And that is where *amor fati* comes in. Love your fate, not because it's glorious and full of vibes, man, but because it's yours. Love it enough to stop fantasizing about a version of life where everything is all shiny and edited, all cleaned up, and sensible, and everything is fair, and you're getting all your carnal desires on a plate as well.

Imagine your life at its worst, then, and then imagine it intensified, a hundredfold, a thousandfold. Now imagine the opposite, the best possible version of your life, where everything goes your way, the world applauds on cue, and every single traffic light you approach turns green.

Amor fati is the decision to honor the life you have, and to stop living as if you are owed some other one.

And if some version of you out there does have it better, well, you'd bloody well want him to enjoy it, surely? Let him have his good luck. You have your own crooked road, your own handful of days, and you can either spit on them or bless them.

#### The five stages, and the problem with tidy grief

This is where people usually drag out that old line that time heals all wounds, and then they start talking about the "five stages of grief."

The model is most famously linked to psychiatrist Elisabeth Kübler-Ross, who introduced it in 1969 in her book *On Death and Dying*. Here is the part that gets missed in the way the idea is shared online: Kübler-Ross was originally describing patterns she observed in people facing their own death, not handing the rest of us a neat little shopping list that every bereaved person must check off in order of completion.

Over the years, the stages got turned into a kind of grief conveyor belt. Denial, then anger, then bargaining, then depression, then acceptance, as if the human mind were as efficient as *the* German efficiency factory, and sorrow a product moving along a belt toward its cardboard packaging for postage.

More recent grief research and clinical work tend to push back against the idea that these reactions unfold in a single, reliable order for everyone.

People can move through them in loops, skip some entirely, feel three of them before their porridge in the morning, and then get hit by a fresh wave of longing while standing in LIDL, because they saw a brand of biscuits their Mum used to enjoy dipping in her tea.

As a counselor, I try to be plain about this: there's no timetable you're failing to follow. There's no correct pace. There's no gold star for "doing grief properly."

What people need is room. Time, aye, but also permission. Permission to be inconsistent. Permission to have a good day and then feel guilty about having a good day. Permission to laugh at something and then feel the pain return like a tide.

And people also need to know this, because nobody says it clearly enough: grief resurfaces because love doesn't vanish on command. Anniversaries arrive unannounced, songs come on the radio, a smell in a shop opens an old door in the mind, and you are right back there with them for a moment.

The goal is never to eliminate grief. The goal is to shoulder it. Therapy can help with that by helping you meet the waves with a steadier footing, and by helping you build a life that can hold sorrow without being ruled by it.

Above all, it has to be said, again and again, until it sinks in: we all grieve differently, because we all loved differently, and we all lost differently.

What matters isn't whether your grief looks tidy from the outside. What matters is to trudge on and endeavour to keep living as best we can, so that we can honor those who've gone before us, and indeed honor the fact that we're still here.
