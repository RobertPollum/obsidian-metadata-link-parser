# [What Jesus Actually Did When People Walked Away | by Dan Foster | in Backyard Church](https://freedium-mirror.cfd/https://medium.com/backyard-theology/what-jesus-actually-did-when-people-walked-away-72a7f0b52672?source=rss----a86f2e1baca9---4)

It turns out you can be completely sincere and still do something deeply wrong**.**

I learned this as a young adult in church, back when church was not just something I attended but the centre of my life. It shaped my friendships, my routines, and my understanding of what faithfulness looked like. Staying connected mattered, and continuing to show up was treated as a moral good. Absence was never neutral.

At the time, that logic felt responsible. Even loving. I believed that commitment to church was a reliable measure of spiritual health, and that walking away was a sign of drift, decline, or quiet rebellion. I was earnest, idealistic, and convinced that good intentions were enough to justify most actions taken in God's name.

There was a man in our church who stopped coming.

He did not make a scene or explain himself. He simply disappeared. No one reached out to ask how he was doing. No one checked in just to be his friend. We did not invite him for coffee or remember his birthday. What we noticed was his absence, and that absence made us anxious.

That anxiety quickly turned into concern, and concern into certainty. We decided he was backsliding. We prayed for his soul. We talked about what needed to be done. Eventually, we came up with a plan.

After church one Sunday night, a group of us drove to his house. Not one or two people, but a crowd. I remember later realising there must have been around fifteen of us. At the time, the number did not strike us as strange. It felt serious. It felt faithful.

We described what we were doing as loving him.

What that actually meant was standing in his space and telling him how disappointed we were that he was no longer attending church. We asked why he had pulled away. We reminded him what he was walking from. We framed our concern as care, our pressure as responsibility, and our intrusion as obedience to God. We genuinely believed we were doing the right thing.

He never came back.

Years later, I can see what I could not then. What we called love was driven by fear. Fear of losing him, fear of what his leaving said about us, and fear that if people were allowed to walk away without consequence, the whole system might begin to unravel.

At the time, we never stopped to ask a more uncomfortable question. Not whether he was wrong for leaving, but whether we were wrong in how we responded.

We assumed that if someone walked away, the faithful thing to do was to chase them, confront them, and try to pull them back in. It never occurred to us to ask whether that instinct actually reflected the way Jesus treated people who chose to leave.

Maybe we should have paid closer attention to what Jesus did when people walked away.

### When People Walked Away from Jesus

Perhaps we could look at a few moments in the Gospels when people walked away from Jesus, starting with the one that should have terrified anyone concerned with momentum, numbers, or commitment.

In John 6, Jesus is at the height of his popularity. He has just fed thousands. The crowd is energised, attentive, and ready to follow. This is the moment when, by modern standards, you protect the brand, clarify the message, and make it easier to stay.

Instead, Jesus does the opposite.

He speaks in a way that is deliberately difficult to accept. He refuses to soften what he is saying or translate it into something more palatable. The result is not confusion, but offence. The text is blunt: many of his disciples turn back and no longer follow him.

These are not critics or enemies. They are followers.

What Jesus does next is striking, not because it is dramatic, but because it is restrained. He does not chase after them. He does not explain himself again in hopes of winning them back. He does not warn them about what they will lose if they walk away.

He turns to the Twelve and asks a simple, disarming question: do you also want to leave?

It is not a test. It is not a threat. It is a genuine question that makes room for an honest answer. Jesus seems willing to accept the possibility that even those closest to him might choose to go.

This was not an isolated moment.

Another well known example appears in the encounter with the rich young ruler. The man comes to Jesus sincerely, asking what he must do to inherit eternal life. The Gospel tells us that Jesus looks at him and loves him. Then he names the one thing that stands in the way of freedom and invites the man to let it go.

The man walks away sad.

And Jesus lets him.

There is no revised offer, no negotiated compromise, no suggestion that he could start smaller or come back later. Jesus does not chase him down to reassure him that he can keep both his wealth and his discipleship. The cost is named honestly, and the choice is left with the person standing in front of him.

Again and again, this pattern repeats.

Some are offended and leave. Some are challenged and walk away quietly. Some decide that the cost is too high. And each time, Jesus resists the instinct to force a decision, protect his numbers, or secure loyalty through pressure.

What stands out is not indifference, but trust. Jesus seems to trust that what is real does not need to be enforced, and that love loses its meaning the moment it becomes compulsory.

Which makes our fear when people leave all the more revealing.

### When a Community Becomes a System

Seen alongside the Gospels, the problem is not that churches care when people leave. Care is human. The problem begins when a living community slowly becomes a system, and systems do not respond to departure the way people do.

A system needs stability. It needs predictability. It needs its story about itself to remain intact. Once a church reaches that stage, someone leaving is no longer just a relational loss. It becomes a disruption. And disruptions demand explanation.

At that point, leaving is rarely interpreted as neutral information. It is almost always interpreted as failure in the person who left. Their faith must have weakened. Their commitment must have slipped. Something must be wrong with them. The possibility that the environment itself might be part of the story is quietly ruled out, not out of malice, but out of self preservation.

This is not unique to churches.

The same thing happens in workplaces when a long term employee resigns and is labelled disloyal rather than listened to. It happens in political movements when dissent is framed as betrayal. It happens in families when someone steps back and is accused of being selfish rather than honest. Once a group becomes a system, it protects itself by locating the problem in the individual.

Churches are simply not immune to this dynamic.

Over time, faithfulness becomes confused with compliance. Presence becomes evidence of health. Staying is rewarded. Leaving is pathologised. A person does not discern a need for space. They drift. They do not step away thoughtfully. They backslide. Language does the work of protection long before anyone makes a conscious decision to defend the institution.

Once this framework is in place, the response becomes predictable. The goal is no longer understanding, but recovery. The question is not "What might this be telling us?" but "How do we get them back?" Intervention feels loving because the system has already decided who is at fault.

This is how care turns into control without anyone intending it to.

The irony is that this instinct does not grow out of faithlessness, but out of anxiety. Not always named, not always acknowledged, but present nonetheless. Anxiety that if people are allowed to leave without consequence, the story the system tells about itself might begin to crack.

And so the system responds the only way systems know how. It tightens. It explains. It intervenes. In doing so, it often loses sight of the very thing it was meant to protect: people.

### What Faithfulness Looks Like When We Stop Chasing

Once you see this pattern in Jesus, it becomes difficult to unsee it.

Jesus does not appear anxious about people leaving. He does not interpret departure as failure, betrayal, or proof that something has gone wrong. He allows people to walk away without being shamed, followed, or pressured into staying. Not because he is indifferent, but because whatever he is offering cannot be sustained by force.

That realisation has slowly undone what I once believed faithfulness required.

I was taught, implicitly and explicitly, that faithfulness meant holding people close. It meant keeping them connected, maintaining their participation, and intervening when they drifted. Jesus seems to suggest something far quieter and far more demanding. Faithfulness means trusting people with their own agency, even when their choices unsettle us. It means allowing space without immediately interpreting it as danger. It means resisting the urge to protect the system at the expense of the person.

This does not mean people never return. Sometimes they do. But in the Gospels, return is never the result of pressure or confrontation. It comes, when it comes at all, from freedom. From a choice that remains genuinely available because it was never coerced.

I still think about the man who never came back to our church. I do not know what might have happened if someone had simply reached out as a friend. If we had asked how he was doing instead of why he was failing. If we had trusted him enough to let him go without commentary or consequence.

What I do know is this. Whatever we offered him that night did not resemble the way Jesus treated people who chose to leave. And that realisation has reshaped how I now understand faithfulness.

Faithfulness is not proven by who stays. It is revealed by how we treat people when they leave.
