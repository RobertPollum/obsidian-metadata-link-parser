# [The Emotion That Christianity Relies on More Than Love | by Dan Foster | in Backyard Church](https://freedium-mirror.cfd/https://medium.com/backyard-theology/the-emotion-that-christianity-relies-on-more-than-love-775ca93579a0?source=rss----a86f2e1baca9---4)

I was sitting in church when a grown man broke from his seat and ran toward the front like his life depended on it.

It was the end of the service, the moment when the music softened and began to repeat itself, creating space for what churches call an altar call. If you did not grow up in church, an altar call is the point where people are invited to leave their seats and walk to the front of the room as a public response to God. It is framed as a decision, a crossing of an invisible line. You do not have to go forward, but everyone knows what it means if you do.

The room had gone quiet in that familiar way. Heads bowed. Eyes closed. The music sitting just underneath the preacher's voice. He spoke about sin, about the weight people carry without realising it, about how exhausting it is to try to hold your life together.

Then the man stood up.

He did not hesitate. He did not look around. He ran. Full pelt. Straight down the centre aisle toward the front of the church, as if slowing down might cost him something.

People opened their eyes. Heads lifted. The sound of his shoes hitting the floor cut through the music, and the atmosphere shifted instantly. Everyone was watching now. The musicians kept playing. The preacher kept speaking. But whatever private moment people thought they were having had ended.

This was the kind of moment churches quietly hope for.

The man reached the front, breathing hard, shoulders hunched. The pastor met him there and guided him toward the altar. The rest of us stayed in our seats, watching with that strange mix of reverence and curiosity that comes with moments like this.

The service wrapped up not long after. One final song. A few tears. Some hugs. Then we went home. The following week, the man was invited back to the front to share his testimony.

He looked like someone else entirely.

He stood beside the pastor with a microphone in his hand, smiling in a way that did not seem forced or performative. He spoke about how lost he had been. About how heavy everything had felt. About the guilt he had been carrying for years without knowing where to put it.

Then he tried to describe what had happened during the altar call. He struggled for words, like people often do when something inside them shifts faster than language can keep up. Eventually, he settled on an image.

He said it felt like a huge weight had been lifted off his shoulders. You could see it on him. In the way he stood. In the way he spoke. In the way his face stayed open as he looked out over the room.

Sitting there as a teenager, watching him, I remember wanting whatever it was he had found. And I say that knowing I had already seen plenty of altar calls by then. Plenty of emotional moments. Plenty of stories that burned bright for a week or two before fading back into ordinary life.

This one did not feel like that.

Something real had happened.

I just did not yet understand what it was.

### What Was Really Happening in That Moment

It took me years to understand what I was actually witnessing that morning. At the time, I assumed the change I saw in that man was the result of love. Love for God, gratitude for forgiveness, joy at being accepted. That was the framework I had been given, and it seemed to fit what I was seeing on the surface.

But looking back now, something else stands out. The man did not leave that altar call with new insight, new beliefs, or a clearer moral vision. What changed was not how he understood God, but how he felt in his own body. The internal pressure he had been carrying was suddenly gone.

By the time an altar call happens, that pressure is already well established. Church has spent years shaping people's sense of themselves by naming sin, failure, and falling short as central features of the human condition. Whether this is communicated softly or forcefully, it creates a persistent background weight. People come to believe, often without realising it, that something about them is fundamentally wrong and in need of resolution.

The altar call does not create this sense of guilt or shame. It concentrates it. It gathers all that internalised discomfort and gives it a single moment and a single action. Stand up. Walk forward. Respond now. The problem becomes tangible, visible, and urgent.

Then, crucially, the solution is offered immediately. Forgiveness. A fresh start. The assurance that the burden you are carrying has been taken from you and placed somewhere else. The emotional effect of this is powerful. Tension releases. Breathing slows. The nervous system settles. What people experience in that moment is not just spiritual reassurance, but physiological relief.

This is why the experience feels so real and so convincing. The body recognises the change before the mind has time to analyse it. When pressure disappears suddenly, the absence feels profound. It is experienced as freedom, peace, even transformation.

This does not mean the experience is fake or manipulative. The relief is genuine. The problem is not that relief is offered, but that it becomes the primary emotional engine of faith. Christianity often functions by first identifying a felt problem in the self and then presenting itself as the only place where that problem can be resolved.

Psychologists refer to this as a "felt need." You do not have to agree intellectually that something is wrong. You only have to feel it. Restlessness, unease, low grade shame, or the constant sense of not measuring up are enough. Once those feelings are active, the promise of relief becomes deeply compelling.

Religion does not always create these feelings, but it is very effective at organising them and giving them meaning. Over time, people learn where their discomfort is supposed to go and how it is supposed to be resolved.

The difficulty is that relief, by its nature, is temporary. When the pressure returns, as it inevitably does, the system has to reapply it in order to offer release again. Guilt must be reactivated. Shame must be named. The weight must be felt once more before it can be lifted.

This cycle can sustain belief for a long time. What it cannot easily produce is maturity.

### What This Looks Like in Real Life

You can usually tell whether a faith is built on relief or on love by how it handles ordinary pressure.

In a relief-based system, discomfort is treated as a problem to be fixed as quickly as possible. If someone feels guilt, anxiety, or doubt, the response is immediate and familiar. Pray harder. Repent again. Go forward. Get right with God. Do something decisive so the feeling goes away. The goal is not understanding or integration. The goal is relief.

This shows up in small, practical ways. People rush to spiritual answers before they have sat with their own experience. Hard emotions are labelled as spiritual failure rather than normal human signals. Doubt is treated as danger. Questions are tolerated only if they lead quickly back to certainty. Anything unresolved is quietly pushed toward the next altar call, conference, or recommitment moment.

A love-based faith responds differently. Discomfort is not immediately spiritualised or solved. It is treated as information. Guilt is explored rather than instantly erased. Shame is named and examined instead of transferred elsewhere. Anxiety is met with presence rather than urgency. The aim is not to make the feeling disappear, but to understand what it is asking for.

This changes how people relate to God and to themselves. Instead of asking, "How do I get rid of this feeling?" the question becomes, "What is this feeling telling me about my life?" Instead of searching for a moment of release, people learn how to stay present with themselves without panic or self condemnation.

Over time, this produces different outcomes. Relief-based faith often creates people who are sincere but fragile. They cope well when the emotional cycle is working and struggle when it is not. Love-based formation produces people who are slower to react but more resilient. They are less certain, but more grounded. They are not constantly looking for a reset because they are learning how to live inside unresolved reality.

This also affects how communities function. In relief-driven churches, honesty is limited by how disruptive it might be. People learn what can be shared and what needs to be cleaned up first. In love-based communities, honesty comes before resolution. People are allowed to be unfinished without immediately being fixed.

None of this requires abandoning faith. It requires changing what faith is for. Faith stops being a tool for emotional pressure management and becomes a framework for staying present, truthful, and compassionate over the long haul.

That shift is not dramatic. It does not come with music or microphones. It shows up in how people listen, how they respond to pain, and how willing they are to remain with what cannot yet be resolved.

That is where love does the work relief never can.

### What Happens When We Teach This to Children

The stakes change when this emotional system is handed to children.

Adults at least have some agency. They have language, experience, and a partially formed sense of self. Children do not. They are still learning how to understand their emotions, their bodies, and their worth. When we introduce guilt and shame before we teach integration, children do not experience those feelings as signals. They experience them as identity.

A child does not think, *I did something wrong.* A child thinks, *Something is wrong with me.*

When altar calls, confession prayers, and repentance language are aimed at children, the internal pressure lands differently. Children cannot separate moral responsibility from personal value. If they are told their heart is sinful, broken, or deceptive, they take that literally. They assume the problem is not behaviour, but being.

And when relief is offered, it feels overwhelming.

Of course it does. A child who has been taught to carry invisible weight without understanding it will cling to whatever makes that weight go away. Tears flow easily. Commitments are sincere. Hands shoot up. The relief feels profound, because the pressure was never age appropriate to begin with.

This is why so many children appear deeply "on fire" for God and then quietly unravel later. Not because they were insincere, but because they were never taught how to carry complexity. They were taught how to offload it.

As they grow older, the system starts to fail them. The relief stops lasting as long. The guilt returns faster. Life introduces grief, injustice, and unanswered questions that cannot be resolved by a moment at the front of a room. When that happens, many assume the problem is them. They must be drifting. They must be failing spiritually. The pressure intensifies, but the release no longer works the way it once did.

That is often the beginning of collapse, not rebellion.

### Where This Left Me

For me, this did not show up as a dramatic loss of faith. It showed up as a quiet inability to stay with myself.

I had learned how to reset, but not how to repair. I knew how to confess, recommit, and move on, but I did not know how to sit with anger, grief, or disappointment without immediately trying to spiritualise them away. Any lingering discomfort felt like a problem to solve rather than a truth to face.

It took a long time to realise that I was outsourcing responsibility for my inner life. Instead of learning how to understand my reactions, I learned how to override them. Instead of developing self awareness, I learned how to numb and reset. The system had kept me sincere, but it had not helped me mature.

Unlearning that did not happen through another emotional moment. It happened slowly, through paying attention to what I felt without immediately trying to get rid of it. It happened through learning that love does not rush me toward resolution. It stays with me long enough for something deeper to change.

That work was quieter. Less impressive. And far more demanding than any altar call I ever responded to.

But it was the first time my faith began to grow up.

### What I Wish We Had Been Taught Instead

I do not doubt that something real happened to that man who ran down the aisle. Whatever he carried into that room, he did not walk out carrying it in the same way. Anyone who has ever felt an internal weight suddenly lift knows how unmistakable that experience is. It changes your posture. Your breathing. The way you meet the world. To dismiss that would be dishonest.

What I question now is not the sincerity of the moment, but the story we told about it.

We treated that sprint as an arrival, rather than a beginning. We celebrated the release without asking what would help him live differently once the feeling faded. We assumed the lightness would carry him forward on its own. No one wondered aloud what would happen when the weight returned, as it always does.

I sometimes imagine that man years later. Not because I think his faith failed, but because life eventually asks more of all of us than relief can carry. Grief arrives. Illness does not resolve. Relationships strain. Children grow up. Certainty thins. At some point, running to the front of the room is no longer enough.

What we needed then, and what many of us still need now, is not another moment of release, but companions who can stay with us when nothing lifts quickly. Communities that do not rush us toward answers. A faith that is less interested in making discomfort disappear and more committed to helping us grow into it honestly.

This does not mean abandoning forgiveness, grace, or confession. It means placing them inside something larger. A slow, patient formation that teaches people how to listen to their inner life, take responsibility for it, and remain present to God and to others when there is no neat resolution.

If I could speak to my younger self sitting in that pew, watching a man run for his life, I would not tell him the moment was wrong. I would tell him it was incomplete.

And I would invite him, gently but firmly, not to keep chasing the feeling of release, but to learn the harder, quieter work that comes after. The work that does not ask him to offload his humanity, but to grow into it.

That is where faith stops sprinting and begins to walk.

And that, I have come to believe, is where it finally starts to last.
