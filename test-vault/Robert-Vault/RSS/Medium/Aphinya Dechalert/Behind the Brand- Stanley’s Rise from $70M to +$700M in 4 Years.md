# [Behind the Brand: Stanley's Rise from $70M to +$700M in 4 Years | by Aphinya Dechalert](https://freedium-mirror.cfd/https://medium.com/@PurpleGreenLemon/behind-the-brand-stanleys-rise-from-70m-to-700m-in-4-years-0209ebb05485?source=rss-3ac3fb3f09e0------2)

#### Brand Analysis & Diagnostics

Stanley cups aren't new, yet, seemingly overnight, they became a social hit sensation that exploded across social media and a status marker. Annual sales revenue shifted from $73 million in 2019 to $700 million in 2023. By 2024, an insulated metal cup with good branding, crossed the $800 million revenue mark.

So how did we get here? And where is Stanley going? With the rise of competitors and alternatives, are Stanleys still in trend and worth the $60 price point?

### Stanley is a mature brand that exploded

Historically, Stanley's not a young brand. The story goes, William Stanley — the founder of Stanley — wanted to keep his coffee hot while he was out and about. So he created a double-walled vacuum flask and stuck his name on it. That was back in 1913.

The idea of a double-walled vacuum flask wasn't new. Stanley's point of difference is that it was lined with stainless steel, rather than glass (which was common back then).

Stanley's flasks quickly became popular with industrial workers, soldiers, and men who just wanted to keep their drinks hot while they're out and about. Stanley, by design, usage, and general patronage was geared towards men. That image remained and the culture of Stanleys amongst the blue-collar working class persisted.

By the 70s, Stanley established itself as the adult's thermos — though, durable, stainless steel to last forever — unlike their competitor's brand Thermos, which was more targeted towards kids and their lunchboxes.

### Cue the 80s — the plastics boom and disposable culture

Up until the 80s, Stanley had it solid. The brand was part of the "rugged Americana" ecosystem and culture, joining other legacies like Levi's workwear, Zippo lighters, and Coleman coolers.

But the 80s came along with styrofoam cups, which reduced the reliance on carrying around a thermos to keep beverages hot. The product remained solid but it wasn't trendy. There were alternative and cheaper options with plastic water bottles and aluminium flasks.

Despite market shifts, Stanley remained the same. To add to that, the brand became associated with the 'dad flask'. Stanley was what your grandpa and dad carried around — old, but respectable.

### The big consciousness dip

Between the 80s, 90s, and 2000s, Stanley had an aging consumer base problem. The older generation stuck with the brand but the new generation weren't touching it. Sales remained steady but that was that — steady.

On top of that, the product hadn't changed at all since the 70s. Gen X and older Millennials preferred lightweight plastic bottles like Nalgene water bottles. They were cooler, lighter, and you can clip them on your bag. On the football field and gyms, Gatorade squeeze bottles became the norm.

The new generation moved onto cold drinks, whilst Stanley was more associated with keeping things hot (along with being heavy). The coffee-to-go culture also reduced the need for a thermos.

By the time 2002 rolled around, Pacific Market International (PMI) acquired the brand and tried to bring it back by expanding the range to include food jars, flasks, camp cookware and non-insulated drinkware. But again, Stanley still had the 'old people' vibes that it couldn't quite shake off.

### Cue the Quencher — the product that almost died

We've all encountered the Quencher, along with its copycats and unbranded derivatives of it. Launched in 2016, Stanley was about to let the product die a quiet death.

In short, it didn't do well. Or rather, the marketing of it didn't do well. It sat in its adventure range and on the brink of obscurity. The general marketing matrix for Stanley at the time was still heritage, durability, outdoor and worksite. The core group marketed to was men.

It was almost discontinued until *The Buy Guide*, a mom blog with a focus on shopping and aesthetic appeal, saw its practicality. It fits in cup holders, had a straw (so spill proof, yay) and is big enough to be useful.

The first run sold out the 5,000 units partnership in five days. *The Buy Guide* provided proof of demand, and Lauren Sullivan, the marketing brand manager at PMI used the case to prove to execs that there is an untapped market beyond the rugged men, work, and outdoor niche.

That was in 2019.

2020 saw leadership changes. Terence Reilly (the guy who made Crocs cool) became Stanley's global president, scaled the Quencher into mainstream consciousness, and repositioned it with limited drops and a wider color range with more feminine options.

### 1 mom blogger didn't change everything overnight

Brand strategy and a coordinated brand refresh did that. *The Buy Guide* helped nudge Stanley in a better direction. Lauren Sullivan provided the proof case, but it was ultimately Reilly who reshaped the face of Stanley without total alienation of the brand's heritage.

He kept that and even leaned into it.

Everything is more feminine, chic, and even leaning into pop culture like K-Pop's Blackpink Jennie.

From 2020 onwards, influencer partnerships became a core strategy. From seasonal themes to placements at Target, REI, Dick's, and Amazon, Reilly increased Stanley's distribution channels to meet demand while maintaining that sense of exclusiveness through limited edition drops, creating a level of FOMO.

### 2022 — the breakthrough year

Stanley reported [$73 million](https://www.bigblue.co/blog/unveiling-stanleys-hype-quenchers-impact-tiktok-viral) in 2019, moved to $94 million in 2020, and the more than doubled to $194 million in 2021. But the breakthrough figures started when it doubled again in 2022 with $402 million and then eventually reaching the $700 million mark in 2023, and then $800 in 2024.

So what happened in 2022?

TikTok happened. Consumer driven advertising moved Stanleys from being something your dad and grandpa used to a fashion accessory, and having more than one was ok. Stanleys became the female version of collecting sneakers.

Owning a Stanley became a status symbol, part of the clean girl, vanilla girl, that girl, and mominfluencer aesthetic culture. Stanleys became part of the get-ready-with-me influencer culture and found its way into every lives of YouTubers, TikTokers, Shorts, Reels and Pinterest. Having Stanleys available at Target also created accessibility for consumers, making it an obtainable luxury.

### Once popular, doesn't mean it'll stay popular forever

It's been half a decade of Stanley's rise and high, but the brand is facing the same issue it originally had — having only one product driving sales.

While there's an element of personalisation with options to customize your Stanley the same way you'd do with print on demand, there's a chance of product fatigue. The color drops and influencer collabs keeps things fresh, dupe culture is surfacing similar alternatives at half the price.

Then there's the issue of product recalls ([2.6 million](https://www.cpsc.gov/Recalls/2025/Stanley-Recalls-2-6-Million-Switchback-and-Trigger-Action-Travel-Mugs-Due-to-Burn-Hazard) Switchback and Trigger Action travel mug for lid defect in 2024), polypropylene lids shrinking with heat and torque (leading to hot liquid spills). While these aren't part of the Quencher line, the brand's long-time association with being dependable and durable, is tarnished.

Added to that, the [class action lawsuit](https://www.aboutlawsuits.com/class-action-lawsuit-lead-stanley-tumblers) about concerns about Stanleys containing lead also doesn't help the perception. While there's no lead on the drink surfaces itself, the mere fact that there is even lead sent consumers looking for alternatives and competitors that proactively make a point to leverage this selling point.

Stanley's popularity has reached a maturity stage in the social media world that it has to figure out what the next product is and how to keep itself relevant.

The fact that Stanleys are also meant to be near indestructible means that consumers will have a limit cap on how many they end up owning. While there is a resale market for limited edition drops, like the Barbie edition going for [$900 on eBay](https://www.delish.com/food-news/a62283164/stanley-tumblers-resale/), the normal, day-to-day Stanleys don't have the same resale valuation.

### 2025 will be interesting

The year hasn't quite ended yet, and 2025 figures aren't out yet. Overall, Stanley's marketing strategy has leaned into what's worked, with the addition of major names for brand ambassadors like Jennie, Post Malone, and Messi, keeping cultural relevance.

Stanleys aren't exactly a fast fashion product, but its marketed like one. Limited edition drops drive the hype market, while your standard Stanley color range maintains the core bread and butter revenue. We've been through half a decade of the Quencher in its full hype mode, it'll be interesting how Stanley maintains relevance for the next five years.
