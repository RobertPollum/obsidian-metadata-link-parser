# [The Great De-Influencing Paradox | by Aphinya Dechalert](https://freedium-mirror.cfd/https://medium.com/@PurpleGreenLemon/the-great-de-influencing-paradox-49e73f67dbdf?source=rss-3ac3fb3f09e0------2)

Surely I'm not the only one. Lately, in my doom scroll, I've encountered more and more content that's aimed at 'de-influencing' you from owning something.

What I've noticed is that in doing so, the 'de-influencer' is actually advocating for the product that they personally use and trust. The premise for de-influencers is simple — don't follow the hype train because what's tried and tested works — and that's why it's worth the investment for that particular product.

If you really look at it, it's trust marketing by stealth.

### Let's look at the origin story — anti-hauls and burnout

I first encountered de-influencing some time last year. To be honest, I probably encountered the idea before that, but it didn't hit my awareness stratosphere quite as hard. Recently, the algorithms have picked up on my microsecond pauses and curiosity gaps, and now it's showing me a ton of de-influencing content.

Suddenly, my feed is full of creators tearing down products I've either bought, wanted, or just felt vaguely guilty about wanting.

It makes sense. People are done watching influencers act like unpaid hosts for PR boxes they didn't even ask for. The whole cycle started feeling artificial — the forced excitement, the "omg guys you *need* this" tone, the mountains of unopened packages in the background. There's only so much fake enthusiasm the internet can metabolize before it starts to taste like plastic.

For years, recommendation was treated as authenticity, until audiences realised they were watching a sales funnel disguised as friendship.

De-influencing reintroduced something people hadn't seen in a while: evidence. Not the kind that comes from claims or campaigns, but from time. We're talking about things like a bottle with worn edges or a product that's lasted through several seasons of use. The visible signs of wear have become a new kind of proof, carrying more credibility than any promise attached to a new release.

### Plot twist — de-influeincing actually sells trust

The funny part is, the point of de-influencing is to push back against overconsumption. However, viewers still want to buy, and de-influencing makes them feel like it's more deliberate.

De-influencing shifts patterns from impulse to justifications. Products are reframed as investments and not mere hyped indulgences.

This aligns neatly with the broader slow-living movement that's been gaining traction over the past few years. People want to feel that what they buy has weight, that it will last, that it says something about how they live. "Investment pieces" and "buy it once" culture give consumers a sense of moral correctness in a system that still revolves around consumption.

### Credibility is the ultimate currency

De-influencing sells trust as the new luxury. When someone says, *"I've had this for years and it's still good,"* it validates the fantasy of stability in a world that refreshes itself every five seconds. The transaction is about the reassurance that the choice was thoughtful, even ethical.

From a behavioral standpoint, credibility functions as a **signal of reliability,** the same way wear and tear signals endurance. Studies on consumer trust show that [67% of buyers](https://som.yale.edu/story/2023/navigating-brand-trust-modern-marketing) say they must trust a brand before purchasing, and consistent behavior over time is the strongest predictor of that trust. The creator who rejects ten products and vouches for one communicates reliability through discernment.

The longer something lasts, the more persuasive it becomes and the more authority the product accumulates. Most things disappear before they have the chance to prove themselves, and most creators are caught in a cycle that rewards novelty over endurance.

### De-influencing = old stuff = good stuff

So many legacy brands have started pulling their past into the foreground because of that same search for credibility. Le Creuset's marketing barely changes because it doesn't need to; the enamel pot already carries decades of proof. It's the kind of object that shows up in family kitchens and outlives multiple recipe trends.

But not every brand has a century to draw from. Some are new but enter the market already aged, borrowing the language, textures, and design cues of the past to build trust faster. It is a kind of manufactured heritage, and it works because people read age as credibility.

Brands like **Graza**, **Ghia**, and **Acid League** build their visual identity around that illusion of time. Graza's olive oil looks like it has always been in the kitchen, familiar yet anonymous. Ghia packages its aperitifs in glass bottles that recall mid-century Italian bars. Acid League, a company barely a few years old, designs its vinegars to resemble something pulled from a vintage pantry. These brands aren't selling nostalgia; they're selling continuity.

### The Takeaway

De-influencing rejects hype. But it also became a form of curation. Excitement used to be a sales tactic, but for conversions of new customers, it's credibility that moves people through to the sales page. The less a creator appears to sell, the more persuasive they become.

This shift says something bigger about the state of marketing: people don't want less; they want meaning. They want to believe their purchases reflect discernment, that what they buy stands for patience, longevity, or ethics in a system that moves too fast. The irony is that de-influencing doesn't dismantle the market but just rebrands it as self-control.

In the end, the product isn't what's being sold. What's really being sold is reassurance and the comforting illusion that we're finally spending wisely.
