# [Curiosity Marketing Is Much More Effective Than "Buy Now" | by Aphinya Dechalert](https://freedium-mirror.cfd/https://medium.com/@PurpleGreenLemon/curiosity-marketing-is-much-more-effective-than-buy-now-39372d931aa7?source=rss-3ac3fb3f09e0------2)

I was listening to Farmhouse on Boone recently when Lisa had a guest — [Leslie Burris](https://youtu.be/pUvA7pqpDZo?si=U0iBOR733Q-uH2-q), the founder of Ellbee's, the company behind [strawberry liver melts](https://leslieburris.myshopify.com/products/ellbees-beef-liver-primal-bites?variant=41321907978353) (which are basically like those freeze-dried yogurt melts you give to babies, except it's liver instead).

In the podcast, Leslie talked about how her business grew by making people curious, and there was no shouting and people to buy. There were no hard sells in the process, just smart targeting with her content that spread organically through her followers.

The premise behind curiosity marketing is simple — you don't push, you invite them into your story until they become so invested that they're willing to try out your product without you ever prompting them to buy.

### Why curiosity marketing works

Traditional marketing tells people what to think. Curiosity marketing relinquishes this and passes the thinking to the customer, leading to more questions and rabbit hole effects for your brand.

Psychologists call this the *information gap theory.* It's where we feel the need to bridge the missing knowledge of what we know and what we want to know. When this happens, our brain releases dopamine to push us towards the answer.

That's why TikToks are so addictive, especially the ones that cut out halfway through a story. You're invested, but the content doesn't quite deliver the resolution it needs to, leaving you with a cliffhanger.

In an A/B test, using the curiosity gap brought in [927% uplift in clicks](https://copyhackers.com/2014/04/curiosity-gap) on a payment plan.

### Authenticity makes curiosity believable

Authentic marketing is the other term being thrown around a lot. It's often linked to UGCs, unpolished videos, real reviews, and off-script reactions that makes people stop scrolling. There is an indie, almost raw Y2K vibe to authenticity, making it feel less scripted and more real.

[92% of consumers](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/384898606_IMPACT_ON_BRAND_TRUST_AND_CONSUMER_BEHAVIOR_LEVERAGINGSOCIAL_MEDIA_INFLUENCERS_TO_DRIVE_CREDIBILITY_ANDENGAGEMENT_ACROSS_INDUSTRIES) trust recommendations from individuals more than brand advertising, even if that person is a stranger on the internet. As long as they are another person talking about the brand, their opinion is valued more than any polished advert the brand brings out.

Why? Because authenticity acts as a mirror to the identities we associate with. When a product shows up in someone's routine (think messy kitchens, unfiltered bathroom shelf or a quick "this actually worked for me" kind of clip), it reflects back on a version of life we recognize. It feels relatable and maybe a little aspirational, depending on the product and the problems it solves.

And that's the hook — curiosity loops you in and authenticity makes you invested.

When a brand tries to fake it with overly scripted 'relatable' ads, the moment feels less genuine and we can't quite project ourselves into it. Traditional selling is losing its power because the Internet has trained us to recognize a pitch within the first millisecond. We scroll past it in an instant without even registering what its trying to sell. We just know that the advert is trying to sell.

Instead of forcing a linear sales funnel — awareness, interest, decision, action — curiosity marketing turns it into a loop. Someone sees something intriguing, explores, connects with the story, and comes back when they're ready. It's slower, but it builds better customers.

### The takeaway

Curiosity marketing works because it feels human. It mirrors how we actually discover things: one small breadcrumb at a time. We sprial ourselves into familiarity with a brand, leading to organic growth of trust.

For small brands, this shift matters. You don't have to outspend competitors; you just have to out-interest them. Curiosity is free. Authenticity is free. You only need to know what makes your story worth telling and what makes someone lean in, even for a second.

So maybe the question isn't *"How do I get people to buy?"* anymore. It's *"What would make them care enough to look twice?"*

Because once they do, the selling part takes care of itself.
