# [Why Students Can't Write Anymore | by Aphinya Dechalert | in Write A Catalyst](https://freedium-mirror.cfd/https://medium.com/write-a-catalyst/why-students-cant-write-anymore-a26ac06a2186?source=rss-3ac3fb3f09e0------2)

I was scrolling through TikTok and hit the space where teachers complain about AI detectors. All stories are the same. They get their students to write up the essays by hand in class, type it up, and hand it in through the system.

Turnitin (or whatever system they use) flags it as AI.

But how could it possibly be AI if they wrote it right in front of them?

The problem isn't the content produced, but students no longer being taught how to write.

### Gen Z are assimilating AI writing patterns

Statistically, almost [40% of active web pages](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2504.08755) are AI-generated. The internet is essentially polluted with AI-sentences that Gen Zs are reading and internalizing. Then when it comes time to produce content, they sound like a bot.

Not because they're using AI, but because they don't know how **not** to sound like one.

[86.5%](https://ahrefs.com/blog/ai-generated-content-does-not-hurt-your-google-rankings/) of top-ranking search results contain some form of AI content. I listen to what my kids are watching on YouTube, and all I can hear is AI-generated content. Next thing I know, they start to talk like ChatGPT.

It's the same for TikTok, Reels, and Shorts — all the things that Gen Zs (and Alphas) consume daily. Their brains are being pushed to speak, write, and think in the same predictive loops that language models are trained on.

Linguistic development comes from exposure. Variations in the English language exist because of the dialect grammars, vocabularies, and accents between different groups of English speakers. It's all English, but how the British form their sentences in daily speech is significantly different from American English speakers. A generation submerged in AI-pattern language is showing signs of an internalized 'synthetic' dialect through AI detector flags, despite actually putting in the hard work.

The problem is not the actual student, but the environment. They're linguistically developing in an era shaped by algorithmic averages.

### Tools that humanize don't truly humanize

The cheat code for passing AI detectors is to find a humanizer AI. But the problem with these 'humanizers' is that they're just playing a form of synonym roulette. 'Humanize' the sentence enough, and it loses meaning and context entirely.

The structural issues created by generative AI do not go away.

There is an abundance of content that teaches kids how to avoid AI detection. On top of that are the bazillion tools promising to make your text sound more human at the click of a button.

The real solution is to teach kids (and adults) how to see patterns that make their writing sound synthetic. Basically, teach kids to be better writers and relearn how to write like humans.

But we're hit with a secondary problem. You can't unlearn something you can't see.

### AI editing is a skill that needs to be developed

There's no escaping ChatGPT and AI-generated content. It's part of our reality now.

As writers, editors, content creators, parents, and teachers, we need to develop our own AI editing skills so we don't fall into the statistical average of generative AI.

LLMs works on probability models and produce content based on what sounds right and not what is correct or with intent. That's why AI content feels smooth but empty. Without editing, the result is language that flows perfectly but means nothing.

If we teach editing for intent, clarity, and meaning, we can raise a generation fluent in English *and* confident in authentic expression.
