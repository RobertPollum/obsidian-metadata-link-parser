### How we got here with em dashes

Em dashes are something special. Before AI, writers used them selectively to present a sudden break in thought before looping back to the main topic.

It doubled as an extra set of commas or parentheses, sometimes to introduce aditional information — much like a colon.

Which is fine. Em dashes are an extension of thought, often taking you down long, winding roads that end up being mini adventures, courtesy of the author's literary decisions.

Emily Dickinson used em dashes liberally, sprinkling them in specific places to create rhythm and emotional tone. Even Stephen King uses them, simply because he can.

That was all back in ye olden' days, pre-Y2K era, and before electricity was a thing. (Ok, maybe we're going too far back with the electricity thing for Stephen King.)

Em dashes used to be special; now they pollute the internet, infecting content like some sort of digital plague. If you dare to have an em dash in your writing, your content risks being flagged as AI.

### How AI uses em dashes

LLMs are the digital brains behind AI. It's analytical, calculating, and runs on the statistical probability of what the next word should be.

The keyword here is ***[statistical probability](https://fieldnotes.theodoreapp.com/ai-generated-content-and-google-ranking/)****,* which means that what it spits out is always right, just what it thinks the next word should be based on what's previously been said.

The problem we're faced with nowadays is that it has skewed towards excessive use of em dashes, especially incorrect usage.

How did we get here? LLM models learn from what already exists. When AI-generated content gets published online and later becomes part of the dataset that future models train on, it starts eating its own tail.

If the original text was written by someone who didn't proofread or understand the grammar behind what they wrote, the model inherits those mistakes as statistical norms. Over time, low-value patterns like overusing em dashes, hedging phrases, or incoherent sentence structures get reinforced as "acceptable" because they appear so frequently in the training data.

[57% of the internet](https://www.windowscentral.com/software-apps/sam-altman-indicated-its-impossible-to-create-chatgpt-without-copyrighted-material) is now AI-generated. [74.2 % of new pages](https://ahrefs.com/blog/what-percentage-of-new-content-is-ai-generated/?) on the internet is AI.

Humans use punctuation to structure thought. LLMs use punctuation to predict rhythm. When uncertain about how to connect clauses, the model often defaults to an em dash because it signals a pause *without committing* to a grammatical relationship (contrast, cause, explanation, etc.).

It usually happens when two ideas feel related, but the model isn't sure how. AI uses em dashes as the syntactical glue to make itself 'feel' human to human readers.

AI doesn't *use* em dashes in the way we use them, but *copies their frequency pattern*. The more the internet misuses em dashes, the more the model does too.

### We can still use em dashes, but do it in a non-AI way

The difference between a human em dash and an AI em dash is intent. [When humans write](https://fieldnotes.theodoreapp.com/students-sound-like-ai/), the em dash acts like a controlled disruption — a pause that *means something*. It signals thought, emphasis, or emotion.

To reclaim it, you have to be deliberate. Use em dashes when they clarify a sentence, not when they just *look* good. If the sentence still works with a comma or period, use those instead. Make the dash earn its place.

Human writers use punctuation with rhythm, not randomness. Read your sentence out loud. If the em dash adds a cadence that helps the reader follow your thought — keep it. If it feels like you're doing it for "style points," delete it.

Deleting em dashes from AI-generated content doesn't make it less AI. It's the deliberate structure and choice of words, strung together to form a coherent thought, is the *thing* that makes you sound human.
