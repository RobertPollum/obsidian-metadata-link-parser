# [How Google Can Tell When Your Content Isn't Human | by Aphinya Dechalert](https://freedium-mirror.cfd/https://medium.com/@PurpleGreenLemon/how-google-can-tell-when-your-content-isnt-human-83052bc7aae2?source=rss-3ac3fb3f09e0------2)

I've read enough Google documentation to know when they're being polite about a problem. Publishing AI-generated content and Google ranking is one of these problems.

Their [official guidance](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/fundamentals/using-gen-ai-content) puts it more diplomatically:

> *Generative AI can be particularly useful when researching a topic, and to add structure to original content. However, using generative AI tools or other similar tools to generate many pages* ***without adding value for users may violate*** *Google's spam policy on scaled content abuse.*

We all see those sites, thousands of posts stitched together from the same three ideas. It's the digital equivalent of styrofoam. The search giant never says "don't use AI." What they actually mean, tucked between the lines, is *don't make the internet worse.*

-   **~****[74% of new web pages](https://ahrefs.com/blog/what-percentage-of-new-content-is-ai-generated/)** **include AI content**. In a dataset of 900,000 pages, Ahrefs categorized 2.5 % as pure AI, 25.8 % as pure human, and **71.7 % as a mix** of AI + human content.
-   **[Human-written content](https://www.rebootonline.com/blog/ai-vs-humans-content-writing-seo-experiment/)** **ranks higher (avg 4.4) vs AI content (avg 6.6)** In an SEO experiment, human content domains averaged rank 4.4, AI content averaged 6.6 (lower ranking).
-   **Human content drives more traffic long term**. In one case study, by month 5, human-created content had **[5.44× more traffic](https://neilpatel.com/blog/ai-vs-human-content/)** than AI-generated content.
-   **Consumers can detect AI copy** **[~50 % of the time](https://www.marketingtechnews.net/news/50-of-consumers-can-detect-ai-generated-content/)**. In a test between AI vs human writing, ~50 % of participants correctly identified the AI version.
-   **Accuracy collapses with** **[AI paraphrasing](https://research.umd.edu/articles/ai-generated-content-actually-detectable)**. University of Maryland researchers found that using paraphrasing reduces the effectiveness of AI detectors to near random chance.

Google doesn't mind if you use a machine to help you write. If you publish something that teaches, entertains, or explains in a way a human would actually read, it passes. If it looks like a soulless content calendar trying to hit quota, it doesn't get to rank.

### What Google evaluates in AI-generated content and ranking

Google doesn't have feelings, but it behaves like it does. It knows when something feels useful and when it doesn't. The [Helpful Content System](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/fundamentals/creating-helpful-content), [E-E-A-T](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/appearance/eeat), and [RankBrain](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/appearance/ranking-systems-guide#rankbrain) aren't mystical acronyms. They're Google's way of answering the same question: *did this help anyone?*

**The algorithm isn't looking for effort in the "I worked hard on this" sense. It's looking for signals that people actually got what they came for**.

At the end of it all, Google doesn't care who wrote it. It doesn't care if it was AI, a freelancer, or a tired marketer at 2 a.m. It cares whether someone stayed long enough to finish the paragraph, whether they clicked to learn more, whether they shared it because it said something new or said it better.

That's the real ranking signal is behavior.

The whole system is basically built to reward curiosity. The moment you start writing for the algorithm instead of for the person at the other end of the screen, you fall out of sync with the thing the algorithm is actually designed to measure.

**When a post reads like it was written to check a box, people skim and leave. When it feels like someone sat down and tried to** ***say something*****, people stay. Google measures that difference. Not directly, it doesn't peek into your head, but through the patterns of engagement that show up again and again across billions of searches.**

### How AI-generated content triggers low-quality ranking signals

You've probably seen this happen already. Someone takes a topic that's been written to death, runs it through ChatGPT, cleans up the grammar, and hits publish. It looks fine. It even reads fine. But then nothing happens. No ranking, no traction, no one reading past the intro.

That's because *fine* doesn't move anyone.

When Google crawls your page, it doesn't need to "detect AI." It can tell when something was written with intent and when it wasn't. The clues are all over the language.

Here's where it usually falls apart.

### 1\. The content says nothing new

AI doesn't create. It reorganizes. That's why most machine-written content feels like déjà vu — familiar phrasing, familiar structure, familiar emptiness. **Google calls it** ***low information gain*****: text that adds nothing new.**

The Helpful Content System is designed to bury that. It rewards fresh perspective, the kind that could only come from someone who's actually thought about the topic. Most AI text just [blends fragments](http://mariehaynes.com/google-ai-systems/) from existing results. It's technically correct but mentally vacant.

-   Before publishing, ask yourself one question: *Does this add anything to the conversation?* If not, stop editing and start researching. Pull up the top three ranking pages for your keyword.
-   If your draft doesn't introduce *one* insight, example, or dataset those pages missed, you're not adding value. You're just rephrasing the index.

### 2\. The rhythm is too clean in AI-generated content

Machines love order. Every sentence ends neatly. Every paragraph mirrors the last. It's easy to read but impossible to feel. **That perfect rhythm is one of the biggest tells of AI writing.** It's what the [Search Quality Rater Guidelines](https://static.googleusercontent.com/media/guidelines.raterhub.com/en//searchqualityevaluatorguidelines.pdf) indirectly punish when they flag "assembled content."

Real writing isn't smooth. It stumbles a little. It stretches. It breathes. That imperfection is what keeps people reading.

-   Read your piece out loud. If it sounds like it could be narrated by an instructional video, it's too clean.
-   Break the template. Follow a long, winding sentence with something short and abrupt. Add a question. Use rhythm as punctuation and not decoration. If your paragraph flows *too well*, make it trip once.

### 3\. The writing doesn't go anywhere

AI explains but rarely explores. It summarizes instead of reasoning. The result is a wall of words that never builds toward anything. Google calls this *low factual density*. Lots of text, not much substance.

The [E-E-A-T framework](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/appearance/eeat) solves that by rewarding experience. A person who's actually done the thing will write differently. They'll reference what it looked like, how it felt, what went wrong. That level of detail can't be generated.

-   Every few paragraphs, ask yourself: *What's the point of this section?* If you can't answer it in one sentence, it's filler.

### 4\. There's no voice behind it

You can spot this one instantly. The tone is neutral, the conclusions are safe, and the whole piece reads like it was written for a brand with no opinions. That's where most AI text dies.

Google's [own post on AI content](https://developers.google.com/search/blog/2023/02/google-search-and-ai-content) says automation's fine when it supports expertise, meaning there still needs to be a human behind the words. Readers can tell when there isn't. They crave perspective, conviction, and a bit of personality.

-   Write one paragraph like you'd say it to a friend. Then keep that energy. Clarity doesn't mean corporate. Don't write for approval.
-   Write like someone might disagree. If your paragraph couldn't offend *anyone*, it probably won't move *anyone* either.

### 5\. It scales too easily, and Google notices

AI makes it dangerously easy to flood the internet. Entire websites can pump out hundreds of pages overnight. Google calls that scaled content abuse, publishing volume without value.

You can't build trust by out-publishing everyone. You build it by saying something worth showing up for.

-   Before writing another post, check if your last one's getting read. If no one finished it, adding ten more won't help.
-   Track *completion rate*, not output. A thousand impressions mean nothing if nobody finishes reading. Your retention is the metric you should focus on, not word counts.

### Why Editing AI-generated Content Doesn't Improve Google Ranking

Most people realize their AI drafts sound a little stiff, so they do the obvious thing, they feed it back into another AI model and say "*make this sound more human.*"

What they get back is still AI, just wearing better clothes.

The rhythm's the same. The logic's the same. The voice is still that weird neutral tone that tries to sound like everyone and no one at the same time.

**The problem is structural.**

AI doesn't understand what makes writing *feel* alive; it just knows how to approximate it. So when you ask it to rewrite itself, it keeps pulling from the same statistical pool of "good writing."

That's why everything starts blending together online. It's because they're all running on the same feedback loop.
