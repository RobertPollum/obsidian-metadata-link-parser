# [The Customer You Made Up Isn't Buying Anything | by Aphinya Dechalert](https://freedium-mirror.cfd/https://medium.com/@PurpleGreenLemon/the-customer-you-made-up-isnt-buying-anything-5291327f8955?source=rss-3ac3fb3f09e0------2)

I remember the first time I got hit with a customer persona — it was back in University and a bunch of freshly out of high school students were trying to create a story for who their products would sell to.

Luckily, I wasn't part of that group project and was merely there as an alternative perspective. Back then, I was the infosys student with a knack of looking at things differently. There was something vanilla and deeply cookie cutter about the entire exercise — no validation, no deeper understanding, no psychology — just a random person that may or may not exist.

Over the years, I'd get hit with customer personas through projects. There was always a generic name and a story attached. I'd read the stories and struggled to find anything of great significance about this 'customer'.

### "Drives a Honda" means nothing

The problem with marketing personas is that they're just personas — made up stories about who your potential buyer could be. It's a fictional label that many junior marketers take too seriously.

These stories become symbolic and are not grounded in reality.

[68% of businesses](https://www.amraandelma.com/poor-marketing-statistics) admit they waste money on ineffective ads, and 42% of marketers say poor audience targeting is their biggest failure.

The worst customer personas I've encountered are the ones where there is existing sales data, but no one has bothered to match the stories up with actual buyers.

There's no validation for demand, no check that the 'persona' is actually interested in the product, or any kind of fact that grounds Jane Doe as an actual person.

### The real reason why your customer persona is failing you

Campaigns based around unvalidated personas often fail to capture the right attention. What proof do you have that oat milk loving Jane who drives a Honda Civic will actually like or need your proudct? Why a Honda Civic and not a Ford Focus?

Personas that are used to represent reality flatten human complexity. The idea of customer personas itself was created a UX empathy tool that turned into an overused marketing caricature.

For example, why does Jane love oat milk? Is it because she's lactose intolerant or because she just likes the taste? Or maybe it's the cheapest plant milk product? Why is this relevant if you're trying to sell shoes?

[86% of consumers' buying choices](https://www.greenbook.org/insights/research-methodologies/the-power-of-emotional-needs-in-consumer-purchases-insights-from-two-studies) were shaped by a set of emotional needs that expands beyond pricing and features. On top of that, [88% of consumers](https://wiserreview.com/blog/word-of-mouth-marketing-stats) trust other customer recommendations, with 13% of all consumer sales being driven by word-of-mouth.

That's why the real savvy marketers use social priming and network mirroring to sell to you instead.

### How 'my phone is listening to me' works

There's a conspiracy that our phones are listening to us. Well, they are, but just not in the way we think.

There's a thing called the Baader-Meinhof phenomenon. In a nutshell, it's when you recently learned, heard, or noticed something and suddenly it's everywhere.

The cognitive mechanics works in two parts — selective attention and confirmation bias. Once your brain tags something as being relevant, it filters more of it into your awareness. Then each time you notice it again, it reinforces your belief that it's suddenly everywhere.

For example, you talk to someone about air fryers, and suddenly you start seeing air fryers everywhere. The awareness is triggered. Social media algorithms take note of your every pause, lingering scroll, and half-second hesitations. Every time there's a spark of interest in something, the system tags it and amplifies the frequency of that advert, conversation, reel, story, and whatever else you may have encountered through your phone.

Each exposure nudges your brain towards familiarity, which feels like trust. It's the [mere-exposure effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mere-exposure_effect) at work. Then you get hit with a relevant time-sensitive nudge, and voilà, you've got yourself a sale.

### User personas can be used as a starting point, but it shouldn't be the only point

If you want your product to sell, you shouldn't spend too much time inventing the 'perfect' persona. Your main focus should be figuring how how people enter the awarenesss stage, which is, the exact moment an idea lodges itself into someone's mind and how it is relevant to them.

Once awareness exists, the next task is to build familiarity through repetition, validation, and timing.

For B2B, it takes around [7 interactions](https://www.b2bmarketing.net/the-marketing-rule-of-7-and-why-its-still-relevant-in-b2b) with your product before a purchase happens — or over [60 touchpoints](https://www.upvise.co/article/average-touchpoints-of-a-b2b-customer-before-a-purchase) for higher ticket and long-term strategic decisions. For B2C, it can take anywhere between [6 to 20 times](https://pathmonk.com/b2c-customer-journey-touchpoints) before a consumer actually goes through the cart process.

The point is — you don't need to predict who your customer is. What you need to do is understand what makes them notice, what keeps them engaged, and what finally makes them decide.
