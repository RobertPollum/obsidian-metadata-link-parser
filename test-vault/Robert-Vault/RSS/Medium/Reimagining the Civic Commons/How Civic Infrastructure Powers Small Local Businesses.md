#### Public spaces benefit communities on many levels — including the people who run the shops, local services and eateries that depend on them.

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*sFAXyxNVd8L7sk_5fCTmEA.jpeg)

The grounds and Welcome Center at Bartram’s Garden in Philadelphia often serve as a market for local vendors. Photo credit: Ray Valdez.

Small businesses are prime beneficiaries of robust civic infrastructure. From street festivals, to cafés catering to parkgoers, to bustling main streets filled with shoppers and diners, the financial and social benefits of a well-maintained public realm for local businesses are evident. In Detroit, Philadelphia, Lexington and Memphis, business owners spoke about the benefits of high-quality public spaces for their bottom line and for strengthening bonds with their clientele and communities.

#### **Growing a city’s walkability and outlook**

High-quality public spaces can enhance more than just the sales for local business owners.

Lexington, Kentucky, which has recently invested in a network of public spaces along the [Town Branch Commons](https://www.lexingtonky.gov/government/mayors-office/town-branch-commons) — an award-winning linear greenway opened in 2022 — local business owners say the city’s outlook has made them feel more optimistic.

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*moi9OofR53GuHpg2ijzjYA.jpeg)

Lexington’s A Cup of Common Wealth coffee shops are drawing more customers thanks to the opening of a new city park nearby. Photo credit: Lilly Bramley.

Salvador Sanchez, owner of A Cup of Common Wealth coffee shops, said the city feels more liveable and walkable. “While there has been a little spike in business, that isn’t what I would highlight. It’s starting to feel even more like a cool, hip city with pedestrian accessibility,” said Sanchez, who has one café next to [Northeastern Park](https://www.kentucky.com/news/local/counties/fayette-county/article272646145.html). “More people walk by the shop and just say hi as if we’re neighbors. The park has more people, and it feels safer.”

The long-awaited reopening of [Phoenix Park](https://www.downtownlex.com/locations/phoenix-park/) in the heart of downtown Lexington has excited nearby restaurateur Ilias Pappas of Athenian Grill. “We cannot be more excited about it! It’s accessible, looks great, and incorporates kids and family,” he said. With the park enticing people to stay in the area longer, Pappas said the restaurant could consider longer hours for downtown workers to have dinner or have a family outing.

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*h-CmguM2ygBLNjBWd8ZgXA.jpeg)

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*yuCgS6hlE9FXUHfh8CPeYQ.jpeg)

More visitors spending more time in downtown Lexington offers new possibilities for local businesses. Photo credits: Amy Wallot and Haley Wartell.

Lexington’s initial investment in Town Branch Commons has started paying off, according to research from the Landscape Architecture Foundation, with more than $110 million in municipal, grant, and philanthropic investments in the development or renovation of parks and a career technical education center for the local school district.

The parks have also started to reshape the image of the city for Sanchez — perhaps expanding the possibilities of what kinds of businesses could serve Lexington residents better in the future.

“More people are walking! More people are out and about and using the path. There is so much more growth that is sure to happen, but it’s nice to see a lot more people out walking, running, and biking around the city,” Sanchez said.

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*u6l_1wtyvROcM0x5AI5O3A.jpeg)

Singer Charity performed in front of the “Detroit Was Made in the Neighborhoods” mural during the 6 Mile Street Festival. Photo credit: Bre’Anna Johnston

#### **Building a customer base from a street festival**

This past September in Detroit, the [Live6 Alliance](https://www.live6detroit.org/) launched the first ever 6 Mile Street Festival, transforming West McNichols Road into a corridor-wide celebration of community, commerce and culture. Live6 Alliance, the Civic Commons convener in Detroit, had much to celebrate: the district’s new Select Level Michigan Main Street designation, a commemoration of Live6’s 10th anniversary and recognition of Detroit as the country’s first UNESCO City of Design.

The festival’s purpose was to elevate more than a dozen local businesses, organizational partners and two anchor institutions — University of Detroit Mercy and Marygrove Conservancy — who activated storefronts and sidewalks with food tastings, art showcases and workshops, live music and family-friendly activities.

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*1J-_F3FFG5U38A5SswCd7w.jpeg)

New bakery Matron Saint enticed customers with fresh treats in front of their nascent location on W. McNichols. Photo _credit: Bre’Anna Johnston_

The event even had its own funny money: a special edition of “$6 Bucks, ” a returning community currency promotion sponsored by First Merchants Bank that distributed $5,000 in vouchers to attendees, encouraging them to shop and dine at participating corridor businesses. The activation strengthened residents’ connection to neighborhood entrepreneurs based along the commercial corridor and reinvigorated the street as a shared public amenity, demonstrating how corridor investment can fuel both community pride and small business growth.

The 6 Mile Street Festival’s ability to connect people with local businesses meant “more people are discovering what our area has to offer, and that means we get to keep our doors open, serve more families, and continue building a place where people feel welcome,” said Leona Milton, owner of the What’s the Dill stuffed pickle sandwiches deli. “It’s not just about sales, it’s about the sense of connection, pride and togetherness.”

The festival’s creative strategies that brought people into local stores and built relationships demonstrates the benefits of civic infrastructure for small businesses. Nearly two thousand people attended the festival, which organizers said was one of the largest crowds seen on 6 Mile in more than a decade, and the promotional “$6 Bucks” were fully spent during the festival at the 17 participating businesses — invaluable marketing for these shopowners and restauranteurs.

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*UWRaKsbsnR900xpm9Ye3EA.jpeg)

#### **Putting welcome centers to work**

The [Bartram’s Garden](https://www.bartramsgarden.org/) Welcome Center helps guide visitors around the popular Philadelphia public park, which is also the country’s oldest preserved botanic garden. If the visitor happens to also need locally produced candles, they’re in the right place. The welcome center has partnered with local artisans and crafters for years, offering its space for onsite sales during events and festivals, including the popular Handmade Holidays shop in December. The support extends beyond the physical center, as well — the Bartram’s Garden website has an online shop where the vendors can sell their [wares](https://bartrams-garden.myshopify.com/products/candles-from-scents-wicks) without the expense and hassle of running their own website. Workshops and special classes run by the vendors are also hosted at the center and promoted by the Bartram’s Garden [website](https://www.bartramsgarden.org/learn/).

Most importantly for Facenda White, who sells all-natural candles at the center, Bartram’s Garden was intentional about ensuring its vendors felt supported in their mission of providing healing, space and resources for their community.

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*mrkm48yL9hlNXRc4GT5LJw.jpeg)

Facenda White set up a table to vend her wares at the Bartram’s Garden 2025 Spring Fest. Image credit: Shannon Sophy.

“Bartram’s Garden gave me my first opportunity to be able to explore connections with that community that’s already involved,’” she said.

White noted other opportunities have emerged since she began selling her Scents & Wicks candles at Bartram’s Garden events, including the Christmas Village, an annual vendor marketplace at Philadelphia’s City Hall and the iconic Love Park in Center City.

Showing support for the local artisans has been in turn a successful strategy for Bartram’s Garden, with sales at the Welcome Center rising 1.5 percent in the past year despite overall economic turmoil across the country.

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*rzUinLJveqxE4brhfoxpSw.jpeg)

#### **Local businesses as building blocks for community identity**

In nascent neighborhoods still developing, the public realm supports flourishing small businesses that provide job opportunities and critical social infrastructure that in turn build community identity.

Located between the distinct neighborhoods of Downtown and Midtown, Memphis’s Medical District has languished without much of a sense of place. With a [decade-long revitalization](https://nextcity.org/urbanist-news/lessons-from-a-downtown-district-thats-bucking-national-trends) of the 2.6-square mile district underway, the [Memphis Medical District Collaborative (MMDC)](https://www.memphismedicaldistrict.org/) is changing this perception and revitalizing the district using investments from anchor institutions like hospitals, medical centers and educational institutions. The Medical District’s anchor development strategy emphasizes place-based investment to enhance commercial and residential density and a comprehensive suite of programs that nurture and activate vibrant public space, parks, and safe streets.

Support for small businesses is a key part of the plan. Dozens of new businesses — many minority- and women-owned — have opened in the district and supported through technical assistance, incentives and grants.

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*aNpQirGEkwBp0hixi0QAnA.jpeg)

Goat Yoga was a popular event in the Medical District’s Health Sciences Park this summer. Image credit: Memphis Medical District Collaborative.

The local businesses help solidify the image of the Medical District as a coherent, thriving community, said senior director of Workforce Strategies Latasha Harris: “All the programs work together to make a stronger, more people-centered district.”

Thanks to the growth of businesses where residents and local workers can shop for goods and services and buy food and meals without ever needing to leave the district, people now feel compelled to linger and spend their leisure time and weekends there. In addition to commercial development, improvements to public space — including enhanced streetscapes, lighting and greenspace — have made the area feel safer and more connected. Special programming and events further activate these spaces, inviting people to gather, enjoy and bring them to life.

“Until recent years, the Medical District felt like a forgotten part of town…an unwelcoming area in between downtown and midtown,” one local employee reported in a community survey. “The first change I noticed was the overall cleanliness and ‘look and feel’ of the general area. Nowadays, it feels like a growing and striving neighborhood with a sense of community.”
