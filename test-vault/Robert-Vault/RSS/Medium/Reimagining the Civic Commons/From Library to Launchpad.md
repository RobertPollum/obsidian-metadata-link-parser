#### **_How Memphis’ Innovator-in-Residence program is evolving public libraries into creative hubs_**

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*B5CRWw2UbaoFbTVef6cEXQ.png)

Cossitt Library in Memphis has redefined its mission to serve the community as stage, studio and creative hub. Image credit: D’Angelo Connell Photography.

Across the country, [new research is shining a light on an unexpected driver of neighborhood vitality: the public library](https://storymaps.arcgis.com/stories/0359eea2298b4e769bc190786d6ea105). Once primarily quiet sanctuaries for learning, today’s libraries are becoming engines of civic life — places that draw people into downtown and neighborhoods, support the desires of local residents and help people and communities reconnect. In Memphis, the library system is taking this promise a step further, turning its branches into creative hubs where innovation, learning and local enterprise meet.

Through its [Innovator-in-Residence program](https://www.memphislibrary.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/03/Innovator-In-Residence-Opportunity-Description.pdf), Memphis Public Libraries is pioneering a new kind of staffing model — part creative incubator, part community catalyst. By inviting artists, designers, content creators and entrepreneurs to embed their work within library spaces, the program transforms public space into a platform for shared creation and civic connection that reaches residents across the city.

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*5NEnt8eh4-n1kUYV09gnJw.jpeg)

Cossitt Library hosted a Teen Musical Production Course where the participants presented their work at the conclusion. Image credit: Memphis Public Libraries.

#### **A civic commons vision takes root**

When Memphis’s historic Cossitt Library reopened in 2023 after a five-year renovation, its manager wasn’t content to simply refresh the stacks. They wanted to reimagine what a library could _do_ — and whom it could empower. One result of that work was the Innovator-in-Residence program, a first-of-its-kind initiative embedding working creatives inside library branches who mentor residents, run educational workshops and even help Memphians wanting to launch small businesses.

The concept traces back to [Shamichael Hallman](https://islandpress.org/books/meet-me-library#desc), then senior manager of Cossitt Library and an early leader in Memphis’ Civic Commons work_._ His belief that libraries could serve as connective public assets sparked listening sessions held with local artists, entrepreneurs and residents. In Memphis, people described wanting accessible creative space: recording rooms, flexible stages and professional equipment they otherwise could not afford.

When Cossitt reopened, that vision became tangible: a café-anchored public space outfitted with a professional-grade recording studio, content labs and a modular stage for live events, all in addition to more traditional library equipment and books. Soon after, the Innovator-in-Residence program launched, pairing local creatives with these new public tools.

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*dKVJ1tyAW3_mC0AxGBv9pQ.png)

The library has used fun, colorful publicity to draw in new audiences. Image credit: Memphis Public Libraries.

#### **Creativity meets community**

The program engages local creative entrepreneurs (of various kinds) to base all or part of their business within a library branch. Each innovator is an active professional in a creative profession and receives a workspace, access to equipment and resources, and an annual stipend, in exchange for providing public programs, mentorship, community engagement and promotional support for the library.

Innovators use the library as both a studio and classroom, running free workshops, offering one-on-one coaching, and leading events that help residents gain creative and entrepreneurial skills. In return, they help expand the library’s public programming and visibility, often introducing new audiences to all the library has to offer.

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*Ie6aDzG-Ll9zciFUcsV6qw.png)

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*qGGm_ARA0pLKSRt8qLoByw.png)

Cossitt Library is holding a fashion design and sewing series inspired by the styles of Theo Huxtable. Image credit: Nathan Weinreich.

Christine Weinreich, the executive director of the Memphis Library Foundation points out that Cossitt Library has become a “creative entrepreneurship hub.” “People come in to learn a skill, to connect with a mentor and often leave seeing a real pathway to building something of their own,” she says.

Each innovator receives a modest stipend — about $10,000 a year — plus access to professional equipment that stays with the library. In return, they deliver public programs, mentor participants, and amplify events through their own networks. What started as a $5,000 pilot is now being scaled through additional funding from a $300,000 state workforce-development grant, contributions from the Memphis Library Foundation and corporate sponsorships.

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*6_a-nYwTq699uNBi37WNKw.jpeg)

Ena Esco launched the Cossitt Library’s first Innovator residency in podcasting. Image credit: D’Angelo Connell Photography.

#### The first innovator

Ena Esco, a veteran radio broadcaster and founder of the [_Verbally Effective_](https://verballyeffective.com/) podcast, launched the library’s first Innovator residency — in podcasting — at Cossitt Library in 2023. Her two decades of experience in traditional media, audio storytelling, business planning, and monetization and marketing for podcasts are put to good use, in workshops for would-be podcasters who also have access to Cossitt Library’s professional studio equipment to record.

Many of Esco’s mentees have gone on to launch podcasts or media projects, with more than 50 community podcasts credited to her work with residents. Esco also coaches podcasters one-on-one, helping them refine their voice, build confidence and frame their personal stories for public audiences — skills she describes as “part media training, part therapy.”

The free studio access is a game-changer for residents, says Esco. “Most people used to record on their phones or pay to book private studio time,” she explains. “Here, you don’t have to pay for anything. You can come in and be as creative as you want. We even added cameras for a visual component, so people can produce full audio-visual podcasts for free.”

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*aBmbA1Vs-1xrf4FzWo97Mw.png)

_Cossitt’s Music Production Innovator, Ty Boyland, gave a presentation at the 2025 PodBox Memphis Podcast Festival. Image credit: Nathan Weinreich._

Esco also oversees the annual [PodBox Memphis Podcast Festival](https://choose901.com/pod-box-memphis-festival-returns-free-bigger-and-built-for-memphis-creators/) at Cossitt Library, now in its third year. Sponsored by iHeart Media, the festival draws hundreds of attendees and national level-speakers to Cossitt. It also draws in new visitors to the library.

“Each year of the festival, we level up,” says Weinreich. “We’ve hosted celebrity keynotes, and each time that means new people discover the library — and they sign up for library cards the same day.”

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*0zcKlZ_NfJ-476_pjy0i_A.jpeg)

Photographer Aisha Raison was a guest speaker at Jessica Chaney’s workshop at the Raleigh branch. Image credit: Memphis Public Libraries.

#### Building networks of creative entrepreneurs

What began with one podcaster has expanded into a city-wide network. For example, Cossitt now hosts multiple innovators spanning music production, animation and design, event production, content creation and social media entrepreneurship. Across town at the Raleigh branch, a demonstration kitchen became the setting for the library’s Innovator-in-Residence Chef Jasmen Richmond. Her cooking demos and nutrition classes double as workforce development for residents seeking jobs — or launching food trucks and catering ventures.

“The community in Raleigh asked for this,” Weinreich explains. “They wanted healthier food options and to learn how to turn that knowledge into opportunity. We built the kitchen for them, and now it’s generating small businesses.”

Alongside Richmond, filmmaker Jessica Chaney is running a film-and-photography residency at the Raleigh branch, teaching everything from smartphone shooting to professional editing. Her capstone project: a neighborhood film festival and photo exhibit showcasing local stories on screen. Branches in the neighborhoods of Orange Mound and Frayser have already requested their own innovators, signaling how eagerly communities want to blend creativity and public learning on behalf of residents.

#### Measuring connection and value

The Innovator-in-Residence program is already popular with residents and library visitors, but the program’s success isn’t just anecdotal. Attendance at library events has surged, especially among first-time visitors drawn by festivals and headline speakers. Once inside, these visitors explore all the other resources available in the Memphis system — from small-business workshops to ESL classes — creating a ripple effect of growing attendance and public support.

Memphis Public Libraries will soon use the [Urban Libraries Council’s Business Value Calculator](https://www.urbanlibraries.org/initiatives/workforce-economic-development/business-value-calculator) to quantify outcomes like new enterprises launched and mentorship hours logged. But the human stories already speak volumes. “I see friendships forming, businesses collaborating and people finding confidence they didn’t know they had,” Weinreich notes.

#### Lessons for other cities

Libraries across the country have long practiced an important role in the community as places of learning and information sharing. In recent years, many libraries have expanded their programming and offerings to include workforce development and small-business support; health, legal, and social-service navigation; and robust arts and cultural programming.

Memphis’ Innovator-in-Residence program adds a new aspect to reinvigorated library programming, by adding in extra staff capacity — bringing in working professionals who can enhance programming without adding pressure to core staff. By managing their own workshops, mentorships, and community outreach, the innovators give staff more breathing room to focus on core services, while simultaneously modeling new ways of engaging the public. This in turn attracts new visitors into the library.

This partnership model deepens program quality while broadening reach, as innovators attract new audiences through their networks, design and deliver specialized events, and co-produce programs alongside librarians who handle logistics and promotion. The result is a more dynamic library system where creative experts extend what staff can offer, expanding the library’s role even more.

As Weinreich puts it, the innovators “expand what the library can do — turning it into a launchpad for connection, creativity and opportunity.”

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*cV7ofTdf0SSTukTH6YDtmg.jpeg)

For libraries or civic leaders eager to replicate the model, Memphis’s Innovator-in-Residence program offers some lessons:

1.  **Co-create from day one**. Listening to artists, entrepreneurs and residents allowed Cossitt Library to focus on the subjects that resonated locally, like podcasting and music production. This in turn enhances greater support and program attendance in a virtuous cycle.
2.  **Design for flexibility**. At Cossitt, flexible performance space, content labs, and modular staging enable live, filmed, and hybrid event formats that attract both broader and specialized audiences.
3.  **Blend trust with structure**. Innovators have a lot of creative latitude to build programming and educational materials, but act within clear library processes and guidelines that ensure consistency and scalability.
4.  **Measure meaningful impact**. It’s not enough to track visitors; to measure success, library staff should track mentorship hours, business outcomes and (if possible) the formation of networks. Partnerships and friendships that happen inside the library via the programming can often extend beyond the library walls, contributing to program success.

Across the country, libraries have been redefining themselves as places of expanded community programming. Memphis’s Innovator-in-Residence program builds upon this work, showing how these public spaces can also evolve to support local talent and strengthen local networks.

“It’s mind-blowing what we’ve accomplished with this residency,” Esco reflects. “It’s changing lives, and it’s changing the story of Memphis.”
