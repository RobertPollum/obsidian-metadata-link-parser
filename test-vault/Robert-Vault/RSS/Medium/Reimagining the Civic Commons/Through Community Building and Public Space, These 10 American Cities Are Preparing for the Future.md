#### How Civic Commons teams are leaning into social infrastructure to weather tumultuous times

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*adFtXjJXpMZLp1riHCmTYw.jpeg)

Lexington’s local Allegro Dance Project performs at the grand opening of Gatton Park in September. Image Credit: Brandon Turner, Unsung Hero Productions

In a year of upheaval at the federal level and deepening national divisions, the work of the [Reimagining the Civic Commons](https://civiccommons.us/) was a beacon of strength and resiliency on the local level. From breaking new ground in Akron to deepening community programs in Philadelphia, the ten cities in the national Civic Commons initiative showed the power of working together in community and learning in collaboration, and the importance of building the foundations of local movements through the public realm at this very moment.

Take a look at how these cities moved their work forward in 2025.

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*BXcjQDj8FlaKVe6khJSNJg.jpeg)

Families enjoyed the new Adventure Playground at the Gatton Park grand opening. Photo Credit: Brandon Turner, Unsung Hero Productions.

#### Lexington: Changing outlooks through changing the landscape

In 2025, the Lexington team advanced a citywide effort to understand how public policy and public space investments can strengthen communities across the city while celebrating a year of public realm milestones. The August opening of Gatton Park on the Town Branch and the September ribbon cutting of the reimagined downtown Phoenix Park represent the completion of over $101 million investment in downtown public spaces along the award-winning [Town Branch Commons linear greenway](https://www.lexingtonky.gov/government/mayors-office/town-branch-commons).

The city’s investment in a network of public spaces along the Town Branch Commons is paying off, according to research from the Landscape Architecture Foundation, with more than $110 million in municipal, grant and philanthropic investments in the development or renovation of parks and a career technical education center for the local school district.

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*phfm_V81kU9wawzylKurRA.jpeg)

Sniffing around the new dog park at Gatton Park. Image credit: Brandon Turner, Unsung Hero Productions.

These parks have also started to reshape the image of the city for residents and local businesses, who feel new energy and optimism about Lexington’s future.

Alongside renewed work on policy and programming in sites across the city, these milestones reflect a growing commitment to community relationships, equitable access to public spaces, and learning how policy decisions translate into everyday civic life on the ground in Lexington.

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*BWJvVKRUH9O3H_ypBtrXSA.jpeg)

Runners and spectators celebrated the Akron Marathon at Lock 3 park. Image credit: City of Akron.

#### **Akron: Keeping strong momentum going**

In 2025, the Akron Civic Commons advanced its vision for accessible, equitable and welcoming public spaces along the 101-mile Ohio & Erie Canal Towpath Trail. In April, the [City of Akron](https://www.akronohio.gov/community/ward_3.php), the [Ohio & Erie Canalway Coalition](https://www.ohioeriecanal.org/ACC), and Summit Lake residents broke ground on the $7.5 million [Summit Lake NorthShore Park](https://www.akronciviccommons.org/summit-lake-vision-plan) — a vibrant new gathering space with a grand pavilion, a boathouse with a canoe and kayak share program, an accessible launch and lakeside seating. Set to open in 2026, the park will be a destination for residents and visitors, and connect Summit Lake’s east and west neighborhoods via the Summit Lake Trail which was completed in 2023.

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*7D8JEhvNXdU5FZe4Vh3Qxw.jpeg)

Residents enjoyed a pontoon boat ride at the inaugural Summit Lake Float. Image credit: Ohio & Erie Canalway Coalition.

Community-centered programs — including the Guided Interpretive Canoe Program, Environmental Stewardship Program and [Summit Lake Youth Ambassador Program](https://medium.com/reimagining-the-civic-commons/4-ways-to-engage-young-people-in-civic-life-beyond-elections-becd5c3f5e19) — continued to foster pride of place. Akron’s Bicentennial celebration featured the “Summit Lake Float,” a free event coordinated by Ohio & Erie Canalway Coalition and [Summit Metro Parks](https://www.summitmetroparks.org/), which included pontoon and kayak rides, guided canoeing and root beer floats, providing many residents their first opportunity or a new way to engage with the lake.

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*873OBFuN7Bm8_6R5HCSfLw.jpeg)

Downtown visitors took in Party on the PNC Plaza at the Akron Civic Theatre. Image credit: Talia Hodge.

The year also marked continued momentum along Akron’s key business and cultural corridor, the Civic Gateway in downtown, following the [$17 million redevelopment of Lock 3 Park](https://www.governing.com/infrastructure/akron-bets-big-on-parks-to-revitalize-downtown#:~:text=Last%2520fall%252C%2520the%2520city%2520unveiled,social%2520cohesion%2520and%2520environmental%2520sustainability.). The space was thoughtfully designed in partnership with residents to create a more inviting atmosphere for locals, students and visitors as a 365-day park. By collaborating with the neighboring [Akron Civic Theatre](https://www.akroncivic.com/) and [Downtown Akron Partnership](https://www.downtownakron.com/), the city curated a diverse lineup of performers, festivals and free programs that enrich the visitor experience and reinforce [Lock 3](https://www.akronohio.gov/departments/lock_3/index.php)’s role as the civic heart of downtown Akron. An estimated 130,000 people attended events and festivals at Lock 3 this year, on top of those who used the park for leisure — more than doubling visitation records prior to its redevelopment.

Concept planning has begun for reimagining Lock 2 park, an underused pocket park in downtown adjacent to the 717 Credit Union Stadium and Akron Children’s Hospital. This initiative in 2026 will highlight the power of collaboration among local government, nonprofit organizations, and the private sector to drive innovation in public space design and development.

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*hlYp3OZIFdcbSUVliE2iuQ.jpeg)

Yellow Jackets ambassadors clean Elijah Perry Park as part of a two year pilot. Image credit: Avi Steinhardt.

#### **Camden: Making public space clean, safe and welcoming**

This year, the team of folks from Camden Community Partnership and Camden Special Service District has been hard at work finding out the nuts and bolts behind what it takes to keep a city park clean, safe and inviting for everyone.

Through a year-long pilot program at four city parks, the Camden Civic Commons partners have collaborated to measure the time, resources and costs needed to maintain public parks at a high standard of cleanliness and care. The initiative has brought together the [Camden Special Services District’s](https://mydowntowncamden.com/camden-special-services-district/) Yellow-Jacket cleaning ambassadors with other city partners to ensure that every park remains a place where families and residents can enjoy themselves.

In this first year, the Yellow-Jackets removed more than 3,000 bags of trash and an additional 50,000 pounds of debris, along with clearing illegal signage, graffiti and biowaste.

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*F_MnUaQrvZXAH6LGTpfU8Q.jpeg)

The City Department of Public Works distributed gear to volunteers and residents participating in the park clean up. Photo credit: Camden Community Partnership.

This pilot joins the city’s yearly Camden Strong Clean Up Initiative. Over the past four years, more than 4,500 people — including members of the Philadelphia Eagles — have participated in the annual initiative, which combines environmental stewardship and neighborhood revitalization through large-scale clean-up efforts of the city’s public spaces.

The city’s Department of Public Works removes an average of 100 to 150 tons of trash per cleanup, totaling more than 1,300 tons of waste and debris removed each season. The cleaner public spaces also deter further littering — city officials have said they’ve seen a decrease in illegal dumping and an increase in clean, safe public spaces across all city wards since the launch of Camden Strong.

Making sure [Camden’s parks remain vibrant](https://camdencommunitypartnership.com/bolstering-a-renaissance-in-camdens-parks/), welcoming and ready for all shows that well-maintained public spaces are more than just beautiful locations — they’re the heart of community pride and connection.

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*8DHSm2bvWMEcQjHnaKhayQ.png)

Community leaders cut the ribbon on the new Mill Creek access point — opening a space to the public for the first time in decades. Photo credit: Tri-State Trails.

#### **Cincinnati: Exploring new possibilities**

The Cincinnati team led by [Tri-State Trails](https://tristatetrails.org/) and [Mill Creek Alliance](https://www.themillcreekalliance.org/) not only hosted a [Studio](https://civiccommons.us/2025/12/civic-commons-studio-10-local-power-lasting-impact/) for more than 120 participants from across the Reimagining the Civic Commons network this year, they also reached new milestones in public space access. The opening of the new Mill Creek access and boat launch point near the Yellow Bridge marks a transformational moment for South Cumminsville, Millvale, and the Beekman Corridor — communities that have long faced disinvestment but remain rooted in resilience and strong neighborhood leadership. Led by the Mill Creek Alliance in partnership with the City of Cincinnati, the Mill Creek Valley Conservancy District and community-based organizations, this project turns a once-fenced-off area into a welcoming public gateway to one of Cincinnati’s most historic waterways.

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*agM0t-FmW5tEv_zzERDlaQ.png)

Kayakers took to the Mill Creek during the Studio for another view on Cincinnati’s public spaces. Image credit: Game Day.

For decades, Mill Creek was seen as a place to avoid; today, it is becoming a place of renewal where residents can connect with nature, launch a kayak, go fishing and imagine new possibilities for their communities. The project also advances the long-term vision for the Mill Creek Greenway Trail as a north-south spine of the [CROWN](https://crowncincinnati.org/) trail network, reconnecting communities through safe, accessible and meaningful public spaces. More than a new boat ramp and access point, this opening represents a broader move toward environmental justice, community-driven planning, and retaining neighborhood assets under public control — demonstrating how collaborative stewardship can shape a healthier, more connected future for the Mill Creek Valley.

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*AUBDWaPeEJ4aEplHOeNZcA.png)

Penumbra Theater cast and staff spoke about their creative journeys at the St. Paul Learning Journey. Image credit: Cavitt Productions.

#### **Minneapolis | St. Paul: Strengthening connections and relationships**

For the Minneapolis | St. Paul team, 2025 was spent building relationships to help leverage public space and place for hope, healing and systemic change. This work stems from initiatives to create a “just neighborhood vision” area plan for Minneapolis’ Longfellow region, and enhance social and economic development along the city’s West Broadway Avenue and St. Paul’s Arcade Street. This year, the team has taken part in events like [Redesign’s](https://redesigninc.org/) Remember and Reimagine, a family-friendly community event rooted in spatial justice intended to gather input on the redevelopment of a former U.S. Bank location that burned in the uprisings following George Floyd’s murder.

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*JBrf7y-TrQXd-9p2TmgbyQ.jpeg)

Taylor Smrikárova and Paul Bauknight discussed the expansion of the Blue Line light rail with Jason Foster from Destination Crenshaw. Image credit: Jasmine Baxter.

The team also planned a successful [Learning Journey](https://civiccommons.us/2025/08/civic-commons-learning-journey-st-paul/), inviting members of other Reimagining the Civic Commons cities to understand the impact of building I-94 through St. Paul’s Rondo neighborhood and see firsthand the community’s efforts at rebuilding and connection despite the complexity of redlining’s effects on the neighborhood. Equally important was the [“Beyond the Tracks”](https://www.ctud.org/post/beyond-the-tracks-national-voices-join-minneapolis-in-reimagining-community-through-transit) event featuring [Jason Foster of Destination Crenshaw](https://destinationcrenshaw.la/dc_team/jason-foster/). His advice encouraged attendees to find opportunities to benefit from the expansion of the Blue Line light rail into their community and prioritize their own economic stability. As the chair of the Metropolitan Council Charlie Zelle said, it was “the best Blue Line community event that \[they\] have ever had.”

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*SU_WFb5ysU8JBduLbafsCQ.jpeg)

The inaugural Six Mile Street Festival welcomed nearly 2,000 visitors along the corridor. Image courtesy of Bre’Anna Johnston.

#### **Detroit: Growing community pride and investment**

This year, the Detroit Civic Commons team advanced a resident-centered revitalization agenda to expand public life initiatives, strengthen small businesses and reinforce long-term community stewardship for Fitzgerald and surrounding communities. The district celebrated its [Select Level Michigan Main Street](https://www.michiganbusiness.org/press-releases/2025/05/live6-alliance-michigan-main-street/) designation and convened civic representatives, business owners, neighborhood leaders and property owners and developers to shape a five-year transformation strategy. Many of these participants now serve across the Livernois-McNichols Main Street governing bodies and project implementation committees, forming a 30-member partnership network that ensures resident and local entrepreneur perspectives guide corridor strategy, storefront activation and improvements to the public realm. The inaugural [Six Mile Street Festival](https://medium.com/reimagining-the-civic-commons/how-civic-infrastructure-powers-small-local-businesses-20c1c5ec52d7) drew more than 2,000 visitors and renewed energy to storefronts, public spaces and institutions, demonstrating the power of corridor-scale collaboration and engagement.

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*IN9jJQAseYHC7JSeuZ6ZOA.jpeg)

San Juan Block Club President Stephanie Harbin and Prairie Street Block Club and Ella Fitzgerald Park Conservancy President Darnetta Banks speak at the announcement of The Kresge Foundation’s $180 million investment in Northwest Detroit. Image courtesy of Hannah Bennett.

This momentum continued into September, when lifelong residents, block club and park conservancy presidents joined The Kresge Foundation to announce its [$180 million investment in Northwest Detroit](https://kresge.org/news-views/kresge-foundation-investing-180m-in-neighborhoods-plans-new-headquarters-in-detroit/) — a milestone that affirmed years of dedicated, place-based work and strengthened local efforts to stabilize housing and support small businesses in Fitzgerald. Together, these achievements sustain and leverage public amenity investments, promote resident investment opportunities, and anchor district growth in the leadership of legacy neighbors and long-established businesses.

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*IFmJqT8lAlQLRgqiwq-5iw.jpeg)

Sunset Skate events have become the signature program for Memphis River Parks Partnership. Image credit: Connor Ryan.

#### **Memphis: Bridging divides through stronger ties**

Over the past year, the Memphis team explored how vibrant public space can help bridge the divides of geography, race, and class by strengthening community ties through shared experiences. Indoors at the [Memphis Public Libraries](https://www.memphislibrary.org/), the [Innovator in Residence program](https://medium.com/reimagining-the-civic-commons/from-library-to-launchpad-29837a4e7a0b) expanded to include a wider variety of creative professionals to host programming in the library. Outside in the parks, flagship events like [Sunset Skate](https://www.memphisriverparks.org/events/sunset-skate-1-9afes-gslle) created places of fun and drew crowds of hundreds of people across the city’s demographics. Sunset Skate has quickly become [Memphis River Parks Partnership](https://www.memphisriverparks.org/)’s signature program, offering an experience that is joyful, inclusive and uniquely rooted in place. Attendance this year was more than double of previous years, as more than 3,300 participants laced up their skates across the four events of Sunset Skate this summer.

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*JJbHjCB081PeHa_AR5iODA.png)

The Mississippi River Gumption Lantern Parade showcased Tom Lee Park’s role as a space for creativity and community connection. Image credit: Connor Ryan.

This year Memphis also celebrated the 100-year anniversary of Tom Lee’s heroic rescue with the [Mississippi River Gumption Lantern Parade](https://www.memphisriverparks.org/gumption-revival-lantern-parade), where more than 1,700 participants illuminated the riverfront with hundreds of handmade lanterns. Memphis River Parks Partnership enlisted the help of [Chantelle Rytter](https://www.weirdgonepro.com/about), a parade artist known for founding the Atlanta Beltline Lantern Parade. The event showcased Tom Lee Park and its ongoing role as a vibrant space for community connection, creativity and pride.

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*cnm69qzjxcaUWwT0cUZFsA.jpeg)

Attendees visited with Lou Seal, the mascot for the San Francisco Giants, at an event on San Pedro Street celebrating the team. Image credit: Lan Nguyen.

#### **San José: Reinventing public spaces for community enjoyment**

The San José team’s intentional investment in three public realm typologies — a downtown park, an underutilized lot, and a downtown street environment — has spurred new park developments, launched community-building programs and nurtured new partnerships that drive quality experiences and new opportunities.

[Arena Green](https://grpg.org/visit/arena-green/), a central city park and part of the Guadalupe River Park, has undergone a major transformation. Following years of neglect, multiple city departments, the San Jose Sharks and the [Guadalupe River Park Conservancy](https://grpg.org/) mobilized efforts that have successfully made it significantly more accessible to the public. These include the completion of a plaza expansion, tree plantings, new events pavilion and additional walkways, a pickleball court and health and performance-related programming. The team’s efforts are continuing through strategic beautification and the activation of underutilized spaces.

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*EySnzILWsA3W15PKSDbiDQ.jpeg)

Park attendees engage with a booth at an SJ Pride Block Party Event in Arena Green. Image credit: Lan Nguyen.

The [San José Downtown Association](https://sjdowntown.com/) is driving a significant beautification effort called “Stitching Districts,” with 17 project installations planned along Santa Clara Street between Market and Fourth streets. This initiative is designed to connect areas of downtown San Jose bookmarked by San Pedro Street and SAP Arena Center, increasing foot traffic, fostering economic growth and enhancing the visibility of other destinations such as Arena Green and the Historic Little Italy District.

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*ya1w5Hm5NKf3_X59-A4VUg.jpeg)

Staff and visitors gather around a garden bed to sow seeds in SoFA Pocket Park. Image credit: Veggielution.

A previously underutilized parking lot was transformed into [SoFA Pocket Park](https://veggielution.org/sofa-pocket-park), an urban demonstration garden, farm stand, dog park and mobile food vending hub in 2021. Since then, the space has been used to promote local food entrepreneurs, provide educational opportunities for the public and create an urban space for [the community to connect to nature and each other](https://medium.com/reimagining-the-civic-commons/how-the-public-realm-aids-in-healing-and-growing-359030c80159). Civic Commons partner [Veggielution](https://veggielution.org/) has advanced its mission to build thriving, connected communities by hosting 11 public events that brought together more than 200 people to celebrate food, culture and shared civic space. These efforts reflect the core values of Reimagining the Civic Commons — demonstrating how inclusive, community-rooted programming can transform public spaces into vibrant commons where everyone has a stake in shaping the future.

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*SgSNMuFnGtnzZPGxHaJvrw.jpeg)

Bike Walk Macon hosted Safe Streets For All events at the Booker T. Washington Recreation Center. Image credit: DSTO Moore.

#### **Macon: Reconnecting as the path to trust-building**

Over the past year, the Macon Civic Commons team has worked to help reconnect the historic Pleasant Hill neighborhood — which was severely affected by the construction of I-75 — by investing in public spaces and supporting community-led programming. Initiatives such as the [Walnut Street Connector](https://medium.com/reimagining-the-civic-commons/building-an-inviting-and-connected-core-5df5b7d175e6), which connects the [Ocmulgee Mounds National Historical Park](https://www.nps.gov/ocmu/) to the [Ocmulgee Heritage Trail](https://newtownmacon.com/oht/), focused on improving safe connections between neighborhoods along with pathway and bike lane planning and small-scale public activations. These programs demonstrated how everyday civic spaces can promote health, belonging and shared care.

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*-OIN4-jDVAnmY-NUTJ3anw.jpeg)

Macon Mental Health Matters brought wellness events and gatherings to the community. Image credit: DSTO Moore.

At the same time, the Macon Civic Commons team supported neighborhood-led events and creative activities including storytelling, music and wellness gatherings that brought residents together and activated both indoor and outdoor community spaces. Together, this work shows the shift away from one-time projects toward long-term relationships, trust-building and revitalization guided by the voices and priorities of local residents.

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*nCYTa9ym1stknKlaXz7r7A.jpeg)

West Fairmount Park’s We Walk PHL group kept a consistent schedule — even in foul weather. Image credit: Albert Yee for Reimagining the Civic Commons.

#### Philadelphia: Investing in public space and collaborative planning

Philadelphia’s Civic Commons partners continued to pair capital investments in public parks with neighborhood-based collaborative programming and strong, mutual relationships in 2025. While major projects in the three key geographies continued — such as the [Welsh Fountain redesign,](https://myphillypark.org/fairmount-park-conservancy-releases-new-welsh-fountain-designs/) and the construction of [Float Lab](https://muralarts.org/artworks/floatlab/) — the partners also consistently hosted events for the neighborhood like teen takeovers, We Walk PHL meetups, an outdoor orchestra performance, an annual 5K event, movie nights, boating programs and yoga. The partners focused on consistency, collaboration and creatively cross-programming to strengthen the ties between park and neighborhood.

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*DN0leHvUu8C4r_uAqodQ7g.jpeg)

Volunteers helped Bartram’s Garden and Tiny WPA build a play space together. Image credit: Albert Yee for Bartram’s Garden.

[Bartram’s Garden](https://www.bartramsgarden.org/) and [TinyWPA](http://www.tinywpa.org/) invited volunteers of all ages to help build a one-of-a-kind, nature-based play space at the Garden in October. The design was inspired by the nature observations made by elementary school students in nearby Southwest Philadelphia. West Fairmount Park’s [We Walk PHL](https://myphillypark.org/what-we-do/programs/we-walk-phl/) group maintained a consistent Wednesday schedule — rain or shine — to explore the historic landscape and the surrounding Parkside neighborhood on a weekly basis. [Fairmount Park Conservancy](https://myphillypark.org/)’s public programs complemented the consistency of We Walk PHL, offering monthly hikes, Wellness Wednesday meditation exercises (immediately following the walks), and historic walking tours. This programming connects residents to the existing capital improvements in West Fairmount Park including the (Reimagining the Civic Commons-funded) Parkside Edge, and offers opportunities to learn about the Welsh Fountain transformation coming to the same landscape.

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*eND4p5Er0pvGWbuJpLEpcg.jpeg)

Neighborhood kids found a way to stretch out at The Yard. Image credit: Tonetta Graham, Strawberry Mansion CDC

This year was the first full year of programming at [The Yard](https://www.strawberrymansioncdc.org/theyard), a community space operated by Strawberry Mansion CDC just across the street from East Fairmount Park. Partnerships through the Civic Commons bolstered The Yard’s launch — for example, the Conservancy and the CDC hosted two movie nights at The Yard, and consistent staffing support from [Philadelphia Parks and Recreation](https://www.phila.gov/departments/philadelphia-parks-recreation/) encouraged neighbors — including local kids—to drop by and make the space their own.
