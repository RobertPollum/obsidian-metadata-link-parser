I’m not a big horror reader normally, but I make an exception in October. I liked [aristhought](https://medium.com/u/df5437246576)’s [take on it](https://aristhought.medium.com/horror-as-an-avenue-for-catharsis-19f94b04e84e): “Horror is a controlled fall into the unknown. It’s a very human way to project our worst fears, our most unspoken questions and worries, and express them in a way that everyone can, in some way, connect with.”

That’s what’s best about storytelling on Medium: Writers come to make the strange feel familiar and the personal universal.

What’s scarier, [Cassie Kozyrkov](https://medium.com/u/2fccb851bb5e) asks, than being sent a thick report from a coworker that looks good on the surface, but when you peek under its mask, it’s actually AI-produced drivel, devoid of any actual insight, thought, or value? If you’ve received something like that recently, congratulations, you’ve just been [workslopped](https://medium.com/data-science-collective/have-you-been-workslopped-yet-d175a8e1a68f).

Being confronted with your own mortality and how meaninglessly you spend your time is another spooky experience. [Alberto Romero](https://medium.com/u/7ba6be8a3022) holds up a mirror to his readers, forcing us to think about [how likely it is that we’ll die mid-scroll](https://albertoromgar.medium.com/you-will-die-mid-scroll-f9746b8915d2?sk=v2%2F65c7fab1-90d7-4846-8213-a5f1f35d87fe). Like a horror story, you’re forced to admit the person you see (someone who spends hours a day on your phone) isn’t who you wanted to become. There’s no escape: You’re bound to keep repeating your haunted habits unless you exorcise them for good.

But the flip side of horror is comedy, as emeritus professor [Mark C Watney](https://medium.com/u/33119d0d191d) demonstrates in his deep exploration into [why a student of his placed him](https://markwatney.medium.com/i-place-dr-watney-in-dantes-inferno-for-gluttony-of-his-students-time-9d1bb284552b) “in Circle 3 for Gluttony of his students’ time.” Don’t worry, though, Dr. Watney was not vindictive. “Finding within myself the Grace to be objective, I reluctantly placed a ‘B+’ on my tormentor’s perceptive paper,” he decided.

Come to Medium to laugh, cry, gasp — or just to [write your story](http://story.new).

— [Zulie @ Medium](https://medium.com/u/a870c0c34e46)

### New (to Medium) writer corner​

> Community manager [Kassandra @ Medium](https://medium.com/u/12b50f862078) spotlights some new writer stories focused on creativity and imagination:

I’ve started to consider imagination as the antidote to despair, the tiny spark that helps us persevere, evolve, and hope. In [Reviving Gaeilge](https://medium.com/statecraft/reviving-gaeilge-da43c514949c), [Maia Weatherstone](https://medium.com/u/f0fefa3f107b) shares how the Irish language survives through music and shared cultural identity.

That thread of music as a connection runs through [I Don’t Know the Life of a Showgirl](https://kayleighmargaret.medium.com/i-dont-know-the-life-of-a-showgirl-15f873541337)_._ In a pop landscape defined by eras, re-releases, and reinvention, writer [Kayleigh](https://medium.com/u/438fc891d53e) asks what happens when an artist’s creativity stops evolving and they lose connection with their fans.

While in [Free the Vampire](https://medium.com/@sorrenbriarwood/free-the-vampire-reanimating-imagination-at-a-childrens-creative-writing-club-ec6fec3ed86b), [Sorren Briarwood](https://medium.com/u/cb9b8599e741) uncovers the importance of recovering imagination as a means of self-exploration and play. Together, these stories celebrate the power of imagination to sustain heritage, challenge stagnation, and reanimate what lies within.

-   [Ben Bryant](https://www.linkedin.com/in/benjamin-bryant-design/) and [Chris DiStasi](https://www.linkedin.com/in/christopherdistasi/) investigate [the interesting cultural juxtaposition Halloween brings us](https://medium.com/reimagining-the-civic-commons/what-halloween-can-teach-us-about-civic-life-1221f3538333): In our individualistic society, we teach our children to come in when it’s dark and not talk to strangers. On Halloween, that’s upended. What could our culture look like if we extended that energy beyond October 31st? (Written via [Reimagining the Civic Commons](https://medium.com/u/98a57c4acb5a))
-   [Samantha McBirney](https://medium.com/u/e8ce32af9ee3) [dives deep into data analysis](https://medium.com/@samanthamcbirney/okay-okay-i-know-its-already-mid-to-late-october-which-means-this-analysis-is-late-this-year-ee4ef55d207e) of last year’s Trick or Treaters (AKA ToTs) to her house, including how many ToTs, how many pieces of candy each ToT averaged, and what candy types were most popular with her ToTs. Come for the numbers, stay for the images and decoration philosophy. (“As tradition dictates, all decorations were gone from the yard before morning, preserving what I call the [‘magic of Halloween](https://medium.com/@samanthamcbirney/2022-the-year-of-the-sour-af91831b9e4a)’. When little ones wake up on November 1st, I want them to feel as if it was all a dream.”)

### Featured by Medium editors

Did you know publication editors can [Feature](https://medium.com/blog/introducing-featured-stories-for-publications-a0a714b8151d) a limited number of stories from their publication per month, [which sends out a notification](https://medium.com/blog/making-featured-stories-even-more-visible-92c7a534929a) and makes that story more likely to be recommended to followers of that publication?

### Evergreen gems

Medium readers come to find takes on topical trends, but also to get practical, evergreen advice. Here are a few stories from the vault that Medium readers still enjoy.

In “[Machine Learning is Fun!](https://medium.com/p/80ea3ec3c471)”, published in 2014, software developer and [now book author](https://www.machinelearningisfun.com) [Adam Geitgey](https://medium.com/u/ba4c55e4aa3d) shared a simple, easily understood tutorial for those who want to get involved in machine learning. All eight parts of his tutorial are still popular with Medium readers today.

Providing evergreen advice for any kind of self-improvement kick, [Bill Barnwell](https://medium.com/u/edcd329b6c92) wrote about [how he lost 125 pounds](https://medium.com/@billbarnwell/the-easiest-way-to-lose-125-pounds-is-to-gain-175-pounds-b4fca27ffcc5) back in 2016. “You simply have to build a plan which works for you in lieu of copying somebody else’s,” he writes. More compassionately, he adds, “More than anything, though, the lesson I learned from all of this was how incredibly important it is to forgive yourself.”

Back in 2018, [Kris Gage](https://medium.com/u/67d9cdce33af) wrote about [the decision to have kids or not](https://medium.com/@krisgage/read-this-if-youre-not-sure-you-want-kids-c24c7895ebd5). “Good decisions are made out of love, not fear,” she wrote. It was true seven years ago, and it’s true today.
